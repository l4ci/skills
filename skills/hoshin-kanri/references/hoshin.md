# Hoshin Kanri: the X-matrix, catchball, and review

Hoshin Kanri (方針管理) is a strategy-deployment method from Japanese Total Quality Management, formalized by Yoji Akao and others in the 1960s and 1970s and adopted at Toyota, Bridgestone, and Hewlett-Packard. The name translates roughly as "direction management" or "policy management"; in English it is usually called policy deployment or strategy deployment. The aim is to align an organization so that a few breakthrough objectives reach every level and connect to daily work, and so that progress is reviewed and corrected on a regular cycle.

Two ideas carry the method. The first is the **vital few**: a year's strategy is concentrated into three or four breakthrough objectives, not spread across everything. The second is **catchball**: objectives and targets are negotiated up and down the organization, like a ball thrown back and forth, so the people who must deliver them shape them and own them.

## The X-matrix

The X-matrix is the signature artifact. One page links four "legs" arranged around a center, with the diagonal lines between them forming the X that gives it its name, plus an ownership grid. Read clockwise:

- **South (bottom): breakthrough objectives.** The 3-to-5-year step-changes the strategy demands. The vital few.
- **West (left): annual objectives.** What must be achieved this year to stay on course for the breakthrough objectives.
- **North (top): improvement priorities.** The initiatives and changes (the "how") that will move the annual objectives this year.
- **East (right): targets to improve.** The measurable targets and metrics that say whether the priorities are working. Each is a number with a level and a date.
- **Far right: ownership grid.** Who is accountable for each priority and which teams contribute.

At each corner where two legs meet, a **correlation mark** records how strongly they relate: strong, weak, or none. The marks make the logic visible. A breakthrough objective with no annual objective pointing at it, or a priority with no target, shows up as a blank where a mark should be.

A faithful X-matrix is hard to render in plain text. In a report, present the four legs and the ownership grid as a structured table, fall back to a nested list where a cell cannot hold the content, and state the correlation marks explicitly rather than dropping them.

## Catchball

Catchball is the two-way dialogue that sets the objectives and targets. Leadership proposes a direction; the next level down responds with what is feasible, what it would cost, and what depends on others; the proposal is revised; the ball goes back. The cascade is not a top-down handout. A target imposed without this exchange tends to be either unrealistic or unowned, and usually both. Plan for at least one round of pushback before the matrix is committed.

## Review cadence and the bowling chart

Hoshin runs on the PDCA loop. The annual plan is reviewed on a regular rhythm, commonly monthly, against each target. The tracking tool is often a **bowling chart**: a table with the target for each period in one row and the actual result beneath it, so a miss is visible early and triggers a corrective countermeasure rather than a year-end surprise. An annual review then feeds the next planning cycle. The matrix is only as useful as this review; built once and filed, it does nothing.

## The golden thread

The test of a deployment is the golden thread: any priority or target can be traced up to a breakthrough objective, and any breakthrough objective has priorities and targets supporting it. Breaks in the thread are the alignment defects the method exists to surface, in either direction. A priority that serves no objective is wasted effort; an objective with no supporting work is a hope.

## Common pitfalls

- **Too many objectives.** Cascading ten breakthrough objectives spreads effort thin and defeats the focus the method is built to create. Hold the vital few.
- **Top-down dictation.** Skipping catchball produces a plan nobody at the working level believes in or owns.
- **Activities posing as targets.** "Run training sessions" is an activity; "cut onboarding time to under five days" is a target. Only the second can be tracked.
- **No owner.** A priority without a named, accountable owner drifts.
- **Build once, ignore.** An X-matrix with no review cadence is decoration. The PDCA loop is part of the method, not an optional extra.
- **Confusing the matrix with the strategy.** Hoshin deploys a chosen strategy and aligns the organization to it. It does not decide whether the strategy is the right one.

## Relationship to nearby methods

- **OKRs** share the cascade-and-measure idea but are lighter and more recent. Hoshin adds the X-matrix, formal catchball, and a longer breakthrough horizon.
- **The Balanced Scorecard** translates strategy into balanced measures and a strategy map; Hoshin is more focused on deploying the vital few into owned annual action.
- **PDCA** is the engine Hoshin reviews on. Each cycle plans, does, checks against target, and adjusts.
