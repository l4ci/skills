# kotter-change

Kotter's 8-step model (John Kotter, *Leading Change*, 1996) is a method for leading organizational change through eight sequential steps: create a sense of urgency, build a guiding coalition, form a vision, communicate it to enlist a volunteer army, remove the barriers in the way, generate short-term wins, sustain acceleration, and anchor the change in the culture. The core idea is that change is led, not announced. You build the conditions for it before you push, prove it works before you scale, and anchor it before you let go. Skipping a step buys apparent speed and then breaks the change later.

This skill facilitates the eight steps with a gate check on each one, holding a step until it answers its question (urgency felt, coalition powerful, vision clear, vision communicated, barriers removed, wins delivered, acceleration sustained, change anchored) before the next begins. Two steps spread the work across parallel subagents: step 1 surfaces urgency evidence from several angles at once (market, customer, financial, competitive, operational), and step 5 inventories barriers across lenses (structure, systems and incentives, skills, people). The findings are pooled and judged with the user, while the order across steps stays fixed. The output is a saved markdown change plan covering all eight steps plus a record of the gates.

The model is built to guard against the ways change dies: skipping urgency so people never give the effort it takes, declaring victory at the first short-term win so momentum collapses while the change is still shallow, and never anchoring it in culture or in who gets promoted so the organization reverts the moment the original leaders move on. For a lighter analytical tool that weighs the forces for and against a change before you commit, the force-field skill fits; for the individual side of who actually adopts the change, ADKAR covers it.
