# Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

Kotter's 8-step model (John Kotter, *Leading Change*, 1996) is a method for leading large-scale organizational change. Its discipline is that change is led, not announced: you build the conditions for change before you push it, you prove it works before you scale it, and you anchor it in the culture before you let go. The steps are roughly sequential. Skipping a step creates the illusion of speed but rarely produces a satisfying result, and skipped steps come back to break the change later.

The model is for leading a real organizational change: a new strategy, a restructuring, a merger integration, a cultural shift, a transformation. It is not a tool for analyzing whether the parts of an organization are aligned (that is the 7-S diagnostic) or for the individual side of who adopts the change (that is ADKAR).

## The eight steps

### 1. Create a sense of urgency
Make the case for why change must happen now, and reduce the complacency that says the status quo is fine. Urgency is built from honest evidence about threats and opportunities (market shift, customer loss, a closing window) delivered so it reaches the heart, not just the head. This is the most-skipped and most-fatal step: without enough urgency, people will not give the extra effort change demands, and the effort stalls.
- **Gate question:** is there enough felt urgency across the people who matter that they will act, not just nod? Manufactured panic or a single anxious leader does not clear it.
- **Common failure:** underestimating how hard it is to move people out of comfort, declaring urgency by memo, or confusing a leadership team's anxiety with organization-wide urgency.

### 2. Build a guiding coalition
Assemble a group with enough power, credibility, expertise, and leadership to lead the change. Change is not led by one champion or by a single department. The coalition needs position power (enough authority that blockers cannot easily stop it), credibility (people respected enough to be believed), and a mix of management and leadership. It works as a team that trusts each other, not a committee that reports up.
- **Gate question:** does the coalition have enough real power and credibility to drive the change against resistance, and does it function as a team?
- **Common failure:** a coalition that looks right on the org chart but lacks real authority, leans entirely on staff functions, or is missing the line leaders whose buy-in the change depends on.

### 3. Form a strategic vision and initiatives
Create a clear picture of the future that is desirable, feasible, and simple enough to communicate fast, plus the strategic initiatives that move toward it. A good vision can be explained in a few minutes and gets a reaction of understanding and interest. It is not a plan or a budget; it is the destination that makes the plan make sense and that lets people make aligned decisions without being told each one.
- **Gate question:** can the vision be communicated in a few minutes and leave the listener clear on the direction and willing to follow it?
- **Common failure:** a vision that is too complicated or blurry, a vision that is really just a financial target, or no vision at all (only detailed plans and directives, which do not move people).

### 4. Enlist a volunteer army (communicate the vision)
Get broad buy-in by communicating the vision constantly and, above all, through behavior. The vision is undercommunicated by orders of magnitude in most failed changes: one speech or one email is far too little against the volume of daily communication people already receive. It must be repeated, woven into routine decisions, and modeled by the coalition. Nothing kills a vision faster than leaders acting against it.
- **Gate question:** do enough people understand the vision and accept it that a volunteer army is forming, rather than a few leaders pushing a passive crowd?
- **Common failure:** undercommunicating, communicating only in words while behaving inconsistently, or treating communication as a one-off launch rather than a sustained effort.

### 5. Enable action by removing barriers
Empower people to act on the vision by clearing what blocks them: structures that work against the vision, missing skills, systems and processes that make the new way hard, and the managers who resist. Once people are bought in, the obstacle is no longer will but ability and permission. Removing barriers, especially the disempowering manager who undercuts the change, is what turns enthusiasm into action.
- **Gate question:** can people actually act on the vision now, with the structural, skill, system, and people barriers identified and being removed?
- **Common failure:** leaving a key barrier standing (a misaligned reward system, a resistant senior manager) so empowered people hit a wall, lose faith, and stop.

### 6. Generate short-term wins
Engineer visible, unambiguous wins early. A short-term win is visible (many people can see it is real), unambiguous (hard to argue away), and clearly connected to the change effort. Wins are not hoped for; they are planned and delivered. They provide proof that the change works, reward the effort, build momentum, and starve the skeptics. Without wins in the first 6-18 months, urgency fades and the change loses support.
- **Gate question:** have real, visible, unambiguous wins been delivered and made clearly attributable to the change?
- **Common failure:** no early wins (so momentum dies), or wins that are vague, disputable, or not visibly tied to the change.

### 7. Sustain acceleration (consolidate gains, produce more change)
Do not let up. Use the credibility from early wins to take on bigger changes: more systems, more structures, more people. Press harder, not softer, after the first wins. The danger here is the inverse of the danger at step 6: the win is real, but it is mistaken for the finish. Premature victory stops the effort while the change is still shallow, and the old culture pulls everything back.
- **Gate question:** is the credibility from wins being spent on deeper, bigger change rather than treated as the finish line?
- **Common failure:** declaring victory too soon. This is the killer at the second half of the model: an early win is celebrated as completion, urgency collapses, and the change reverts before it is anchored.

### 8. Institute change (anchor in culture)
Make the new ways "how we do things around here." Change anchors in culture only after it has visibly produced results and people have connected the new behavior to those results, and only when it is reinforced through norms and through leadership succession. Culture changes last, not first: you do not start by changing values, you change behavior, show it works, and then make sure the next generation of leaders embodies it so the change outlasts the people who drove it.
- **Gate question:** are the new behaviors visibly tied to results, reinforced in norms, and protected in who gets promoted, so the change survives the original leaders leaving?
- **Common failure:** never anchoring, so when attention moves on or the sponsoring leaders depart, the organization quietly reverts to the old way; or trying to anchor by decree before results have made the new way credible.

## The Accelerate update (2014)

In *Accelerate* (XLR8), Kotter reframed the eight steps for a faster, more competitive environment. Three shifts matter. The steps run as concurrent and continuous "accelerators" rather than a rigid one-time sequence. Change is run by a volunteer army drawn from across the organization, not only by a small senior coalition. And the model sits in a dual operating system: the traditional management hierarchy keeps the business running while a more agile, network-like structure drives change alongside it. The eight ideas are the same; the framing moves from a single finite project to an ongoing capability.

## How to use it

1. **Hold the gates.** Each step has a question it must answer before the next begins: urgency felt, coalition powerful, vision clear, vision communicated and accepted, barriers removed, wins delivered, acceleration sustained, change anchored. Do not advance on a step that has not answered its question.
2. **Never skip urgency.** It is the foundation. Skipping it to save time is the most common and most fatal mistake; everything built on weak urgency collapses under the first real resistance.
3. **Never declare victory at the first win.** The early win is fuel, not the finish. Premature victory is what kills change in its second half.
4. **Anchor before you let go.** A change that is not built into the culture and into leadership succession reverts the moment attention moves elsewhere.

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping urgency**, so people never give the effort change requires and the push stalls at the first obstacle.
- **A coalition without real power**, so the change has visible sponsors but cannot overcome the people and structures that block it.
- **An undercommunicated or uncommunicated vision**, so people do not understand where they are going or do not believe leaders mean it, especially when leaders behave inconsistently with the vision.
- **Leaving barriers standing**, so bought-in people cannot act and give up.
- **Declaring victory too early**, mistaking a short-term win for completion and letting urgency and momentum collapse.
- **Never anchoring in culture**, so the change is not built into norms or succession and the organization reverts to the old way once the original drivers leave.
