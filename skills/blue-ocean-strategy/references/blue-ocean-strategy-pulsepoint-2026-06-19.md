# Blue Ocean Strategy: PulsePoint

> _Illustrative example output for the `blue-ocean-strategy` skill. PulsePoint is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Business:** PulsePoint, a budget gym chain in mid-size US cities, in the fitness-club industry.
- **Current positioning:** Mid-pack on the three factors everyone competes on, equipment range, 24/7 access, and monthly price (~$29/mo).
- **Decision informed:** Whether to reposition rather than enter a price war. Main substitutes: home workouts and boutique studios.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Framework:** Kim & Mauborgne's Blue Ocean Strategy, value innovation via the strategy canvas and the ERRC four-actions grid.

---

## 1. Verdict

PulsePoint should stop selling *access to a room full of equipment* and start selling **a coached, structured fitness habit at a budget price**: the result you actually came for, not the building. Eliminate the equipment arms race and 24/7 promise, reduce floor space and contract lock-in, and fund a created factor the budget tier has never offered: a guided, app-driven program with light in-club coaching that makes a beginner feel they belong. The move targets the largest pool of non-customers in fitness, people who pay for a gym and quit, and people too intimidated to start.

**Tagline: "A coach in your pocket, a gym you'll actually keep."**

## 2. Strategy canvas

**Competing factors** (the buyer-valued attributes the industry invests in), scored 1 (low) to 5 (high) for each player and substitute:

| Competing factor | Budget chains (PulsePoint today) | Premium full-service gym | Boutique studio | Home workout (app/equipment) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Equipment range / breadth | 3 | 5 | 2 | 1 |
| 24/7 access | 4 | 3 | 1 | 5 |
| Low monthly price | 4 | 1 | 1 | 4 |
| Facilities (pool, sauna, classes) | 2 | 5 | 2 | 1 |
| Premium ambience / design | 2 | 4 | 5 | 1 |
| Coaching / guided programming | 1 | 2 | 5 | 2 |
| Community / belonging | 1 | 2 | 5 | 1 |
| Results / habit formation | 2 | 3 | 4 | 2 |
| Convenience (no commute) | 3 | 3 | 3 | 5 |

**Reading the canvas.** The three budget-tier curves (PulsePoint and its direct rivals) sit almost on top of each other: high on price and 24/7 access, low on everything that produces an actual result. That convergence: every budget gym offering more of the same equipment at a similar price, is the red-ocean signature. Premium gyms diverge upward on facilities and break the cost ceiling; boutiques diverge upward on coaching, community, and results but at $150–250/mo. **The valley nobody serves is the bottom-left-to-coaching diagonal: low price *with* coaching, belonging, and results.** That valley is where the budget tier's own churned members go, they quit not because the price was wrong but because nothing made them stick.

## 3. The ERRC grid

| **ELIMINATE** (cuts cost) | **RAISE** (adds cost, lifts value) |
|---|---|
| **Equipment-range arms race.** Stop matching rivals' machine count; stock the ~20 movements 90% of members actually use. *Cost: −, large capex and floor-space saving. Value: + for target buyer (less intimidating), neutral for gear enthusiasts (not the target).* | **Results / habit formation.** Make the visible promise "you will still be here in 6 months," tracked and celebrated, not "we have the most machines." *Cost: +, modest (software + staff time). Value: ++, this is the unmet need.* |
| **24/7 unstaffed access.** Drop the always-open, always-empty promise; run staffed hours when coaching is present. *Cost: −, security, utilities, liability. Value: roughly neutral: most budget members visit in a few predictable windows.* | **Community / belonging.** Onboarding cohorts, named coaches who know members, beginner-only hours. *Cost: +, low. Value: ++, the boutique moat, delivered at budget scale.* |
| **REDUCE** (cuts cost) | **CREATE** (adds cost, opens new demand) |
| **Footprint / square footage.** A coached-program gym needs far less floor than an equipment showroom. *Cost: −−, rent is the largest line. Value: neutral.* | **Guided digital program + light in-club coaching.** An app that assigns each member a structured plan and adapts it, paired with a floor coach during staffed hours. The factor the budget tier has never offered. *Cost: +, software amortized across the chain + part-time coaches. Value: +++, the whole move.* |
| **Contract lock-in / friction.** Drop annual contracts and cancellation mazes. *Cost: − (offset by retention from results, not handcuffs). Value: ++, removes the budget tier's most-hated attribute.* | **Beginner-safe environment.** Designed for the intimidated non-customer: no mirror walls of grunting lifters, judgment-free hours, clear "you start here" path. *Cost: +, design + scheduling. Value: ++, unlocks the largest non-customer pool.* |

Eliminate + Reduce (equipment, 24/7, floor space, contracts) free the capital that funds Raise + Create (coaching, community, the guided program). Both columns are populated: this is value innovation, not a one-sided play.

## 5. Value-innovation test

**Cost down?** Yes. Eliminate (equipment depth, 24/7 staffing/utilities) and Reduce (square footage, the dominant cost line, and contract administration) materially lower the cost base versus today's mid-pack club. A coached-program box is roughly half the floor area of an equipment showroom.

**Value up?** Yes. Raise + Create lift the curve on the three factors the budget buyer most lacks and most quits over: a guided program, belonging, and a visible result. These are boutique-grade values delivered at a budget price.

**Do Eliminate/Reduce fund Raise/Create?** Yes, directionally. Rent and equipment capex saved cover the per-member software cost and part-time coaching. The model rests on coaches serving cohorts (one coach, many members in a window), not 1:1 boutique economics; that ratio is the load-bearing assumption (see Risks).

**Focus.** The curve commits to three factors (coaching, community, results) and deliberately abandons others. Not high on everything, passes.

**Divergence.** Visibly different from all three rival profiles; it does not track the budget tier it came from. Passes.

**Tagline.** "A coach in your pocket, a gym you'll actually keep." One sharp line, speaks to the buyer's real fear (I'll quit again), states both halves of the value innovation (coaching + you'll keep it). Passes.

## 6. The move and new demand

PulsePoint becomes the **first budget gym that sells the result, not the room.** It stops competing on machine count, all-night access, and a price that is already at the floor, and instead delivers the structure, coaching, and belonging that today only the $200/mo boutique offers, at ~$29–39/mo.

**Non-customers this opens** (Kim & Mauborgne's three tiers):

- **Tier 1, soon-to-be non-customers:** the budget tier's own churned members, who quit within 90 days because nothing made them stick. The biggest and cheapest pool; PulsePoint already half-acquires them.
- **Tier 2, refusing non-customers:** people who looked at budget gyms, found them intimidating or pointless without guidance, and chose nothing. The beginner-safe environment is built for them.
- **Tier 3, unexplored non-customers:** people who assumed real coaching meant boutique prices and never considered a gym at all; they currently sit in the home-workout substitute.

**Why rivals are not already here.** Budget chains are built around amortizing equipment and real estate over high headcount at low price; their entire cost structure and brand promise is "more machines, all hours, cheap." Adding coaching and community to *that* model raises cost without removing any of it, so they read coaching as a margin-killer, not as the thing that funds itself once equipment and floor space come out. Boutiques can't drop to budget price without breaking their 1:1 coaching economics. The empty diagonal stays empty because reaching it requires the eliminations neither incumbent will make.

---

## 7. Risks

- **Coach-to-member ratio fails (the cost side breaks).** The model funds boutique-grade value only if coaches serve cohorts, not individuals. If members demand 1:1 attention, costs drift toward boutique while price stays budget, margin collapses. *Tied to: the Create move.* Mitigate by designing the program app-led and coach-supervised from day one, and pricing/scheduling around windows.
- **Eliminated factors turn out to be needed.** Cutting 24/7 access and equipment breadth may repel the segment of current members who genuinely use them and were paying the bills. *Tied to: the two Eliminate moves.* The bet is they are a minority of a churning base, but if they are the *retained* core, the move trades a paying member for a hoped-for one.
- **The app is the move, and the app is imitable.** A guided-program app is the divergence engine and also the most copyable piece. A funded rival could ship a competing app in a year. *Tied to: the Create move.* The durable moat is the in-club coaching + community ritual, not the software; weight execution there.
- **New demand won't pay for the value.** If churned and intimidated non-customers won't pay even a small premium ($29 → $39) for coaching, the raised value isn't funded by price and must be funded entirely by cost cuts, thinner than modeled. *Tied to: Raise/Create vs. the price factor.*
- **Brand drag.** "Budget gym" priors may make buyers disbelieve the coaching promise, or premium-seekers may still discount it as cheap. *Tied to: the repositioning itself.*

---

## 8. Caveats

- The framework finds the **move**, not the execution. Whether PulsePoint can hire and retain good floor coaches at budget economics is an operating question this analysis does not answer.
- The value curves are **informed judgment, not measurement.** Every 1–5 score here is illustrative; before committing capital, the convergence claim and the size of the churned/intimidated non-customer pools should be replaced with real member and market data.
- A blue ocean **attracts imitators and eventually reddens.** The coached-budget space, if it works, invites entry; the question becomes how long the community/coaching moat holds.
- The move still has to survive **cost and demand realities the canvas cannot prove**: chiefly the coach-to-member ratio and whether the target non-customers convert and pay. The canvas shows the opening; it does not guarantee the opening is profitable.
- All figures here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
