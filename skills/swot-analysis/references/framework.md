# SWOT and TOWS: framework, checklists, and scoring

SWOT sorts the factors bearing on a goal into four quadrants along two axes: internal versus external, and helpful versus harmful.

| | Helpful | Harmful |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal** (within the subject's control) | Strengths | Weaknesses |
| **External** (the environment, not controllable) | Opportunities | Threats |

Two rules decide which quadrant a factor belongs in, and getting them wrong is the most common SWOT error:

1. **Internal vs external.** Strengths and Weaknesses are attributes of the subject itself (its resources, capabilities, position). Opportunities and Threats live in the outside world (market, competitors, regulation, technology) and exist whether or not the subject does. "A growing market" is an opportunity, not a strength. "We have a strong brand" is a strength, not an opportunity.
2. **Anchor to an objective.** A SWOT is meaningless in the abstract. The same fact can be a strength for one goal and a weakness for another. Every analysis names the objective it serves first, and every factor is judged relative to that objective.

## Scoring

Tag each factor so the lists can be prioritized rather than left flat:

- **Magnitude** (1 to 5): how much this factor matters to the objective.
- For Opportunities and Threats, also **Likelihood** (1 to 5): how probable it is over the horizon.
- **Confidence**: high, medium, or low, based on evidence. Lower it when data is thin rather than inventing certainty.

Ground factors in evidence (figures, named competitors, documented trends) where retrieval tools allow.

## Quadrant checklists

### Strengths (internal, helpful)
What the subject does well and what advantages it holds. Probe: distinctive capabilities, proprietary technology or IP, brand and reputation, cost or scale advantage, financial resources, talent, customer base and loyalty, distribution, culture, efficient processes. Ask: what do we do better than others, what unique resources do we hold, what do outsiders see as our strengths.

### Weaknesses (internal, harmful)
Where the subject is deficient or disadvantaged. Probe: capability gaps, weak brand, cost disadvantage, thin finances, talent gaps, technical debt, poor processes, single points of dependency, weak position or location. Ask: what should improve, what do we lack, what do others see as our weaknesses, where do we lose.

### Opportunities (external, helpful)
Favorable conditions in the environment the subject could exploit. Scan with PESTEL plus market and competitive shifts: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal, plus market growth, unmet needs, new segments, competitor weakness, partnership openings, changing customer behavior. Ask: what trends could we ride, what needs are unmet, where are competitors exposed.

### Threats (external, harmful)
Unfavorable conditions in the environment that could harm the subject. Scan with PESTEL plus competitive forces: new entrants, substitutes, competitor moves, adverse regulation, economic downturn, technological disruption, supply or input risk, shifting customer preference, reputational risk. Ask: what could go against us, what is the competition doing, what shifts could erode our position.

## TOWS: turning the quadrants into strategy

A SWOT that stops at four lists is not finished. The TOWS matrix crosses the internal and external quadrants to generate strategic options. This is where the analysis earns its value.

| | Opportunities | Threats |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths** | **SO (offensive):** use strengths to seize opportunities | **ST (defensive):** use strengths to counter threats |
| **Weaknesses** | **WO (improvement):** fix or bypass weaknesses to capture opportunities | **WT (survival):** minimize weaknesses and avoid threats |

For each cell, pair the highest-magnitude factors and write concrete strategic options, not restatements of the factors. An SO option names what the subject should actually do to apply a specific strength to a specific opportunity. Then prioritize the resulting options across all four cells by impact on the objective and feasibility, since a SWOT typically yields more options than anyone can pursue.
