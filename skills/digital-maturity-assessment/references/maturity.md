# Digital Maturity Assessment

Digital maturity is how effectively an organization uses digital technology, data, and ways of working to create value. This assessment scores an organization across seven dimensions on a five-level scale, locates the gaps against its ambition, and produces a roadmap. It draws on the common structure shared by the established models (MIT/Capgemini, Deloitte, and similar), which agree that maturity is as much about strategy, people, and culture as about technology.

## The five maturity levels

Apply the same scale to every dimension:

1. **Nascent:** ad hoc, reactive, no strategy. Digital is isolated experiments or absent.
2. **Emerging:** pockets of activity, some pilots, but siloed and inconsistent. No coordination.
3. **Connecting:** a defined approach, coordinated across silos, standardized in parts. Investment is deliberate.
4. **Managed:** digital is integrated, measured, and governed across the organization. It is a normal part of how work is done.
5. **Leading:** digital is a core capability and source of advantage. Continuous, data-driven, adaptive, and shaping the market.

## The seven dimensions

1. **Strategy and leadership:** a clear digital vision tied to business strategy, executive ownership, governance, and committed investment.
2. **Customer experience:** digital channels, journey design, personalization, and the use of customer data to serve and retain.
3. **Technology and architecture:** the technology estate, cloud, integration, scalability, security, and the ability to change systems quickly.
4. **Data and analytics:** data quality and governance, analytics and AI capability, and how far decisions are actually driven by data.
5. **Operations and process:** digitized, automated, and agile processes, and the ability to improve them continuously.
6. **People and culture:** digital skills, talent, ways of working, leadership behavior, and the organization's appetite and capacity for change.
7. **Innovation and ecosystem:** the capability to innovate, to experiment and scale new ideas, and to work through partners and platforms.

## How to use it

1. **Set the target.** Maturity is not maximized for its own sake; the right level depends on the industry and ambition. Establish the target level per dimension (or overall) before scoring, so gaps are meaningful.
2. **Assess each dimension** against the five-level descriptors, using evidence (systems in place, metrics, behaviors, examples) rather than aspiration. Score 1 to 5 and justify it. Note that an organization is usually uneven, advanced on some dimensions and nascent on others.
3. **Find the gaps.** The gap between current and target per dimension, and the imbalances (a high technology score wasted behind a low people-and-culture score is a classic pattern).
4. **Build the roadmap.** Sequence the moves that raise the binding dimensions toward the target, with quick wins and longer plays, recognizing that the lagging dimension often caps the value of the advanced ones.

## Common pitfalls

- **Scoring on aspiration.** Strategy decks and pilots are not maturity. Score what is in production and in behavior.
- **Technology tunnel vision.** Maturity is dragged down by the weakest of strategy, people, culture, and data, not lifted by technology alone.
- **Maturity for its own sake.** Level 5 everywhere is rarely the goal. Anchor to a target that fits the ambition.
- **Average without profile.** A single overall number hides the uneven profile that actually matters; report the dimension profile, and treat the lagging dimension as the constraint.
