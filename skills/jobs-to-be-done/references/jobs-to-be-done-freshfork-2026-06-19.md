# Jobs to Be Done: FreshFork

> _Illustrative example output for the `jobs-to-be-done` skill. FreshFork is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Subject:** FreshFork, a weekly meal-kit subscription, for time-pressed dual-income households with young kids.
- **Customer:** Not a demographic. A parent in the household who has just realized, at 6pm on a weeknight, that no one planned dinner and the kids are already hungry.
- **Circumstance:** The 6pm weeknight scramble, work ran late, the fridge is a half-stocked guess, and a decision is needed in the next ten minutes.
- **Decision this informs:** Whether to add a 15-minute "express line" to the kit lineup, or to redirect that effort at the churn that shows up around month three.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Note:** The job below is **solution-agnostic**. It describes the progress this customer is trying to make, not a FreshFork roadmap. "Meal kit" is one hired solution among several; the job would survive if FreshFork disappeared.

---

## 1. The job story

> **When it's 6pm and no one planned dinner and the kids are already melting down, I want to put a real meal on the table fast without a decision or a store run, so I can feed my family tonight and feel like I've got the evening under control rather than failing at it.**

The story names a situation, a motivation, and an expected outcome, and names no product. The three dimensions it implies:

- **Functional:** Get a complete, non-junk dinner from "nothing decided" to "on the table" inside roughly 30 minutes, with no trip to a store and no separate planning step.
- **Emotional:** Stop the low-grade dread and end-of-day guilt of the scramble; feel competent and in control instead of caught out again.
- **Social:** Be seen, by a partner, by the kids, by one's own standards, as a parent who feeds the family properly, not one who defaulted to cereal or delivery a fourth night running.

The functional job is what most meal-kit marketing addresses. The emotional and social jobs are what actually drive the switch, and the churn.

## 2. The job in detail

Five facets, each from a parallel analyst. Every material claim is marked **fact** (evidence exists) or **assumption** (a hypothesis still to test). Confidence is lowered where the evidence was thin.

### 2.1 The functional job

**Summary.** The practical work is to convert an unplanned, time-boxed evening into a finished family dinner, without the two steps the customer hates most: deciding what to make, and acquiring the ingredients. The job is *not* "cook"; cooking is a means. The job is "get fed, properly, now, without the upstream decisions."

| Functional sub-task | Evidence / reasoning | Fact/assumption |
|---|---|---|
| Eliminate the decision ("what's for dinner?") | Recurring theme in support chats and cancellation surveys: "I just don't want to think about it" | Fact (support logs) |
| Avoid the store run on a weeknight | Onboarding survey: 71% cite "no time to shop midweek" as a top-2 reason to subscribe | Fact (survey) |
| Produce a meal in the time available (~30 min) | Stated prep times cluster at 30–40 min; the complaint is they run long with kids underfoot | Fact (recipe data + reviews) |
| Feed everyone, including picky kids, in one cook | "Had to make a second dinner for the 5-year-old" is a frequent 2-star review | Fact (reviews) |
| Keep cleanup minimal | Mentioned but secondary; one-pan recipes rate higher | Assumption (correlation, not tested) |

**Confidence: High.** The functional job is well-evidenced and stable.

**Key tension for the express-line decision:** stated 30-minute kits are already missing the *actual* time available at 6pm (closer to 15–20 minutes before the meltdown peaks). The functional gap is real and it points toward speed.

### 2.2 The emotional and social job

**Summary.** Underneath the timer is an emotional job: converting nightly dread and guilt into a sense of control and adequacy, and a social job, sustaining an identity as a competent, caring parent in front of partner, kids, and self. These dimensions, not the recipes, predict who stays.

| Dimension | What the customer is trying to feel / signal | Evidence | Fact/assumption |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emotional: relief from the scramble dread | "The Sunday box means I don't have the 6pm panic" appears in promoter NPS comments | Fact (NPS verbatims) |
| Emotional: feel competent, not rescued | Pride in "I made this": kits that feel like assembly, not cooking, draw "might as well order takeout" | Assumption (interview hunch, n small) |
| Emotional: guilt relief about feeding kids well | "At least I know they ate vegetables" recurs | Fact (reviews/verbatims) |
| Social: be the parent who has it together | Photos of plated kit meals shared in family group chats; cited in win-back calls | Assumption (anecdotal) |
| Social: not be seen defaulting to delivery again | Delivery framed as the shameful fallback in churn interviews | Assumption (interview hunch) |

**Confidence: Medium.** Emotional signals are strong in verbatims; the social job is plausible but rests on thin, qualitative evidence.

**Key tension:** a 15-minute express line risks *winning the functional job while losing the emotional one*: if "express" feels like reheating, it satisfies speed but undercuts the "I made this / I've got it together" payoff that retains customers. This is the central risk in the decision.

### 2.3 Desired outcomes

| # | Outcome statement | Imp | Sat |
|---|---|---|---|
| O1 | Minimize the time from "no plan" to "meal on the table" on an unplanned weeknight | 9 | 4 |
| O2 | Minimize the likelihood of having to make a second meal for a picky child | 8 | 3 |
| O3 | Minimize the number of evening decisions the parent has to make about dinner | 9 | 7 |
| O4 | Reduce the likelihood of a midweek store run to complete a meal | 8 | 8 |
| O5 | Minimize the active attention cooking demands while supervising kids | 8 | 4 |
| O6 | Increase the parent's sense of having fed the family something healthy | 7 | 6 |
| O7 | Minimize the cleanup time after the weeknight meal | 6 | 5 |
| O8 | Increase the feeling of having cooked it myself (vs. assembled/reheated) | 6 | 7 |
| O9 | Minimize the upfront time to plan and order the week's meals | 7 | 7 |

**Confidence: Low–Medium.** The *list* of outcomes is well-grounded in reviews and support data; the *scores* are internal judgment and the largest gap in this analysis.

### 2.4 Competing solutions (including workarounds and nonconsumption)

The full set the customer hires at 6pm: not just rival meal kits. Nonconsumption and workarounds are the loudest signal here.

| Solution | Type | Where it falls short for this job | Fact/assumption |
|---|---|---|---|
| HelloFresh / Blue Apron | Direct rival | Same 30–40 min cook; same picky-kid and time-overrun complaints | Fact (category reviews) |
| Grocery prepared-meal section | Indirect | Requires the store run the customer is trying to avoid; feels like "giving up" | Assumption |
| Takeout / delivery (DoorDash, pizza) | Indirect / cross-category | Wins speed decisively; loses the emotional + social job (guilt, "again?"), and the cost adds up | Fact (churn interviews cite delivery as the fallback) |
| Pantry/freezer scramble (pasta, nuggets, eggs) | **Workaround** | The default fallback; fast but reintroduces the decision and the guilt: the exact pains the job is about | Fact (the "what I do when I skip the box" answer) |
| Batch-cooked Sunday leftovers | **Workaround** | Solves the decision but demands weekend planning labor and gets monotonous; abandoned by Wednesday | Assumption |
| **Skip dinner / cereal / "fend for yourselves"** | **Nonconsumption** | Abandons the job; the highest-guilt outcome and the one the customer most wants to never repeat | Fact (named in surveys as the worst night) |

**Confidence: Medium–High.** The competitive set is clear; the relative frequency of each fallback is estimated, not measured.

**The signal:** the biggest competitors at 6pm are **delivery (wins speed) and the pantry scramble + nonconsumption (the guilt-laden defaults)**: not other meal kits. Customers don't churn to Blue Apron; they churn to DoorDash and to "fend for yourselves." That is where the job is underserved.

### 2.5 The four forces of progress

For the switch *into* a FreshFork-style subscription, and the switch *out* (churn at ~month three).

| | Drives the switch | Resists the switch |
|---|---|---|
| **Tied to the present** | **Push:** the recurring 6pm dread; guilt over delivery/cereal nights; the felt incompetence of being caught out again. Strong and chronic. | **Habit:** delivery and the pantry scramble are zero-onboarding, zero-commitment defaults that already "work." Strong. |
| **Tied to the new** | **Pull:** a planned, no-decision, no-store dinner that makes me feel like I've got it together. Moderate–strong at signup. | **Anxiety:** "boxes will pile up unused," "it'll still take too long with the kids," "I'll be locked into a subscription I forget to skip." Strong, and it grows after a few overrun weeknights. |

**Read on the balance:**
- **At signup, Push + Pull beats Anxiety + Habit**: the dread is acute and the promise is fresh. This is why acquisition works.
- **By month three, the balance inverts.** The Pull decays (boxes feel like a chore, recipes blur together), Anxiety is confirmed (kits ran long, a second kid-meal was needed, two boxes went uncooked), and Habit reasserts (delivery is right there). **Push + Pull no longer beats Anxiety + Habit, so the customer fires the kit.** This is the churn mechanism, stated in forces.

**Confidence: Medium.** The signup balance is well-supported; the month-three inversion is a coherent reading of the churn data but not yet validated against individual customer timelines.

## 3. Opportunity ranking

`Opportunity = Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)`. Sorted high to low. Underserved (high importance, low satisfaction) called out first.

| Rank | Outcome | Imp | Sat | Opportunity | Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | O1: time from "no plan" to "meal on table" | 9 | 4 | **14** | **Underserved** |
| 2 | O2: avoid making a second meal for a picky kid | 8 | 3 | **13** | **Underserved** |
| 3 | O5: minimize active attention while supervising kids | 8 | 4 | **12** | **Underserved** |
| 4 | O3: minimize evening decisions about dinner | 9 | 7 | 11 | Served (near table stakes) |
| 5 | O6: feeling of having fed the family healthily | 7 | 6 | 8 | Served |
| 6 | O4: avoid the midweek store run | 8 | 8 | 8 | Served (table stakes) |
| 7 | O7: minimize cleanup time | 6 | 5 | 7 | Served |
| 8 | O9: minimize weekly planning/ordering time | 7 | 7 | 7 | Served |
| 9 | O8: feeling of having cooked it myself | 6 | 7 | 6 | **Overserved**: do not over-invest |

**Underserved, in order: O1 (speed), O2 (one meal for the whole family), O5 (low-attention cooking).** These three share a root: *the meal demands too much time and attention at the exact moment the customer has the least of both.* That cluster, not flavor variety or recipe novelty, is the opportunity.

**Note on O8 (overserved):** "feeling I cooked it myself" scores *higher* satisfaction than importance, current kits already deliver enough of the cooking-pride feeling. This matters directly to the express-line question: a faster, lighter-touch line can sacrifice some O8 without much loss, *provided* it doesn't tip into "this is just reheating," which would re-damage the emotional job (§2.2).

## 4. Implications

### 4.1 Where current solutions fail

Crossing the competitive set (§2.4) against the underserved outcomes (§3):

- **No solution serves O1+O5 (speed + low attention) well for this circumstance.** Meal kits target ~30 min of attentive cooking; the customer has ~15 min of *divided* attention. Delivery wins speed but forfeits the emotional/social job. The pantry scramble wins speed but is the guilt itself. **The underserved outcome is "a real, parent-credible dinner in the actual time and attention available", and nobody owns it.**
- **No solution serves O2 (one meal for the whole family).** The "second meal for the picky kid" is a near-universal complaint with no good answer in the category. This is a quiet, large, unserved outcome.
- The workarounds and nonconsumption (scramble, cereal, "fend for yourselves") are the strongest evidence: customers regularly *abandon a paid subscription* to do something worse, because the kit didn't fit the 6pm reality. They are firing the kit for the very job it claimed to do.

### 4.2 Force balance and the lever

The forces favor a switch **at signup** but invert **by month three** (§2.5). The churn is not an acquisition problem; it's a sustain problem.

- **Raising Pull (more recipes, new cuisines)** is the instinctive move and the weakest lever here, Pull isn't where the balance breaks. O8 being overserved confirms the customer doesn't need *more* cooking richness.
- **The decisive lever is cutting Anxiety and the confirmed-Anxiety experience that drives month-three churn:** the kit running long, the second kid-meal, the uncooked boxes. Every one of those maps to an underserved outcome (O1, O2, O5). **Deliver the underserved cluster and you remove the very experiences that flip the force balance.**
- **Loosening Habit** (one-tap skip, easy pause, delivery-speed framing) helps at the margin but is secondary to fixing the experience.

### 4.3 Concrete moves: the express-line vs. churn decision

The question was: add a 15-minute express line, or fight churn? The analysis says **these are the same move, if the express line is built to serve the underserved cluster rather than as a separate speed gimmick.**

1. **Build the express line, but spec it against O1+O5+O2, not just "15 minutes."** The win condition is *real dinner, ~15 min, divided attention, one pan, feeds the picky kid too*, not "fastest possible." Speed alone (O1) without the family-fit (O2) and low-attention (O5) outcomes would still churn.
2. **Protect the emotional job (§2.2) explicitly.** Frame and design express meals as *cooking made fast*, not *reheating*, a hot pan, a fresh step the parent does, plating that photographs well. O8 is overserved, so you have room to lighten the cooking, but tipping into "assembly" forfeits the "I've got it together" payoff that retains. This is the single biggest risk in the decision.
3. **Solve O2 inside the express line.** A "one pan, both a kid-friendly and an adult version from the same base" format directly attacks the second-most-underserved outcome and a top churn complaint. No rival owns this.
4. **Re-aim retention messaging at the month-three inversion, not at flavor variety.** Skip/pause that is one tap; proactive "fast night?" nudges that surface express options before the box goes uncooked; framing that beats delivery on guilt, not on speed.
5. **Before committing build cost, replace the assumed scores (§2.3) with a real customer survey of Importance/Satisfaction** on these nine outcomes, and validate the month-three inversion against actual churned-cohort timelines. The opportunity ranking is directionally strong but rests on team estimates.

**The verdict for the decision:** the express line and the churn fight are not alternatives, the churn is *caused* by failing the underserved speed/attention/family-fit cluster, and a properly-scoped express line is the instrument that serves it. Build the express line *as the retention play*, spec it to O1/O2/O5, and guard the emotional job while doing it.

## 5. Caveats

- JTBD describes the customer's **motivation and the progress they seek**: not a product roadmap, a market size, or a financial case. The moves in §4.3 are implied by the job; whether they sell, and at what margin, is a separate question this analysis does not answer.
- The **Importance/Satisfaction scores in §2.3 are team estimates, not customer survey data.** They are assumptions. The opportunity ranking is only as good as those numbers; re-measure with real customers before allocating build budget (Move 5).
- Several emotional and social claims (§2.2) and the **month-three force inversion (§2.5)** rest on thin or anecdotal evidence. They are coherent readings, flagged as assumptions, and are the items most worth validating with interviews.
- The job is **solution-agnostic by construction.** "Express line" is one way to serve the underserved cluster; the analysis identifies *what* progress to enable, not that this specific feature is the only or best instrument.
- All figures, quotes, and ratings here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
