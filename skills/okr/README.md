# okr

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) turn strategy into measurable goals. The method came out of Intel under Andy Grove and reached a wide audience through John Doerr's *Measure What Matters* (2018), which is where most teams pick it up. The idea is a pairing: a qualitative objective says what you want to achieve and why it matters, and 3-5 quantitative key results prove it, so that hitting every key result means the objective is met by definition.

This skill facilitates one drafting cycle. It clarifies the objective as a direction-setting sentence with the metrics stripped out, drafts the key results with numbers and baselines, then stress-tests them with a small set of critics running in parallel, one checking whether each key result is an outcome rather than a task, one whether it is actually measurable, one whether the set fully covers the objective, and one whether the ambition is sandbagged or impossible. After the rewrite it sets the ambition level, cadence, owners, and scoring, and checks how the OKRs ladder up to higher-level goals. The output is a saved OKR document with a check log.

The critics exist because of where OKRs usually go wrong. Key results drift into task lists that can all be done while the objective still fails. Objectives turn into vanity statements that sound good and commit to nothing. And targets get sandbagged so every score comes in at 1.0. The skill names committed versus aspirational OKRs so a 0.7 reads correctly, and it keeps scoring separate from compensation, since tying the two together pushes teams to sandbag their targets. OKRs are the goal-setting layer. Works alongside the balanced-scorecard skill for the wider performance-measurement frame.
