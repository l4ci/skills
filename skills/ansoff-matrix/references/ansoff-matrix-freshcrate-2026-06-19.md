# Ansoff Matrix: FreshCrate

> _Illustrative example output for the `ansoff-matrix` skill. FreshCrate is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Subject:** FreshCrate, a UK meal-kit subscription service with ~80,000 active subscribers.
- **Objective:** Double revenue within three years (≈ £42m → £85m run-rate) without raising churn.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Context:** Strong in London and the South East, flat in the Midlands; kitchen and logistics capacity at ~60%; no presence in ready-to-eat or retail; a chilled supply chain that is hard to extend beyond a two-day delivery radius.

---

Top options per quadrant, with risk at a glance. Risk rises down-left to bottom-right.

| | **Existing products** | **New products** |
|---|---|---|

## 2. Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets)

Lowest risk: known product, known customers. With capacity at 60% and a flat Midlands aside, the London/SE base is where the cheapest revenue still sits.

| Option | Risk | Main risk sources | Capabilities required | Capability fit | Investment | Reward |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reactivate lapsed subscribers (win-back offers to the ~120k who churned in 24 mo) | **Low** | Discount cannibalises active base; reactivated cohorts re-churn fast | CRM, lifecycle marketing, win-back creative | **Have**: existing CRM stack | £0.3–0.6m / yr | +£3–5m run-rate at ~2.5x LTV/CAC; protects churn if targeted |
| Lift order frequency (à la carte sides, breakfast/weekend boxes for current members) | **Low** | Adds SKUs to picking; margin dilution on low-AOV add-ons | Menu ops, fulfilment slotting | **Mostly have**: uses spare kitchen capacity | £0.4m | +£4–7m; raises AOV ~12–18% with no new acquisition |
| Annual / prepaid plans + loyalty to cut churn | **Low** | Deferred-revenue accounting; discounting margin | Billing, pricing, retention analytics | **Have** | £0.2m | Churn -1.5 to -2.5pts; compounds every other option's LTV |
| Win share from rivals in London/SE via referral + paid | **Low–Med** | Rising CAC in a saturated metro; share-of-wallet ceiling | Performance marketing, referral mechanics | **Have**, but CAC is climbing | £1.5–3m / yr | +£6–10m, but the most expensive marginal subscriber |

**Quadrant read:** the single highest-confidence, lowest-cost lever set. None requires the constrained cold chain to stretch. The frequency and retention plays are nearly pure margin on idle capacity: they should be running regardless of any expansion decision.

---

## 3. Market Development (existing products, new markets)

Same kits, new buyers. Medium risk concentrated in market unfamiliarity and, critically, the two-day chilled-delivery radius.

| Option | Risk | Main risk sources | Capabilities required | Capability fit | Investment | Reward |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fill the Midlands (Birmingham/Nottingham/Leicester) with existing kits | **Med** | Flat today for a reason: demand or last-mile cost unproven; within radius but routes are thinner | Regional marketing, route density build-up, depot/hub | **Partial**: fulfilment exists, density does not | £2–4m | +£8–14m if density reaches London-like economics; the largest "same product" prize |
| Office / corporate lunch & team boxes | **Med** | Different buying cycle (B2B), invoicing, weekday-only demand | B2B sales motion, account billing | **Gap**: no B2B sales team | £1–2m | +£4–8m; higher AOV, lower per-order CAC, but new muscle |
| White-label B2B kits for gyms / clinics / wellness brands | **Med–High** | Channel-partner dependency; margin shared; bespoke menus | Partnerships, co-branding, flexible production | **Gap** | £1.5–3m | +£3–6m; diversifies acquisition but dilutes brand control |

**Quadrant read:** the Midlands is the natural next step *because the product and the cold chain already reach it*: it is market development with the smallest unfamiliarity gap. The B2B options are attractive on unit economics but require sales capabilities FreshCrate does not yet have; treat them as a second wave, not a first move.

---

## 4. Product Development (new products, existing markets)

New products to the loyal London/SE base. Medium risk in execution. The base is the asset; the constraint is the cold chain and the lack of any non-kit product muscle.

| Option | Risk | Main risk sources | Capabilities required | Capability fit | Investment | Reward |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Premium / dietary tiers (high-protein, low-cal, family) on the existing kit platform | **Low–Med** | Menu R&D cost; tier cannibalisation | Culinary R&D, pricing tiers | **Have**: same fulfilment, new recipes | £0.5–1m | +£5–9m; raises ARPU, sharpens positioning, near-zero new ops risk |
| Ready-to-eat chilled meals to current subscribers (heat-and-eat, no cooking) | **Med** | Two-day chilled radius limits reach; new production line; shorter shelf life | Chilled RTE production, new line, food-safety regime | **Gap**: no RTE capability | £4–7m capex | +£10–16m; large adjacent occasion, but a real build |
| Pantry & grocery add-on marketplace (oils, snacks, drinks bolted onto a box) | **Med** | Inventory/SKU sprawl; thin grocery margins | Merchandising, 3P inventory or dropship | **Partial** | £1–2m | +£3–6m; lifts AOV, but a distraction if it sprawls |

**Quadrant read:** premium/dietary tiers are quietly the best risk-adjusted move in the whole matrix: they reuse every existing capability and lift ARPU on the exact base FreshCrate retains best. Ready-to-eat is the strategically biggest product bet but the first one that demands genuinely new operational capability and capex.

---

## 5. Diversification (new products, new markets)

New products *and* new markets, nothing is known territory. All three are at least one bridge too far while penetration and development remain open. Noted as related vs. unrelated.

| Option | Type | Risk | Main risk sources | Capability fit | Investment | Reward |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Own-brand chilled RTE SKU on supermarket shelves nationwide | **Related** | **High** | Retail listing/slotting fees, trade margin, ambient brand vs. fresh promise, retailer power | **Large gap**: no retail or trade-sales capability | £6–12m | +£12–25m *if* listed and it rotates: but high failure rate, brand dilution risk |
| Frozen ready meals shipped nationwide by parcel courier | **Related** | **High** | New product (frozen ≠ chilled), new market (national), new logistics (frozen parcel): three unknowns at once | **Large gap** | £8–15m | Escapes the two-day radius entirely; large TAM, but bets the balance sheet |
| FreshCrate-branded cafés / dark kitchens (delivery food) | **Unrelated** | **Very High** | Property, hospitality ops, aggregator economics: a different business | **No fit** | £10m+ | Speculative; no transferable capability advantage |

**Quadrant read:** every option here stacks two or three simultaneous unknowns. The retail and frozen plays are *related* (they share the food brand and customer type), which is why they are the only diversification moves worth even holding on the roadmap: but both demand capabilities FreshCrate wholly lacks and would be premature with the core market unexhausted. The café idea is unrelated conglomerate diversification with no capability bridge; it should be off the table.

## 6. Prioritized options

Ranked by fit to the objective (double revenue, hold churn) and risk-adjusted reward. Lower-risk, capability-matched moves rank first; capability gaps push otherwise attractive moves down.

| Rank | Option | Quadrant | Risk | Cap. fit | ~Reward (run-rate) | Why here |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Frequency + add-on sides/weekend boxes | Penetration | Low | Have | +£4–7m | Pure margin on idle capacity; no new risk; supports the no-churn constraint |
| 2 | Annual/prepaid + loyalty (churn reduction) | Penetration | Low | Have | Churn -2pts | Directly serves the "without raising churn" objective; compounds all LTV |
| 3 | Premium / dietary tiers | Product Dev | Low–Med | Have | +£5–9m | Best ARPU lift per pound; reuses every capability |
| 4 | Reactivate lapsed subscribers | Penetration | Low | Have | +£3–5m | Cheap revenue from known buyers; watch re-churn |
| 5 | Fill the Midlands with existing kits | Market Dev | Med | Partial | +£8–14m | Largest "same product" prize; within the cold-chain radius |
| 6 | Ready-to-eat chilled range | Product Dev | Med | Gap | +£10–16m | Big adjacent occasion, but first move needing new line + capex |
| 7 | Office / corporate lunch channel | Market Dev | Med | Gap | +£4–8m | Strong unit economics; needs a B2B sales build |
| 8 | Pantry/grocery add-on marketplace | Product Dev | Med | Partial | +£3–6m | Useful AOV lift; risk of SKU sprawl |
| 9 | Own-brand retail chilled SKU | Diversification (related) | High | Large gap | +£12–25m | Big but unproven; new capability stack; hold for a trigger |
| 10 | Frozen nationwide ready meals | Diversification (related) | High | Large gap | large/uncertain | Only path past the two-day radius; bets the balance sheet |

**Pitfall flag:** the tempting "double revenue" instinct is to leap to retail or frozen (ranks 9–10) for their headline TAM. But penetration (ranks 1–4) and the Midlands (rank 5) are still wide open and far cheaper. Reaching for high-risk diversification while the home market is unexhausted is the classic Ansoff error: and FreshCrate lacks the retail/frozen capabilities those moves require.

## 7. Recommended growth path

A sequence over the three-year horizon, each step funding and de-risking the next. The path is designed to clear the £42m → £85m gap without a churn-raising forced march.

**Now → Month 9, Exhaust penetration + launch tiers (low risk, self-funding).**
Run ranks 1–4 in parallel: frequency add-ons, prepaid/loyalty, lapsed-subscriber win-back, and premium/dietary tiers. All reuse existing capability and idle capacity. *Capabilities needed:* none new, lifecycle CRM, menu R&D, pricing.
*Target contribution:* +£14–22m run-rate. *Guardrail:* tiers and add-ons must not push monthly churn above baseline; kill any discount cohort whose 90-day re-churn exceeds the active base.

**Month 6 → Month 24, Develop the Midlands (medium risk, funded by Stage 1).**
Build route density in 2–3 Midlands metros with existing kits. Treat it as a density problem, not a marketing problem, the London moat was geographic. *Capabilities to build:* regional hub/depot, last-mile density, regional acquisition. *Trigger to scale:* a pilot city reaches CAC payback < 4 months and contribution margin within 5pts of London. *Target contribution:* +£8–14m run-rate.

**Month 18 → Month 36, Build ready-to-eat for the existing base (medium risk, deliberate capex).**
Stand up a chilled RTE line and sell heat-and-eat meals to the London/SE/Midlands base inside the two-day radius. This is the first move requiring genuinely new operational capability, so it follows only once Stages 1–2 prove the revenue trajectory. *Capabilities to build:* RTE production line, food-safety regime, shorter-shelf-life logistics. *Target contribution:* +£10–16m run-rate.

**Trigger-gated, not scheduled, Retail / frozen diversification.**
Hold ranks 9–10 off the timeline. Only justified if (a) Stages 1–3 fall short of the £85m target *and* (b) FreshCrate has acquired or hired real retail-trade or frozen-logistics capability, *or* (c) the two-day chilled radius becomes a hard ceiling on growth. Until a trigger fires, these stay as options, not plans. The café idea is rejected outright.

Stacked, Stages 1–3 plausibly reach +£32–52m run-rate, enough to clear the doubling objective *without* invoking diversification, which is the point.

---

## 8. Verdict

**Double revenue by mining the home market and one adjacent market first, not by diversifying.** FreshCrate's objective is reachable through the three lower-risk quadrants alone, and its capability profile insists on it: penetration and product-tier moves reuse everything it already does well, the Midlands is market development *within* the cold-chain radius, and ready-to-eat is the one bigger bet that still serves a base it understands.

**First concrete moves (next two quarters):** (1) launch premium/dietary tiers on the existing platform; (2) ship prepaid/annual plans and a loyalty mechanic to bend churn down; (3) turn on add-on sides and weekend boxes to lift AOV on idle capacity; (4) run a targeted lapsed-subscriber win-back. These are low-risk, capability-matched, and self-funding, and they explicitly protect the "without raising churn" constraint while buying the runway to develop the Midlands next.

Diversification into retail or frozen, the headline-grabbing way to "double revenue", is deferred behind explicit triggers, because it asks for capabilities FreshCrate does not have and would be premature while the home market and the Midlands remain open.

---

## 9. Caveats

- **Risk rises with unfamiliarity, but capability gaps drive risk as much as market novelty.** The retail and frozen options are rated high-risk less because the markets are new than because FreshCrate has *no retail-trade or frozen-logistics capability*, the gap is the risk.
- **New markets fail for reasons invisible from the home market.** The Midlands is flat today for a reason that London data cannot explain; the office/B2B channel runs on a buying cycle FreshCrate has never sold into. Validate locally before scaling.
- **The matrix prioritizes options; it does not validate demand.** Nothing here proves customers want ready-to-eat meals or that Midlands density is achievable. Pilot the top moves (a tier launch, one Midlands metro) before committing capex.
- All figures, subscriber counts, and revenue targets are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
