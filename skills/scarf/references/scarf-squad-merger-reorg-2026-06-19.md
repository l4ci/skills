# SCARF: Squad-Merger Reorg Announcement

> _Illustrative example output for the `scarf` skill. This reorg and the people in it are fictional; every name, reaction, and detail below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Situation:** A Friday all-hands announcement that the two product squads are merging into one, under a new shared lead. As part of the merge, three current tech leads will become senior individual contributors reporting to that new lead.
- **People analyzed:** The three tech leads losing their lead title (Priya, Marcus, Dani, named for the read; pseudonymous), and the ~18 engineers across both squads who do not yet know which manager they will report to.
- **Context:** The last reorg, ~18 months ago, was sprung with no notice; people are still sore about it. The goal is to reach Friday with the team engaged, not defensive.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Note:** SCARF is a heuristic for noticing social threat, not a measurement instrument. The severities below are analyst judgment, not readings.

---

## Verdict

This announcement lands hardest in **Status** and **Fairness**, and the two compound each other. The three tech leads take a visible demotion (lead title → senior IC), which is a textbook Status threat; and because the last reorg was sprung with no notice, every recipient will read *this* announcement against that memory and ask "is this being done *to* us again?", a Fairness threat that colors how all the other domains are received. Certainty is a close third: 18 engineers not knowing their manager is unresolved ambiguity at the exact moment you need them open.

The first move is not a better-worded all-hands. It is **talking to the three tech leads privately before Friday** (defuses Status and the worst of Fairness at once), and **showing the team the reasoning and the process** rather than just the outcome (defuses Fairness, which is the wound the last reorg left). Polishing the plan's detail would over-serve Certainty while leaving the Status and Fairness threats live.

| Domain | Threat | Reward | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Status** | **High** | Low | High |
| **Certainty** | High | Low | High |
| **Autonomy** | Medium | Low | Medium |
| **Relatedness** | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| **Fairness** | **High** | Low | High |

**Most at risk: Status and Fairness.** These set the agenda; the rest follow.

## Domain reads

### Status: relative importance to others

- **Threat (High).** Three tech leads lose their lead title and scope and move from leading to reporting under a single new lead. Loss of title and scope is one of the sharpest Status triggers there is, and it is happening in public, at an all-hands, in front of the same engineers they used to lead. The comparison is not abstract: Priya, Marcus, and Dani will be measured by their teams against who they were last week. A public, unframed announcement maximizes the loss of face.
- **Reward (Low).** "Senior IC" can be positioned as a respected craft track, and the new shared lead role is a Status reward for whoever gets it, but only one person, and not the three in question. As written, the announcement offers the three nothing that raises status.
- **Severity rationale:** High threat, low reward, the gap is wide and the loss is public.
- **Who feels it most:** The three tech leads, acutely and personally. The 18 engineers feel it secondhand (their leads being demoted is unsettling) but it is not their own status on the line.
- **Levers:**
  - Tell the three privately, before Friday, framed against their own trajectory ("you've outgrown the coordination overhead; this puts you back on the hardest technical problems"), not against the new lead.
  - Name a concrete, higher-status remit for each in the new structure (architecture ownership, a staff track, tech-lead-of-a-domain) so "senior IC" is a step toward something, not a step down.
  - At the all-hands, have the new lead publicly credit what the three built; protect their face rather than narrating a demotion.

### Certainty: ability to predict the future

- **Threat (High).** Eighteen engineers will leave the all-hands not knowing who their manager is. Reporting line is one of the most prediction-critical facts in anyone's work life, and leaving it open spikes Certainty hard. "We'll figure out reporting lines later" reads as withheld information, which is worse than bad news clearly stated.
- **Reward (Low).** The merge itself, *if* sequenced and dated, can become a Certainty reward (a clear plan beats a vague one). As written there is no timeline, so the reward is unrealized.
- **Severity rationale:** High threat because the single most-asked question (who's my manager?) is unanswered; reward is available but not yet built in.
- **Who feels it most:** The 18 engineers, evenly. The three tech leads also feel it (what exactly does "senior IC" mean for my work?) but their bigger wound is Status.
- **Levers:**
  - State at the all-hands what is *decided* (the merge, the new lead) and what is *still open* (specific reporting lines), with the date each open item resolves, "you'll know your manager by next Friday," not "soon."
  - Name what will *not* change: pay, location, current projects, on-call rotation, whatever is genuinely staying put. Listing the unchanged shrinks the ambiguity.
  - Sequence the merge into dated stages (announce → reporting lines → first joint planning) so the change is a path, not a cliff.

### Autonomy: sense of control and choice

- **Threat (Medium).** The reorg is a top-down mandate; no one was offered a choice in it. For the three tech leads the loss of a lead role is also a loss of decision scope. For the 18, having a manager assigned without input is a control loss. It is medium rather than high because reorg decisions are legitimately leadership's to make, and people grant that, what they resent is having *zero* say in the parts that could reasonably involve them.
- **Reward (Low).** None offered as written.
- **Severity rationale:** A real but expected control loss; bounded because some of it is structurally unavoidable.
- **Who feels it most:** The three tech leads (scope loss), then the 18 (assigned manager). Engineers with strong preferences about who they work with feel the assignment most.
- **Levers:**
  - Where the decision genuinely allows it, give the 18 a voice in reporting lines (preference survey, conversations) before finalizing, opt-in beats assignment even if not everyone gets their first choice.
  - Offer the three tech leads a real choice about their next focus area within the new structure (which domain, which problem), so they steer the *how* even though the title change is fixed.
  - Be honest about the boundary: name what is decided and non-negotiable, then hand back genuine latitude inside it rather than faking consultation on a closed question.

### Relatedness: sense of safety and belonging with others

- **Threat (Medium).** Two squads merging means two in-groups becoming one, with a new lead who may be an out-group figure to at least one side. Merges quietly create us-versus-them ("their squad" vs "ours") and the new lead starts as a stranger, default out-group until connection is built. Medium because both squads already share a company and likely some history, so they are not strangers, but the merge reshuffles the lines of belonging.
- **Reward (Medium).** A genuine shared goal (one product, one team) is a Relatedness reward if framed as joining forces rather than absorption. There is real raw material here for an approach response.
- **Severity rationale:** Threat and reward are both live; which one wins depends almost entirely on framing.
- **Who feels it most:** Whichever squad perceives itself as the one being absorbed; the new lead, who must convert from stranger to in-group fast.
- **Levers:**
  - Frame the merge as one new team with a shared mission, explicitly *not* "Squad A absorbs Squad B"; choose a new team name/identity rather than keeping one squad's.
  - Have the new lead do deliberate 1:1 connection with every engineer in the first two weeks, connection before content, so they stop being a stranger.
  - Create early shared wins (a joint kickoff, a first goal the merged team owns together) so belonging is built around something, not announced.

### Fairness: perception of fair exchange

- **Threat (High).** This is the domain the *history* loads. The last reorg was sprung with no notice and people are still sore, so this announcement does not arrive on a blank slate; it arrives pre-suspected. If the criteria for who became the new lead, and why three leads were stepped down, are not explained, people will fill the gap with "favoritism" and "done to us, not with us." Unfairness fires a strong threat response, and an unexplained reorg on top of a remembered one is the strongest version of it.
- **Reward (Low).** None as written. The reward is fully available, explaining the why, showing the criteria, giving voice, but the announcement as drafted delivers an outcome with no rationale, which is the Fairness anti-pattern.
- **Severity rationale:** High, and the most leveraged of the five because it gates how every other domain is received. A team that feels the process was fair will extend benefit of the doubt on Status, Certainty, and Autonomy; a team that feels ambushed again will read threat into all of them.
- **Who feels it most:** Everyone, but most sharply the people who remember the last reorg and the three tech leads, who will most want to know *why them*.
- **Levers:**
  - Explain the *why* of the whole merge (the business reasoning) and the *why* of the role changes (the criteria for the new lead, the rationale for the IC moves), make the trade-offs visible rather than hidden.
  - Name the last reorg directly and say what is being done differently this time (notice, explanation, voice). Acknowledging the old wound is what converts "here we go again" into "okay, they heard us."
  - Give a genuine voice before finalizing the open pieces (reporting lines), so the decision is made *with* people on the parts that can be, not purely *to* them.

## Synthesis

### Ranked threats

1. **Fairness (High)**: gates everything; loaded by the sprung last reorg.
2. **Status (High)**: three public demotions, personal and acute.
3. **Certainty (High)**: 18 unknown reporting lines; the most-asked question, unanswered.
4. **Autonomy (Medium)**: expected top-down loss of control and scope.
5. **Relatedness (Medium)**: two in-groups merging; salvageable with framing.

Three domains sit at High. By the asymmetry principle, removing these beats adding any new reward. Fairness is ranked first not because it is "worse" than the Status demotion in isolation, but because it is the lens: a team that judges the *process* unfair will read threat into Status, Certainty, and Autonomy alike, while a team that judges it fair extends slack across all of them.

### Cross-domain check

The trap here is **fixing Certainty to feel like progress while Status and Fairness stay live.** Writing a crisp, detailed announcement deck, timeline, org chart, FAQ, is satisfying and easy, and it does serve Certainty. But the real wounds are the three public demotions (Status) and the memory of being ambushed (Fairness). A polished plan delivered cold to the three tech leads at an all-hands would *increase* the Status and Fairness threat, not lower it, however clear the slides. Fix the domains that carry the threat: handle the three privately, and lead with rationale and acknowledgment, *then* let the clear plan serve Certainty on top.

### Designed moves per at-risk domain

**Fairness (defuse first, it gates the rest).**
- Before Friday, write and deliver the *why*: the business reason for the merge and the explicit criteria behind the leadership and IC decisions.
- In the all-hands, name the last reorg out loud and state what is different this time: advance notice, an explanation, and a real voice on the open pieces.
- Open a genuine input window on reporting lines before they are locked.

**Status (defuse before it becomes public).**
- Tell Priya, Marcus, and Dani privately, each in a 1:1, before any group hears it, never let the all-hands be how they learn it.
- Frame the IC move against their own growth and a concrete higher remit, not against the new lead.
- Have the new lead publicly credit their work at the all-hands; protect face.

**Certainty (build the reward, then let it carry).**
- Separate what is decided (merge, lead) from what is open (reporting lines), and date every open item.
- List what is *not* changing.
- Sequence the rollout into dated stages.

**Autonomy (give back what the structure allows).**
- Voice on reporting-line preferences for the 18; choice of focus area for the three.

**Relatedness (frame for joining, not absorption).**
- One new team identity; new lead does 1:1 connection in the first two weeks; an early shared win.

## Priority moves

Do these three first, they remove the most threat for the effort:

1. **Talk to the three tech leads privately, 1:1, before Friday.** *Defuses:* Status (no public-blindside demotion) and the sharpest edge of Fairness (they hear the *why them* directly, with respect). This is the single highest-leverage move; an all-hands that demotes someone they haven't spoken to is the worst version of this announcement.
2. **Lead the all-hands with rationale and an explicit acknowledgment of the last reorg.** *Defuses:* Fairness, the domain that gates how everything else is received. Saying "last time we sprung this on you; this time here is the reasoning, and here is your voice on what's still open" converts "done to us again" into a fair-process read, which buys slack on Status, Certainty, and Autonomy alike.
3. **State what's decided vs. open, with dates, and promise reporting lines by a named day.** *Defuses:* Certainty for the 18, whose loudest unspoken question is "who's my manager?" Answering "you'll know by [date], and you'll have input first" turns the worst ambiguity into a dated, voiced step.

## Caveats

- SCARF is a popularization of social-neuroscience findings, a heuristic for noticing where people will feel socially threatened, **not a measured or clinically validated instrument.** The High/Medium/Low ratings are analyst judgment, not readings off a meter.
- The same announcement threatens different people in different domains. This read centers the three tech leads (Status) and the 18 engineers (Certainty); a quieter senior engineer, a contractor, or the incoming lead would weight the domains differently, and this report would not hold unchanged for them.
- Defusing threat raises the odds of an *approach* response, engagement instead of defensiveness. It does **not** guarantee agreement. People can feel fairly and respectfully treated and still dislike the decision.
- All names, reactions, and details here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
