# Ansoff Matrix (Product/Market Growth Matrix)

Igor Ansoff's matrix (Harvard Business Review, 1957) maps growth strategies along two axes: products (existing or new) and markets (existing or new). The four cells are four routes to growth, each with a distinct risk profile. Risk rises as a strategy moves away from what the firm already knows.

| | Existing products | New products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing markets** | Market Penetration (lowest risk) | Product Development (medium risk) |
| **New markets** | Market Development (medium risk) | Diversification (highest risk) |

## The four strategies

### Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets)
Grow by selling more of what you have to the customers you already serve. Lowest risk, because both the product and the market are known. Tactics: win share from competitors, increase usage or purchase frequency, pricing and promotion, loyalty and retention, convert non-users in the existing market. Usually the first place to look for growth.

### Market Development (existing products, new markets)
Take existing products to new markets: new geographies, new customer segments, new channels, or new use cases. Medium risk, concentrated in the unfamiliarity of the new market. Requires understanding the new market's needs, regulation, and competition, and adapting positioning more than the product.

### Product Development (new products, existing markets)
Build new products for the customers you already understand. Medium risk, concentrated in execution and innovation. Leverages existing customer relationships and brand. Requires development capability and a real grasp of unmet needs in the current base.

### Diversification (new products, new markets)
New products for new markets. The highest risk, because nothing is known territory. Two forms:
- **Related (concentric) diversification:** the new product or market shares some link with the firm (technology, channel, customer type), giving synergy and lower risk.
- **Unrelated (conglomerate) diversification:** no meaningful link, pursued for financial or risk-spreading reasons. Highest risk of all.
Justified when the existing business is constrained or threatened, or when a genuine capability advantage transfers to the new space.

## How to use it

1. **Generate concrete growth options in each cell** for the firm and its objective, not generic descriptions of the cell.
2. **Assess each option** for risk, the capabilities it requires, the investment, and the likely reward.
3. **Sequence a growth path.** Most firms exhaust lower-risk growth before taking on higher-risk moves: penetrate the current market, develop into adjacent markets or products, and diversify only with a clear reason and capability fit. The matrix is a menu to prioritize, not four equal options.

## Common pitfalls

- **Jumping to diversification** because it sounds ambitious, while penetration in the current market is unexhausted and far cheaper.
- **Underrating the risk of new markets**, which fail for reasons (regulation, local competition, channel access) that are invisible from the home market.
- **Generic options.** "Enter new markets" is not a strategy; "launch the existing SaaS in the DACH region through a reseller channel" is.
- **Ignoring capability fit.** A move is only as good as the firm's ability to execute it; risk is as much about capability gaps as about market unfamiliarity.
