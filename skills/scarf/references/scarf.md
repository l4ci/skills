# SCARF: the five domains, triggers, and levers

SCARF (David Rock, 2008) names five domains of social experience the brain weighs much as it weighs physical safety. In each domain a situation can register as a threat, pushing the person toward an *avoid* response (defensiveness, disengagement, narrowed attention), or as a reward, pulling them toward an *approach* response (engagement, openness, willingness). A threat in one domain can swamp rewards in the others, and a threat fires faster and harder than an equivalent reward.

SCARF is a popularization of social-neuroscience findings, not a measurement instrument. Treat it as a structured heuristic for predicting where people will feel threatened and where to add reward, not as a quantified or clinically validated model. The severities below are analyst judgment, not readings.

## The five domains

### Status: relative importance to others
How a person ranks against others, and how that ranking is moving. Status threat is felt as keenly as a physical threat and is easily triggered without intent.

- **Threat triggers:** criticism, being told what to do, unfavorable comparison to peers, public correction, having expertise overlooked, demotion or loss of title or scope.
- **Reward triggers:** recognition and positive feedback, opportunities to learn and grow, increased responsibility, being asked for input, the chance to outperform one's own past performance (a comparison that raises status without threatening anyone else's).
- **Levers:** give specific positive feedback before corrective; frame growth against a personal best rather than against peers; let people teach or present; protect public face when correcting.

### Certainty: ability to predict the future
The brain craves prediction; ambiguity consumes attention and registers as threat. Even minor unexplained change can spike it.

- **Threat triggers:** ambiguity, broken expectations, unexplained change, withheld information, vague timelines, mixed messages.
- **Reward triggers:** clear expectations, transparency about what is known and unknown, breaking a large change into defined steps, explicit timelines, naming what will *not* change.
- **Levers:** state what is decided and what is still open; sequence change into stages with dates; over-communicate the plan; say "I don't know yet, and here is when you will."

### Autonomy: sense of control and choice
A perception of control over one's environment. Removing choice is threatening even when the imposed option is good.

- **Threat triggers:** micromanagement, mandates, removal of options, decisions made for people without consultation, rigid process.
- **Reward triggers:** real choices (even small ones), delegation of decisions, control over *how* the work gets done, opt-in over opt-out, latitude within clear boundaries.
- **Levers:** offer options rather than instructions; let people choose method even when the goal is fixed; consult before deciding; define the boundary, then leave the inside to them.

### Relatedness: sense of safety and belonging with others
Whether others are friend or foe, in-group or out-group. Strangers default to out-group (threat) until connection is established.

- **Threat triggers:** being treated as an outsider, isolation, lack of connection, remote distance without contact, new people or teams with no shared ground, competition framed as us-versus-them.
- **Reward triggers:** connection and rapport, shared goals, trust-building, inclusion, common ground, safe spaces to speak.
- **Levers:** create deliberate connection before content; surface shared goals; pair newcomers with insiders; make belonging explicit; invest in trust before asking for it.

### Fairness: perception of fair exchange
Whether exchanges and treatment are even-handed. Unfairness triggers a strong threat response even at personal cost to resist it.

- **Threat triggers:** unfair, opaque, or inconsistent treatment; hidden criteria; rules applied unevenly; broken reciprocity; perceived favoritism.
- **Reward triggers:** transparency, clear and consistently applied rules, giving people a voice in decisions that affect them, visible reciprocity, explained rationale.
- **Levers:** publish the criteria and apply them evenly; explain the *why* behind decisions; give a genuine voice before deciding; make trade-offs visible rather than hidden.

## Reading a situation

For each domain, ask two questions of the specific situation:

1. **Where is the threat?** What in this change, message, or interaction will register as avoid in this domain, for which people, and how strong is it?
2. **Where is the reward, or where could it be?** What already creates approach in this domain, and which levers would add more?

Rate each domain's threat and reward as **high / medium / low**, with the evidence. A high threat in one domain outranks medium rewards spread across the rest: people act on the threat.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating SCARF as science.** It is a heuristic for noticing social threat, not a metric. Do not report severities as measurements or imply clinical validity.
- **Fixing the wrong domain.** Polishing certainty (a detailed plan) while the real threat is autonomy (no one was consulted) spends effort where it does not land. Prioritize by which domain carries the biggest threat.
- **Generic remedies.** "Communicate more" is not a lever. Each fix must name the domain and the specific move (offer a choice, publish the criteria, sequence the change).
- **Ignoring asymmetry.** Threats outweigh rewards. Removing a high threat usually beats adding another reward.
- **One-size people.** The same change threatens different people in different domains. Note who, not just what.
