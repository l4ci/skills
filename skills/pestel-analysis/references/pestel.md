# PESTEL macro-environment analysis

PESTEL scans the macro-environment, the broad external forces that shape every player in a market but that no single firm controls. The six factors are Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal. (The model is also called PEST or PESTLE; Environmental and Legal are split out from the older four to give them their own weight.)

PESTEL covers the macro layer. It sits above industry forces (Five Forces) and the firm's own position (internal analysis). Keep it at the macro level and do not drift into competitor or company specifics.

## The six factors

- **Political:** government policy and stability, tax policy, trade and tariffs, subsidies, the direction of regulation, political risk, public spending priorities.
- **Economic:** growth and the economic cycle, inflation, interest and exchange rates, unemployment, disposable income, cost and availability of capital.
- **Social:** demographics, values and lifestyle, attitudes and expectations, education levels, cultural and health trends, workforce attitudes.
- **Technological:** the pace of innovation, R&D direction, automation and AI, digital adoption, disruptive technologies, intellectual property, infrastructure.
- **Environmental:** climate change, sustainability pressure, resource and energy scarcity, environmental regulation, ESG expectations, physical climate risk.
- **Legal:** specific laws and their direction: employment, consumer protection, competition and antitrust, health and safety, data protection, licensing, compliance burden.

The line between Political and Legal is fuzzy: treat Political as the direction of policy and power, and Legal as the concrete laws and rules in force.

## How to use it

1. **Identify the forces in each factor** that actually bear on the subject, not a generic list. For each force capture: what it is, its direction (rising, falling, shifting), its timeframe, whether it is an opportunity or a threat, and how certain it is.
2. **Prioritize.** A flat list of forces is not analysis. Rank them by impact on the subject crossed with certainty:
   - High impact, high certainty: plan and act on these now.
   - High impact, high uncertainty: build scenarios and monitor; these are the watch list.
   - Low impact: note and move on.
3. **Find the linkages.** Factors interact (a technology shift drives new regulation; demographic change reshapes the economy). The consequential story is usually a chain across factors, not a single force.
4. **Draw the implications.** End every force with a "so what" for the subject. PESTEL feeds the Opportunities and Threats of a SWOT and the assumptions behind a strategy.

## Common pitfalls

- **The laundry list.** Dozens of unranked forces with no prioritization and no implications. The "so what" and the ranking are the value.
- **Wrong altitude.** Drifting into competitors, customers, or the company itself. That is industry or internal analysis, not macro.
- **Ignoring uncertainty.** Treating a speculative force and a near-certain one the same way. Separate what to plan for from what to monitor.
- **No subject anchor.** A force only matters relative to a specific subject and geography. State both.
