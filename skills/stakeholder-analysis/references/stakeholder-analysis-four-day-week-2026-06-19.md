# Stakeholder analysis: four-day work week pilot

> _Illustrative example output for the `stakeholder-analysis` skill. This company and its people are fictional; every name, score, stance, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Initiative:** Piloting a four-day work week across the engineering and support teams for one quarter, with no pay cut.
- **Phase:** Proposal. Not yet approved; the decision sits with the executive team, with the board kept informed.
- **What success requires:** A go-decision that survives the quarter, i.e. coverage holds (no degradation in support SLAs or engineering delivery), the CFO's cost fears are answered with data, and the pilot produces a clean read on whether to make it permanent.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Framework:** Mendelow power-interest grid (1991). This is a snapshot of stances and positions as they read today, in the proposal phase; it will move with each decision and announcement and should be re-run at the pilot's start and midpoint.

---

## 1. Verdict

**The field is winnable but not yet won.** The initiative has a high-power sponsor (the CEO) who has already spent public credibility on it, and the group whose work changes most, engineering ICs, is strongly in favour. The decisive obstacle is a single high-power skeptic, the CFO, whose concern is concrete (coverage cost and SLA risk) and therefore answerable with a pilot designed to measure exactly that. The second risk is quieter: the support team, high-interest but low-power, is afraid the four-day week will arrive as compressed longer shifts rather than genuine relief, and if that fear hardens into organized opposition it borrows power from the CFO's coverage argument. The path to a durable yes runs through converting the CFO from blocker to neutral and keeping the support team from drifting from anxious to opposed.

**Boundary watch.** Three stakeholders sit on lines and could move with one decision:
- **Support leads (P3)** rise into *manage closely* the moment coverage scheduling becomes the live question: they own the rota, which makes them a lever, not just an affected party.
- **Customers / key accounts (I2 → I5)** jump from *keep satisfied* to *manage closely* the instant a missed SLA or a "we're only open four days" rumour reaches them. Their interest is dormant, not absent.
- **Head of People (P3)** is functionally a key player on execution even though formal power is mid: HR designs the pilot's measurement and the coverage model. Treat as manage-closely in practice.

## 3. Per-stakeholder detail

Led by the key players (manage-closely quadrant), then the high-interest informed group, then the satisfied quadrant.

### CEO: Power 5, Interest 4: Supporter: Confidence: High
- **Power lever:** Final decision authority over the pilot and the budget envelope. Can greenlight over the CFO's objection, though doing so against the CFO is costly.
- **Interest:** Floated the idea publicly, so the company's external positioning and the CEO's own credibility are now attached to it. High, and partly reputational rather than operational.
- **Stance evidence:** Public statement floating the four-day week; framed it as a talent-retention and culture bet.
- **Wants:** A visible win on culture and retention, and to be seen following through on a public commitment. Underlying interest is reputation and the talent story, not the mechanics of coverage.
- **Fears:** A botched pilot that becomes a public retreat; being seen to have over-promised; an internal CEO-vs-CFO fight that leaks.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Sways the board and sets the tone for the whole company. Listens to the CFO on anything with a cost number attached, and to the VP Engineering on delivery feasibility.
- **Engagement lever:** Give the CEO a pilot design rigorous enough to defend publicly either way, a credible "we tested it properly" story protects the reputation even if the result is no. Do not let the initiative become a personal CEO-vs-CFO contest; frame it as a measured experiment.

### CFO: Power 5, Interest 5: Blocker: Confidence: High
- **Power lever:** Controls the budget and can withhold the coverage spend; carries decisive weight with the CEO and board on any cost question. The de facto veto on this initiative.
- **Interest:** Maximal, this is a direct hit on cost and operational risk, the CFO's core terrain.
- **Stance evidence:** Has voiced skepticism specifically about coverage costs (backfill, overtime, or hiring to cover the fifth day).
- **Wants:** Cost discipline and no SLA degradation that lands as a customer or board problem. Underlying interest is being able to stand behind the numbers, not to kill the idea for its own sake, but not to be caught endorsing an unfunded one.
- **Fears:** A pilot that quietly costs more than stated (overtime creep, contractor backfill), an SLA miss that reaches a key account, and being made to look as if Finance waved through a vague, unmeasured experiment.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Strong sway over the CEO and board on cost; moved by data and by a defined downside, not by enthusiasm.
- **Engagement lever:** Co-design the pilot's cost and coverage metrics *with* the CFO. Pre-agree the measures, the kill-criteria, and a capped coverage budget. The concern is concrete and therefore answerable: turn the CFO from owner-of-the-objection into co-author-of-the-test. This is the single highest-leverage relationship in the whole map.

### VP Engineering: Power 4, Interest 5: Supporter: Confidence: High
- **Power lever:** Controls engineering delivery commitments and the team's schedule; can make or break the "delivery didn't slip" half of the success case.
- **Interest:** High, owns the team most expected to benefit and is accountable for whether output holds.
- **Stance evidence:** Has championed the idea internally as a retention and focus play; cites a tight hiring market.
- **Wants:** Retention and reduced burnout without a delivery hit; a credible answer to give the team. Underlying interest is keeping a strong team intact in a competitive market.
- **Fears:** Roadmap slip getting blamed on the four-day week; the pilot being measured loosely so any delivery noise becomes "see, it doesn't work".
- **Influences / influenced by:** Trusted by engineering ICs; respected by the CEO on delivery questions. Can speak to the CFO peer-to-peer on the engineering-side cost case.
- **Engagement lever:** Make the VP the co-owner of the engineering delivery metrics, paired with the CFO on cost. A supporter who also owns the measurement is the strongest possible asset, keep them equipped, not taken for granted.

### Head of People (HR): Power 3, Interest 5: Supporter: Confidence: Medium
- **Power lever:** Designs the pilot mechanics, the measurement framework, and the coverage/scheduling model. Formal authority is mid, but execution runs through HR, treat as a working key player.
- **Interest:** High, this is squarely a People initiative and a flagship retention case.
- **Stance evidence:** Supportive in principle; has raised that the support team's shift structure is the hard part.
- **Wants:** A retention win and a defensible, well-run pilot. Underlying interest is policy that actually works at scale, not a one-quarter PR exercise that rebounds.
- **Fears:** Being handed an under-resourced pilot to run and owning the fallout; the support team's concerns being ignored until they blow up.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Bridges executives and the floor; trusted by support leads. Influenced by the CEO and the evidence base.
- **Engagement lever:** Hand HR the measurement and coverage-model design jointly with the CFO and VP, this answers the CFO's data demand and the support team's fairness fear in one instrument.

### Engineering ICs: Power 2, Interest 5: Supporter: Confidence: Medium
- **Power lever:** Little formal power individually; collective signal (attrition risk, morale) is a soft lever the CEO and VP weigh.
- **Interest:** Maximal, their week changes directly.
- **Stance evidence:** Inferred strong support from the general appeal of the benefit and the VP's read; not yet directly surveyed, hence medium confidence.
- **Wants:** Real time back, not a compressed five-into-four week. Underlying interest is recovery and focus, not just a day off in name.
- **Fears:** "Four-day week" turning into the same workload in fewer hours; the pilot being cancelled and the goodwill lost.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Their retention signal influences the VP and, through the VP, the CEO. Influenced by the VP and by visible, trusted senior engineers.
- **Engagement lever:** Recruit the most-trusted senior engineers as visible advocates and ground-truth sources; keep ICs informed so support doesn't curdle into "promised and pulled" cynicism.

### Support team leads: Power 3, Interest 5: Neutral: Confidence: Medium
- **Power lever:** Own the support rota. Coverage, the CFO's central fear, is theirs to solve or to declare unsolvable, which makes them a lever, not a bystander.
- **Interest:** Maximal, they have to make the coverage math work on the floor.
- **Stance evidence:** Cautious, not opposed: open to it if coverage can be staffed without burning their people. Genuinely neutral, not assumed.
- **Wants:** A rota that covers the customer week without forcing longer or worse shifts on their team. Underlying interest is protecting their people and hitting SLAs at once.
- **Fears:** Being told to deliver four-day coverage with five-day demand and no extra heads; owning the SLA miss the CFO is worried about.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Trusted by support ICs (they speak for the floor); influenced by HR and by whether the coverage model is real.
- **Engagement lever:** Bring leads into designing the coverage model rather than receiving it. If the leads can author a rota they believe in, they convert the support team's fear and answer the CFO's coverage objection from the inside, the highest-leverage move on the low-power side.

### Support ICs: Power 2, Interest 5: Blocker (conditional): Confidence: Medium
- **Power lever:** Low individually; collective discontent (or visible relief) is a signal HR and the CEO read. Their lived experience is the reputational proof or disproof of the pilot.
- **Interest:** Maximal, and most exposed, since support is where compression bites hardest.
- **Stance evidence:** Explicitly afraid of longer individual shifts, the stated tension. A *conditional* blocker: opposed to the compressed version, not to relief.
- **Wants:** Genuine reduction in working time, not the same coverage burden squeezed into four longer days. Underlying interest is relief and fairness, not the policy label.
- **Fears:** Longer, harder shifts dressed up as a perk; engineering getting a real four-day week while support gets a worse four-day week.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Influenced by their leads and by HR; their morale signal influences the CEO's reputational read.
- **Engagement lever:** Address the fear directly and early, show the coverage model does not mean longer shifts, or explicitly scope a support-specific design. Silence here converts a conditional blocker into an organized one.

### Customers / key accounts: Power 4, Interest 2: Neutral / Unknown: Confidence: Low
- **Power lever:** A key account that escalated a coverage complaint to the CEO or board would hand the CFO a decisive argument. High latent power, dormant.
- **Interest:** Low today, most don't know and don't care how the work gets staffed, only that SLAs hold.
- **Stance evidence:** None, genuinely unknown. Marked low confidence rather than assumed neutral.
- **Wants:** Uninterrupted service at the agreed SLA.
- **Fears (theirs):** Slower responses, reduced availability, the "you're only open four days" misread.
- **Influences / influenced by:** A vocal account sways the board and CFO directly.
- **Engagement lever:** Keep satisfied and invisible: ensure SLAs are explicitly protected in the pilot and say nothing that raises their interest. The goal is for customers never to notice a change.

### Board: Power 4, Interest 2: Neutral: Confidence: Medium
- **Power lever:** Governance oversight; can pressure the CEO if it senses cost or reputational risk. Not running the decision but able to disturb it.
- **Interest:** Low while it stays a contained operational experiment; spikes if it reads as cost risk or a public misstep.
- **Stance evidence:** No position taken, aware via the CEO's public framing.
- **Wants:** No surprises; confidence that the experiment is measured and capped.
- **Fears:** An uncapped cost experiment, or a public retreat that reflects on governance.
- **Influences / influenced by:** Influenced by the CEO and especially the CFO on cost.
- **Engagement lever:** Keep satisfied with a one-page framing, capped budget, clear metrics, defined kill-criteria, routed through the CEO. Do not over-brief; over-attention here invites intervention.

## 4. Engagement plan (by quadrant)

### Manage closely: CEO, CFO, VP Engineering
The decisive quadrant; most effort goes here.
- **CFO (the swing):** Co-design the cost and coverage metrics, the capped coverage budget, and explicit kill-criteria. Move from "approve my idea" to "help me design the test that answers your fear." Owning the measurement converts the objection into a stake in the result.
- **CEO:** Keep the sponsor equipped with a defensible-either-way pilot. Protect the relationship with the CFO, never let it become a public CEO-vs-CFO fight, which would damage the initiative whichever side wins.
- **VP Engineering:** Co-owns the delivery metrics. Pair with the CFO so the engineering cost-and-output case is made peer-to-peer, not top-down.

### Keep satisfied: Customers / key accounts, Board
Comfortable, low-touch, no interest-spike.
- **Customers:** Protect SLAs explicitly in the pilot design and stay silent externally. **Interest-spike trigger to watch:** any missed SLA, or a public "four-day company" rumour, either jumps them to manage-closely overnight.
- **Board:** A single capped-budget, clear-metrics, kill-criteria briefing via the CEO. **Interest-spike trigger:** an uncapped cost signal or a press question. Re-brief only at the pilot's start and end.

### Keep informed: Head of People, Engineering ICs, Support leads, Support ICs
Communicate, answer questions, recruit advocates, harvest ground truth.
- **Head of People:** In practice manage-closely, give HR the measurement-and-coverage design jointly with CFO and VP.
- **Engineering ICs:** Recruit trusted senior engineers as **advocates** and as ground-truth on whether the week is genuinely lighter. Keep informed to prevent "promised then pulled" cynicism.
- **Support leads:** Recruit as **co-authors of the coverage model**, not recipients. The leads are the bridge to the floor and the answer to the CFO's coverage fear.
- **Support ICs:** Communicate directly and early about shift length. The advocates to recruit here are the leads; the failure mode is silence hardening the conditional blocker into an organized one.

### Monitor
Nothing material this quarter. Re-check at the midpoint in case a low-interest party (e.g. another function eyeing the same benefit) moves up.

## 5. Coalition and blockers

### The supporter coalition
- **CEO (P5)**: the sponsor; carries the board and sets company tone.
- **VP Engineering (P4)**: carries engineering ICs and can argue the cost case to the CFO peer-to-peer.
- **Head of People (P3)**: carries the support leads and owns the instrument that answers both the CFO and the support floor.
- **Engineering ICs (P2)**: the morale-and-retention evidence the CEO and VP cite.

**Who carries weight where you can't reach directly:** The CFO is not moved by enthusiasm, so the VP (cost case) and HR (measurement rigor) are the coalition members who can actually reach that objection. The support floor is reached through the leads and HR, not through executive messaging.

### The blockers
- **CFO (P5), high-power, the priority.** Real concern: coverage cost and SLA exposure landing as a board or customer problem. **Influence path:** direct co-design of metrics and a capped budget; reinforced by the VP on the engineering cost case and HR on measurement rigor. Aim is neutral ("the test is sound, I'll judge the data"), not conversion.
- **Support ICs (P2), low-power, conditional.** Real concern: compressed longer shifts. **Influence path:** through their leads and HR, by making the coverage model demonstrably not mean longer shifts. Low individual power, but ignored it becomes organized opposition that lends weight to the CFO's coverage argument, the two blocker concerns are the same concern (coverage) seen from two ends.

The two blockers share a root: **coverage**. Solve coverage credibly and both objections lose their fuel at once.

## 6. Highest-leverage moves

Sequenced by how much each shifts, not by headcount.

1. **Convert the CFO from blocker to neutral by co-authoring the pilot's cost-and-coverage test.** (Blocker → neutral.) The single decisive relationship: a P5 veto whose concern is concrete and therefore answerable. Pre-agree metrics, a capped coverage budget, and kill-criteria *with* Finance. Do this first; the rest of the plan assumes the CFO is judging the data, not fighting the idea.
2. **Make the support leads co-authors of the coverage model.** (Neutral → support, and pre-empts the IC blocker.) The leads own the rota, the very thing the CFO fears and the support ICs fear, from opposite ends. A coverage model the leads believe in answers both at once and is the highest-leverage low-power move.
3. **Address the support ICs' shift-length fear explicitly and early.** (Conditional blocker → neutral.) Silence is what turns this from a manageable worry into organized opposition that borrows the CFO's power. Cheap to do now, expensive to undo later.
4. **Protect SLAs in the design and keep customers' interest dormant.** (Hold the keep-satisfied line.) A single escalated account would hand the CFO a decisive argument; the move is to ensure there's nothing to escalate and to say nothing that wakes the dormant interest.
5. **Keep the CEO equipped and out of a personal contest with the CFO.** (Hold and deepen the sponsor.) A defensible-either-way pilot protects the CEO's public credibility regardless of result; framing it as a measured experiment, not a mandate, keeps the strongest supporter from becoming a liability.

---

## 7. Caveats

- **This is a snapshot, not a fixture.** It reads the field in the proposal phase. Stances will move with each decision: re-run at the pilot's go-decision and again at its midpoint. The CFO's stance in particular should flip to neutral or supporter if move 1 lands, and that re-rates the whole map.
- **Scores are informed judgment, not measurement.** Power and interest are placed from stated positions and role, not metricated.
- **The thin-evidence reads are flagged and should be checked, not trusted.** Engineering IC support is inferred, not surveyed (medium confidence); the support leads' neutral and the support ICs' conditional-blocker stances rest on the single stated tension; customers' stance is genuinely unknown (low confidence). Close these gaps, especially a quick support-team read, before committing, because the support floor is where the pilot's reputation is actually decided.
- **Watch the boundary stakeholders.** Customers (dormant interest) and support leads (rota power) are one event away from jumping quadrants; the plan's stability depends on coverage holding.
