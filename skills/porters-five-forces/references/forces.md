# Porter's Five Forces: determinants and scoring

Michael Porter's framework (Harvard Business Review, 1979; *Competitive Strategy*, 1980) explains the profit potential of an industry through five competitive forces. The stronger a force, the more it pulls profitability down. Where all five are strong, average returns are competed away. Where they are weak, the industry can sustain high returns.

The unit of analysis is an **industry** (a market for a defined product or service in a defined geography), not a single company. A firm's own strengths are separate from industry structure, though the analysis informs how that firm should position itself.

## Scoring rubric

Rate each force on intensity from 1 to 5:

| Score | Intensity | Effect on industry profitability |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Very weak | Strongly favorable to incumbents |
| 2 | Weak | Favorable |
| 3 | Moderate | Neutral |
| 4 | Strong | Erodes profit |
| 5 | Very strong | Severely erodes profit |

Assess each determinant below as pushing the force up or down, then settle on one intensity score for the force with a short justification. Cite real evidence (concentration figures, switching costs, growth rates, named competitors or substitutes) where retrieval tools are available. If no data can be found, say so and lower confidence rather than inventing figures.

## 1. Threat of New Entrants

How easily can new competitors enter and compete away profits? High barriers mean a weak threat (good for incumbents).

Determinants:
- Economies of scale (cost disadvantage for sub-scale entrants)
- Capital requirements to enter
- Customer switching costs
- Access to distribution channels
- Incumbency advantages independent of scale: brand, proprietary technology or IP, network effects, learning-curve cost advantages, favorable locations, government subsidies
- Expected retaliation from incumbents
- Government policy, licensing, and regulation
- Restricted access to inputs or essential resources

A force is **strong (4 to 5)** when barriers are low and entry is easy.

## 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Can suppliers capture value by raising prices or cutting quality? Powerful suppliers squeeze industry margins.

Determinants:
- Supplier concentration relative to the industry it sells to
- Dependence of suppliers on the industry for revenue (industry volume matters little to a powerful supplier)
- Switching costs to change suppliers
- Differentiation or uniqueness of the input
- Availability of substitute inputs
- Credible threat of forward integration by suppliers into the industry
- Input cost as a share of total industry costs

A force is **strong (4 to 5)** when suppliers are few, differentiated, or hard to switch away from.

## 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers

Can customers force prices down or demand more? Powerful buyers capture value from the industry.

Determinants:
- Buyer concentration and purchase volume per buyer
- Buyer switching costs
- Product standardization versus differentiation (commodities give buyers power)
- Buyer price sensitivity (the product's share of the buyer's cost, the buyer's own profitability)
- Credible threat of backward integration by buyers
- Buyer information and price transparency
- Importance of the product to the buyer's own quality or performance

A force is **strong (4 to 5)** when buyers are concentrated, price-sensitive, and face low switching costs.

## 4. Threat of Substitutes

Can a different product or service meet the same need from outside the industry? Substitutes cap the price the industry can charge.

Determinants:
- Relative price-performance of substitutes
- Buyer propensity to substitute
- Switching costs to move to the substitute
- Whether the substitute is improving on a faster trajectory than the industry's own products

Distinguish substitutes (a different way to meet the same need, for example video calls versus air travel) from rivals (the same product from a competitor). A force is **strong (4 to 5)** when good substitutes exist at attractive price-performance.

## 5. Competitive Rivalry

How hard do existing competitors fight each other? Intense rivalry, especially on price, transfers value to customers.

Determinants:
- Number of competitors and their relative balance (many, equally matched competitors raise rivalry)
- Industry growth rate (slow growth turns competition into a zero-sum fight for share)
- High fixed costs or perishable inventory that pressures firms to fill capacity
- Lack of differentiation or low switching costs
- Capacity added in large increments, causing overcapacity
- Diversity of competitors (different goals and origins make behavior less predictable)
- High strategic stakes
- High exit barriers that keep weak competitors in the game

Rivalry is most destructive when it competes on price. A force is **strong (4 to 5)** when competitors are numerous, undifferentiated, growth is slow, and exit barriers are high.

## Beyond the five forces

Note in any analysis:
- **Complementors (the "sixth force").** Products that increase demand for the industry (for example apps for a phone platform). Porter's original model treats these only implicitly; flag them when they materially shape the industry.
- **Dynamics.** The forces are a snapshot. Industries shift as barriers rise or fall, substitutes improve, or consolidation changes concentration. Note the direction each force is trending, not just its current level.
- **Government.** Often acts through the five forces (regulation as a barrier, as a buyer, and so on) rather than as a separate force, but call it out explicitly when its role is large.
