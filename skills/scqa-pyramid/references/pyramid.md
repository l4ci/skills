# The Pyramid Principle: SCQA, structure rules, grouping, and pitfalls

The Pyramid Principle is Barbara Minto's method for structuring writing and argument so the reader follows it with the least effort (*The Pyramid Principle*, 1973; revised 2009). It became the standard communication method at McKinsey and beyond. Its central claim: a reader can absorb ideas most easily when they arrive top-down, the governing thought first, then its support, rather than bottom-up, the support first and the conclusion at the end.

The method has two linked tools. **SCQA** frames the reader's context and delivers the answer up front. **The pyramid** is the hierarchy of ideas that defends that answer, where every idea is supported by the group of ideas beneath it.

---

## SCQA: the introduction

The introduction earns the reader's attention by telling a small story that ends on the Question they already have in mind, then answering it. Four parts:

- **Situation.** The uncontroversial background, stated so the reader nods. It is something they already know and accept, the stable starting point. No new claims here.
- **Complication.** What changed, went wrong, or came into tension with the Situation. This is the engine of the introduction; it creates the need for an answer.
- **Question.** The question the Complication naturally raises in the reader's mind. It is not asked aloud so much as implied; if the Situation and Complication are right, the reader is already asking it.
- **Answer.** The governing thought that answers the Question. This is the top of the pyramid. Everything in the rest of the document exists to support this one sentence.

The Answer goes near the front, not the end. The Situation and Complication are short; their job is to make the Answer land on the reader's actual question, not to rehearse the analysis.

### Variant orderings

The four elements can be reordered for tone without changing the logic, as long as the Answer still leads the body:

- **Standard (S-C-Q-A):** the neutral default. Background, tension, question, answer.
- **Direct (A-S-C-Q):** answer first of all, for a reader who wants the bottom line immediately and the context after.
- **Concerned (C-S-Q-A):** open on the Complication to convey urgency.
- **Confident (Q-S-C-A):** open by posing the Question outright.

Pick the ordering by how much the reader already feels the tension. A reader who knows the problem wants the direct form; a reader who does not yet see a problem needs the standard form to build it.

## The pyramid: the rules of structure

Below the Answer, ideas form a hierarchy:

- **Governing thought** at the apex (the Answer from the SCQA).
- **Key line:** the row of supporting arguments directly beneath it. Each is a complete thought, and together they prove the governing thought. The key line should be MECE as a set, two to five points.
- **Evidence:** the data, facts, or sub-arguments supporting each key-line point. Deeper pyramids repeat the pattern at each level.

Two rules govern whether the structure holds.

### Rule 1: ideas summarize the ideas below them

An idea at any level must *summarize* the ideas grouped beneath it, not merely *label* them. The parent states the combined message of the children. The reader should be able to read only the top of the pyramid and get the whole argument.

- **Label (wrong):** "Three findings about the product." This names a topic; it carries no message and could sit above any three findings at all.
- **Summary (right):** "The product is ready to ship but priced too low." This states what the findings add up to. A reader who stops here still has the point.

The test: cover the children and read only the parent. If the parent still tells you what the children collectively say, it summarizes. If it only tells you the subject they are about, it labels.

### Rule 2: ideas in a grouping are the same kind and logically ordered

Every group of ideas under one parent must be:

- **The same kind.** All reasons, or all steps, or all components, or all parts of one deductive chain. A group that mixes a reason with a step with an example is not a group; it is a pile.
- **Logically ordered.** The sequence follows a real principle, not the order they occurred to the writer (see ordering principles below).

### The vertical question and answer dialogue

The pyramid reads vertically as a dialogue. Each idea raises a question in the reader's mind, and the level directly below it answers that question. The governing thought "we should exit this market" raises "why?", and the key line answers it. A key-line point "the margins are unrecoverable" raises "why?" or "how do you know?", and the evidence answers it. If a level answers a question the level above did not raise, the structure has drifted off its line.

Horizontal logic (the grouping holds together) and vertical logic (each level answers the level above) must both be true.

## Deductive versus inductive grouping

A grouping is built one of two ways. A single grouping is one or the other, never both.

### Deductive

A chain of reasoning: a statement, a comment on that statement, and the conclusion that follows.

> Premise: This market's margins have fallen below our cost of capital.
> Premise: No structural change ahead will restore them.
> Therefore: We should exit this market.

Deductive groups are persuasive but rigid: the reader must hold the whole chain to reach the conclusion, and one weak premise breaks it. Use deduction sparingly and at the top, where a single clear line of reasoning carries the argument. Minto's advice is to prefer induction below the top level, because long deductive chains are tiring to read.

### Inductive

A set of ideas of the same kind, named by their shared property.

> We should exit this market because:
> - the margins are unrecoverable, **and**
> - the brand fits poorly here, **and**
> - the capital is worth more elsewhere.

The parent names the commonality ("reasons to exit") and summarizes the set ("we should exit"). Inductive groups are easier to read and easier to make MECE. The key line of most documents is inductive.

The tell: deductive points connect with "therefore"; inductive points connect with "and". If you cannot decide which word joins your points, the grouping is mixed and must be recut.

---

## Ordering within a grouping

An inductive group's points must follow a principle, chosen to match what the points are:

- **Time order.** First, then, finally. For groups of steps, stages, or a process. Follows cause to effect.
- **Structural order.** By the parts of a structure: by region, by business unit, by component, following how the thing is actually divided. Mirrors a MECE cut.
- **Degree order (ranking).** Most important first, then descending. For groups of reasons or recommendations, where the reader should meet the strongest point first.

Picking the order is also a check: if the points do not yield to any of the three principles, they are probably not the same kind, and the grouping is wrong.

## Pitfalls

- **Burying the answer (the detective story).** Building up evidence and revealing the conclusion at the end. The reader spends the whole document not knowing where it is going and has to reread to assemble the point. State the answer first.
- **Key-line points that are labels, not arguments.** "Market analysis", "financial considerations", "next steps" name topics and carry no message. Each key-line point must be a complete statement that says something.
- **Mixing deductive and inductive in one grouping.** Joining some points with "therefore" and others with "and" under the same parent. The reader cannot tell whether they are following a chain or counting a set. Keep each grouping one kind.
- **A non-MECE key line.** Supporting points that overlap (the same idea twice in different words) or leave a gap (an obvious objection the key line never addresses). The key line must pass the same ME and CE test as a decomposition tree.
- **A parent that labels instead of summarizes.** "Four things to know" above four points. Cover the points and read only the parent: if it carries no message, rewrite it to state what the points add up to.
- **A Question nobody asked.** An SCQA whose Question is not the question this reader has. The Answer then lands on a question the reader is not asking, and the whole pyramid defends a point they did not need made. Frame to the reader's real Complication.
- **Drift off the vertical line.** A level that answers a different question than the level above raised. Each level must answer the "why?" or "how?" of its parent.

---

## What good looks like

A finished pyramid:

- Opens with an SCQA that ends on the reader's real Question and answers it immediately.
- Has a governing thought that the reader could act on after reading nothing else.
- Has a key line that is MECE, where each point is a complete summarizing statement, not a label.
- Uses one kind of grouping per group, deductive or inductive, stated, with points in a real order.
- Reads cleanly top to bottom as a question-and-answer dialogue, each level answering the one above.
- Can be flattened into prose, an exec summary, or a deck storyline without losing its structure.
