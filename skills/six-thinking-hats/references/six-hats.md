# The Six Thinking Hats: the six modes and their discipline

Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats turns a single topic over in six distinct modes of thinking, one mode at a time. Each hat is a direction of thought, not a type of person. The discipline is that everyone looks in the same direction at once.

## The core insight: parallel thinking

Ordinary argument is adversarial: one person defends, another attacks, and the discussion zig-zags as each defends their position. Parallel thinking points everyone the same way at the same time. When the group wears the Black hat, everyone hunts for risks together; when it switches to Yellow, everyone hunts for value together. No one is stuck defending a corner.

Here the "group" is a set of subagents. All five substantive hats examine the same focus question simultaneously, each confined to its own mode, and a Blue-hat pass integrates them. This separates ego from performance: a person (or agent) can argue hard for a benefit under Yellow and hard against it under Black without contradiction, because the hat, not the thinker, owns the view.

## The six hats

| Hat | Mode | What it does | Discipline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **White** | Information | Facts, figures, data available, data needed, and the gaps between them. | Neutral and objective. Report what is known and what is missing. No opinions, no interpretation dressed as fact. |
| **Red** | Feelings | Emotions, hunches, intuition, gut reactions, likes and dislikes. | Permitted **without** justification. Stating the feeling is the whole job; do not rationalize it. |
| **Black** | Caution | Risks, weaknesses, why it may fail, critical judgment, legal, ethical, and logical flaws. | Must be logical and specific, not free-floating negativity. The most useful hat and the most overused. |
| **Yellow** | Benefits | Value, feasibility, why it will work, best case, the upside. | Optimism grounded in logic. Name the mechanism by which the benefit arrives, not just the hope. |
| **Green** | Creativity | New ideas, alternatives, provocations, ways around the problems the Black hat raised. | Generate, do not judge. Quantity and movement over polish. |
| **Blue** | Process | Thinking about the thinking: sets the focus question and agenda, sequences the hats, summarizes, and concludes. | Manages the session. Stays above the content; does not argue the topic. |

## The Red hat earns its place

The Red hat looks indulgent, but it is structural. Feelings exist whether or not they are invited; if they are not invited, they leak in and contaminate the supposedly rational hats, dressed up as facts or as risks. Giving emotion a legitimate slot, with no demand for justification, drains it out of the others. A strong negative gut read under Red is data the synthesis must weigh, even when no Black-hat argument supports it.

## Failure modes to guard against

- **Black-hat dominance.** Caution is the easiest mode and the one people default to, so it crowds out the rest and kills ideas before they form. Always force Yellow and Green around the Black: every risk gets a fair search for value and for a way past it.
- **Mixing hats.** The discipline collapses the moment a hat strays. A "fact" with a judgment baked in (White doing Black's job), an "idea" smuggling in its own critique (Green doing Black's job), a "risk" that is really a dislike (Black doing Red's job): each stays strictly in its lane. One mode at a time is the entire method.
- **Rubber-stamping.** Running the hats to ratify a decision already made, steering each hat toward the predetermined answer. The session has to be able to change the conclusion, or it is theater. The Blue hat opens by stating that the outcome is genuinely open.

## Sequence

The Blue hat sets the order. A common general-purpose sequence:

**Blue (open) → White → Green → Yellow → Black → Red → Blue (close)**

White first to lay down the facts; Green early to generate before judgment hardens; Yellow before Black so value gets a fair hearing first; Black to stress-test; Red near the end to surface the gut read once the thinking is done; Blue to conclude. Sequences vary by purpose: a session to fix a known problem may lead with Black, an idea-generation session may dwell in Green, an emotionally charged decision may open with Red to clear the air. The dispatch can run the five substantive hats in parallel; the sequence still governs how the Blue hat reasons over the results and how the report orders them.
