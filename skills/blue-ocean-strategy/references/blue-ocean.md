# Blue Ocean Strategy: value innovation, the canvas, and the four actions

W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne's framework (*Blue Ocean Strategy*, Harvard Business School Press, 2005) divides markets into two kinds. **Red oceans** are the industries that exist today: the boundaries are defined, the rules of competition are known, and rivals fight over a shrinking pool of demand until margins bleed out. **Blue oceans** are the market space that does not yet exist: uncontested, with demand created rather than fought over, and growth that does not depend on beating anyone.

The engine of a blue ocean is **value innovation**. Conventional strategy treats value and cost as a trade-off: you either differentiate at higher cost or you cut cost by offering less. Value innovation breaks that trade-off by pursuing differentiation **and** low cost at the same time. You lift buyer value (often by creating attributes the industry never offered) while you strip out the costly attributes the industry competes on but buyers no longer care about. The cost saving funds the value lift, and the value lift opens demand the cost structure can profitably serve. Value innovation is the simultaneity. A move that only differentiates, or only cuts cost, is not it.

The unit of analysis is a **strategic move**: the set of actions and decisions that creates a new market. Not a company, not an industry. A company can run red-ocean and blue-ocean moves at once.

## A. The strategy canvas

The strategy canvas is both a diagnostic and a building tool. It captures the current state of play in the known market space.

- The **horizontal axis** lists the factors the industry competes on and invests in. These are buyer-valued attributes, not internal features: "ease of finding a wine you like" rather than "SKU count". Get this wrong and the rest of the analysis describes the firm's org chart instead of the market.
- The **vertical axis** is the offering level buyers receive across each factor, low to high.
- A **value curve** is the line plotting one player's offering level across all factors. Drawing the curves of the main players and any substitutes shows where everyone competes alike. When the leading curves converge, the industry is a red ocean: everyone offers more of the same things, and competition collapses to price.

The canvas exposes the question a blue-ocean move must answer: which factors does the industry take as given that no longer earn their cost, and which factors has nobody thought to offer?

## B. The Four Actions Framework (the ERRC grid)

To build a new value curve you act on four levers. Two of them pull cost down; two of them push buyer value up. Run together they produce value innovation.

| Action | Question | Effect on cost | Effect on buyer value |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors that the industry has long competed on should be eliminated? | Cuts cost | Removes attributes buyers no longer value; can open a new buyer group that the old attribute repelled |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard? | Cuts cost | Stops over-serving buyers on dimensions the industry has overshot |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard? | Adds cost | Removes compromises the industry forces buyers to accept |
| **Create** | Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered? | Adds cost | Creates new demand and shifts the basis of pricing |

Eliminate and Reduce are the discipline that keeps a blue-ocean move profitable: without them the move only adds value and ends up a high-cost niche, not value innovation. Raise and Create are what lift the offering out of the existing competitive space. The grid forces all four at once, which is why it is hard to fake a one-sided move past it: a grid with an empty Eliminate and Reduce column is a differentiation play wearing the framework's clothes.

Filling the grid:
- **Eliminate** and **Create** are the searching questions. They push past the boundaries of accepted competition; the industry rarely asks them. Most of a move's divergence comes from here.
- Tie each entry to a buyer-valued factor on the canvas, and state the cost and value effect, not just the action.
- An ERRC grid that names only Reduce and Raise is usually a tune-up of the existing curve, not a new one.

## C. The six paths to reconstruct market boundaries

Blue oceans are found by looking systematically across the boundaries that competitors take as fixed. Six paths:

1. **Across alternative industries.** Buyers weigh alternatives that serve the same goal by different means (a cinema versus a restaurant for an evening out), not just rivals. The space between alternatives is often blue.
2. **Across strategic groups within the industry.** Groups defined by price and performance (luxury cars, economy cars) compete inside their tier and ignore the others. Ask why buyers trade up or down between groups, and combine the decisive factors of more than one.
3. **Across the chain of buyers.** Purchaser, user, and influencer are often different people who value different things. Industries converge on one of them; shifting focus to a neglected one redefines value.
4. **Across complementary products and services.** Look at what happens before, during, and after your product is used. The pain often sits in the complement (parking and babysitting around the cinema visit), and solving it expands demand.
5. **Across functional and emotional appeal.** Industries train buyers to expect either a functional offering or an emotional one. Stripping the emotional premium off an emotionally-priced industry, or adding feeling to a purely functional one, opens new space.
6. **Across time.** Look at decisive external trends on a clear, irreversible trajectory and ask what the market will look like once the trend plays out, then deliver that value now.

## D. The three tests of a good blue-ocean strategy

A new value curve should pass three tests. They are read off the canvas.

- **Focus.** The curve is not spread thin across every factor. It commits to a few, which is what keeps the cost structure viable. A curve that is high on everything has no focus and no cost advantage.
- **Divergence.** The curve looks visibly different from the rivals' curves. If it tracks the industry profile, the move is reactive and offers buyers no reason to switch. Divergence comes mostly from Eliminate and Create.
- **A compelling tagline.** A focused, divergent strategy can be stated in one true, sharp line that speaks to buyers. If you cannot write the tagline, the strategy is probably not focused, and a vague tagline signals a muddled curve. The tagline is a test, not decoration.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating it as pure differentiation.** Lifting Raise and Create while leaving Eliminate and Reduce empty produces a richer, costlier offering, not value innovation. The cost side is half the framework.
- **Treating it as pure cost-cutting.** Stripping factors without creating new value is a low-end discount move that competitors match on price. It stays in the red ocean.
- **A me-too value curve.** If the new curve has no divergence from rivals, there is no blue ocean: buyers have no reason to leave the incumbent. Convergent curves are the signature of a red ocean, including the one you just drew.
- **Factors defined by internal features, not buyer-valued attributes.** A canvas built from the firm's specs and processes maps the company, not the market, and every later step inherits the error.
- **Ignoring the cost side.** A move that lifts value but does not lower cost can still be unprofitable: if the new demand will not pay for the added attributes, the blue ocean does not hold. Check that Eliminate and Reduce actually fund Raise and Create.
- **No tagline.** A strategy with no sharp tagline is usually not focused. Diffuse curves resist one true line, and that is the tell.
