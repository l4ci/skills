# McKinsey 7-S: elements and alignment

The 7-S framework (Peters, Waterman, Pascale, and Athos at McKinsey, around 1980) holds that organizational effectiveness comes from the alignment of seven interdependent elements. There is no hierarchy among them; change one and the others must be realigned or the change fails. Shared Values sit at the center and connect the rest.

The seven split into "hard" elements (tangible, easier for management to direct) and "soft" elements (intangible, culture-driven, harder to change but often decisive).

## The hard elements

- **Strategy:** the plan to build and sustain competitive advantage. How the organization intends to win, given its market and competition.
- **Structure:** how the organization is arranged. Reporting lines, hierarchy, divisions, centralization versus decentralization, who answers to whom.
- **Systems:** the daily procedures and processes that get work done. Workflows, IT systems, planning and control, incentives and reporting mechanisms.

## The soft elements

- **Shared Values (center):** the core beliefs and culture, the things the organization actually stands for. Originally called "superordinate goals." Everything else orbits this.
- **Skills:** the capabilities and competencies of the organization as a whole, not of individuals. What the firm is genuinely good at.
- **Style:** the leadership and management style. How leaders actually behave, spend time, and make decisions, and the culture that behavior creates.
- **Staff:** the people. Numbers, types, recruitment, development, and how talent is managed.

## How to use it

1. **Assess each element's current state**, grounded in evidence rather than aspiration. The soft elements are where assessments most often drift into wishful thinking; describe what is, not what the brochure says.
2. **For a change context, also assess the desired state** of each element and the gap to it.
3. **Check alignment across the elements.** The framework's payoff is in the fit between elements, not the elements alone. Look especially at:
   - Each element against Shared Values (does the culture support or fight it).
   - Strategy against Structure and Systems (does the organization's wiring actually deliver the strategy).
   - Style and Staff against the Skills the strategy requires.
   Flag every place two elements pull in opposite directions; these misalignments are why organizations underperform or why change stalls.

## When it is used

Diagnosing why an organization underperforms, planning and sequencing a major change, integrating after a merger or acquisition, or realigning the organization after a strategy shift.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating elements in isolation.** The insight is the interdependence. An element-by-element checklist with no alignment analysis is not a 7-S analysis.
- **Neglecting the soft elements.** They are harder to assess and harder to change, and they usually decide whether change succeeds.
- **Reducing Structure to the org chart.** Structure includes how coordination and decisions actually flow, not just the boxes.
- **Vague Shared Values.** "Integrity and excellence" is a poster, not a finding. Name what the organization truly rewards and tolerates.
