# Cynefin: the five domains, their decision models, and diagnostic questions

Cynefin (Welsh, roughly "habitat") sorts a situation by the kind of cause-and-effect that governs it, because the kind of cause-and-effect dictates how you should act. It is not a categorization model that drops things into static boxes; it is a sense-making model. The same situation can hold issues in several domains at once, and issues move between domains over time.

The framework has five domains. Four sit in the corners; the fifth, Disorder, sits in the center. Two of the corners (Clear and Complicated) are *ordered*: cause-and-effect is knowable, and the right response can be worked out in advance. Two (Complex and Chaotic) are *unordered*: cause-and-effect is not knowable in advance, so the response has to be discovered by acting.

Each domain has its own decision model, a fixed sequence of moves, and its own kind of practice.

| Domain | Knowledge | Cause and effect | Decision model | Practice |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Clear** | Known knowns | Obvious to all | Sense → Categorize → Respond | Best practice |
| **Complicated** | Known unknowns | Knowable through analysis or expertise | Sense → Analyze → Respond | Good practice |
| **Complex** | Unknown unknowns | Coherent only in retrospect | Probe → Sense → Respond | Emergent practice |
| **Chaotic** | Unknowable | No discernible relationship | Act → Sense → Respond | Novel practice |
| **Disorder** | Unknown which | You don't know which domain you are in | Break into parts and classify each | — |

---

## Clear (known knowns)

Cause-and-effect is obvious to everyone. There is a single right answer, and it is the same right answer every time. The work is to recognize which standard situation you are in and apply the established procedure.

- **Decision model:** SENSE the facts, CATEGORIZE them against known categories, RESPOND with the established procedure.
- **Practice:** BEST practice. One right answer exists, so codify it: checklists, standard operating procedures, fixed processes.
- **Risk:** complacency. Because the answer is settled, people stop paying attention. Conditions shift underneath a process that no longer fits, and confidence in the recipe is precisely what blinds them to the change. This is the edge of the cliff (see below).
- **Diagnostic questions.** Is the cause-and-effect obvious to any reasonable observer? Is there a single, agreed, repeatable right answer? Does a known procedure already exist and reliably work? Would experts agree instantly and without debate? If yes to all, it is Clear. If experts would need to think or disagree, it is Complicated, not Clear.

## Complicated (known unknowns)

Cause-and-effect exists and is knowable, but only through analysis or expertise. There may be more than one good answer. This is the home of experts and of most analytic frameworks: the answer can be worked out in advance by someone who knows how.

- **Decision model:** SENSE the facts, ANALYZE them (or bring in expertise), RESPOND with a defensible answer.
- **Practice:** GOOD practice, not best practice. Because there is more than one right answer, you choose among defensible options rather than following a single recipe.
- **Risk:** the experts' answer crowds out novel options, and analysis paralysis where the situation is actually Complex and no amount of analysis will yield the answer.
- **Diagnostic questions.** Does solving this require expertise, investigation, or analysis rather than just recognition? Could a competent expert, given time, work out a reliable answer in advance? Are there several defensible right answers rather than exactly one? Is the relationship between cause and effect stable enough that what you learn will still hold tomorrow? If yes, it is Complicated. If the answer only becomes clear after you act, it is Complex, not Complicated.

## Complex (unknown unknowns)

Cause-and-effect can only be understood in retrospect. The system is dynamic: acting on it changes it, so the answer cannot be known in advance, only discovered. Analysis cannot deliver it because the relationships are not stable enough to analyze. The right move is to run small, safe-to-fail experiments, sense which ones produce a desirable pattern, and amplify those while damping the rest, letting good practice *emerge*.

- **Decision model:** PROBE with safe-to-fail experiments, SENSE the results, RESPOND by amplifying what works and damping what does not.
- **Practice:** EMERGENT practice. The right approach reveals itself through action; it cannot be specified up front.
- **Risk:** treating it as Complicated and over-analyzing, which wastes time and still fails because the answer is not knowable in advance; or treating it as Clear and imposing a recipe, which fails outright. Also: experiments that are not actually safe-to-fail, turning a probe into a catastrophe.
- **Diagnostic questions.** Will the pattern only be clear after the fact? Does acting on the system change the system? Have repeated attempts to analyze your way to the answer failed? Are there many interacting variables with no stable, repeatable relationship? Is the right move to try several small things and see what emerges rather than to study harder? If yes, it is Complex. Most failed plans are Complex problems that were managed as Complicated ones.

## Chaotic (no discernible cause-and-effect)

There is no discernible relationship between cause and effect at the system level: turbulence, crisis, things actively coming apart. There is no time to probe and no pattern to find. The only move is to act, immediately, to establish some order, then sense whether the situation has stabilized, then respond. The first goal is to get out of chaos into Complex or Complicated, where you can think again.

- **Decision model:** ACT to stabilize, SENSE whether order is returning, RESPOND to move out of chaos.
- **Practice:** NOVEL practice. There is no precedent to follow; you do whatever stops the bleeding.
- **Risk:** freezing and analyzing while the situation worsens; or, having stabilized, staying in command-and-control mode after the crisis has passed instead of moving back to a domain that allows learning.
- **Diagnostic questions.** Is there no time and no pattern? Would any action that establishes order be better than studying the situation? Is the system in free fall or active crisis? Is the immediate task to stabilize rather than to optimize? If yes, it is Chaotic. Note that some leaders deliberately enter chaos for a moment to break a stuck Clear situation open before steering it into Complex.

## Disorder / Confusion (the center)

The state of not knowing which of the other four domains you are in. This is the most common starting state and the most dangerous, because when people do not know the domain, they fall back on their *comfort domain*, the way of acting they personally prefer, and apply it regardless of fit. The bureaucrat imposes process; the expert calls for more analysis; the firefighter acts. Disorder is where misdiagnosis is born.

- **Decision model:** there is no action model for Disorder. The move is to break the situation into its constituent parts and classify each part into one of the four real domains.
- **Goal:** reduce the size of Disorder. A well-run analysis shrinks the share of the situation that sits in Disorder by classifying its pieces.

---

## The cliff: the fold between Clear and Chaotic

Clear and Chaotic are not neighbors on a gentle gradient; they are separated by a *cliff*, a fold in the model. The boundary runs one way. Complacency in the Clear domain (the answer is settled, attention drifts, the process is no longer questioned) does not degrade gracefully into Complicated or Complex. It collapses: the unnoticed shift in conditions breaks the recipe all at once, and the situation falls off the cliff straight into Chaos. Climbing back out is hard and costly. The practical warning: the most dangerous issue in any portfolio is often the one everyone is most confident is under control.

---

## The core insight and the classic failure mode

The whole value of Cynefin is choosing the *kind* of response that matches the *kind* of cause-and-effect. The classic failure mode is **misdiagnosing the domain**, and it runs in both directions:

- **Best-practice recipes applied to a Complex problem fail.** A single right answer imposed on a system that has no single right answer produces confident, repeated failure.
- **Over-analyzing a Complex problem also fails**, just more slowly and more expensively, because the answer is emergent: it cannot be known in advance no matter how much expertise is brought to bear. The team mistakes Complex for Complicated, studies harder, and the plan still fails. This is the single most common and most costly misdiagnosis in real organizations.
- **Treating Chaotic as anything else** wastes the only thing that matters, time, while the situation worsens.
- **Treating Clear as permanent** invites the fall off the cliff.

Two further truths shape every real analysis: most real situations span several domains at once, so the unit of analysis is the situation broken into issues, not the situation as a single thing; and domains are not permanent, so a classification is a working hypothesis to be revisited as the situation reveals itself.

## Movement between domains

Issues migrate, and recognizing the migration is part of the work:

- **Complex → Complicated.** A safe-to-fail experiment reveals a repeatable pattern. What was emergent becomes analyzable; you can now codify good practice.
- **Complicated → Clear.** Good practice, repeated and stabilized, hardens into best practice and standard procedure.
- **Clear → Chaotic.** Complacency and an unnoticed shift in conditions send a settled process off the cliff.
- **Chaotic → Complex (or Complicated).** Stabilizing action restores enough order that the situation can be re-sorted and thought about again.
- **Anything → Disorder.** A situation grows or changes until no one is sure which domain its parts belong in.
