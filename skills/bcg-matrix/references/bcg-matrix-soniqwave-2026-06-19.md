# BCG Growth-Share Matrix: SoniqWave Audio

> _Illustrative example output for the `bcg-matrix` skill. SoniqWave is a fictional company; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Company / portfolio:** SoniqWave Audio, a consumer-electronics firm with four product lines: wireless earbuds, a legacy MP3 player, a new smart-home hub, and a Bluetooth speaker range.
- **Objective:** Allocate next year's R&D and marketing budget across the four lines, on a three-year horizon.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Thresholds used:** Market growth, **high ≥ 10%/yr, low < 10%/yr**. Relative market share, **high ≥ 1.0 (market leader), low < 1.0**. Both applied identically across all four units so placements are comparable.
- **Note:** Every placement depends on how the unit's market is defined. The definitions are stated per unit in section 3; widen or narrow one and the same product can change quadrant.

---

**Portfolio at a glance:** two Question Marks (one large and near-Star, one tiny), one Cash Cow whose market is shrinking under it, one Dog. **No clean Star** anchoring the future and **no durable cash engine**: the Cow's market is in decline. This shape is the headline finding, not the four labels.

## 2. Per-unit detail

### Wireless earbuds: Question Mark (on the Star/Question-Mark line)

- **Market definition:** true-wireless (TWS) earbuds, global, mass + premium tiers combined. Narrowing to "premium TWS above $150" would raise SoniqWave's relative share toward leadership; broadening to "all wireless headphones incl. over-ear" would dilute it. The 0.9× figure uses the combined-TWS definition.
- **Placement data:** market growth **18%/yr** (high). SoniqWave share ≈ leader × 0.9, i.e. relative share **0.9×**: second place, close behind the category leader. Revenue **$120M**, the largest line and ~55% of portfolio revenue.
- **Why this quadrant:** high growth, share just under 1.0: formally a Question Mark, but it sits on the boundary and is a credible Star if it takes the leader. Unlike a typical Question Mark it is already at scale and likely contribution-positive, so it behaves more like an under-funded Star than a speculative bet.
- **Recommended strategy: INVEST to take share leadership.** This is the one unit where a marginal share gain converts a Question Mark into a Star and, as TWS growth matures over the three-year horizon, into the portfolio's next Cash Cow. Confidence **Medium**: relative-share figure is an estimate against an unnamed leader.

### Smart-home hub: Question Mark

- **Market definition:** standalone consumer smart-home hubs / controllers (excludes hubs bundled into voice speakers and routers). Under a broader "smart-home control" definition that includes Big Tech voice assistants, SoniqWave's share is even smaller.
- **Placement data:** market growth **30%/yr** (high, the most attractive market in the portfolio). Relative share **0.2×**: a distant follower against entrenched platform incumbents. Revenue **$8M**, ~4% of the portfolio.
- **Why this quadrant:** classic Question Mark, attractive market, weak position, cash-consuming, uncertain future. The 0.2× share against platform owners with ecosystem lock-in is the worrying part: building share here is expensive and may be structurally blocked.
- **Recommended strategy: SELECTIVE / build-or-divest, leaning toward a small fixed-budget bet.** Fund only against an explicit kill-criterion (e.g. reach 0.4× relative share within 18 months) and a defensible niche rather than head-on platform competition. Otherwise divest the IP. Confidence **Low**: small base, fast-moving market, share estimate weak.

### MP3 player: Cash Cow (melting)

- **Market definition:** dedicated portable MP3 / hi-res audio players (excludes smartphones, which functionally killed the mass market). Within this narrow surviving niche SoniqWave is the leader.
- **Placement data:** market growth **negative, ≈ −12%/yr** (clearly low). Relative share **1.4×**: the leader of a small, declining niche. Revenue **$40M**, ~18% of the portfolio, and presumed the highest-margin line (low marketing, no R&D).
- **Why this quadrant:** high relative share in a no-growth market = Cash Cow. The caveat is that the *market itself is shrinking*, so this is a melting cow, it throws off cash now but the base erodes each year over the horizon.
- **Recommended strategy: HARVEST.** Milk it efficiently: minimal R&D, minimal marketing, defend the loyal niche (hi-res audiophiles), and route the surplus to the earbuds. Do not invest to "revive" it. Confidence **Medium**.

### Bluetooth speakers: Dog

- **Market definition:** portable Bluetooth speakers, global, all price tiers. A narrow "rugged/outdoor speaker" sub-segment might show SoniqWave stronger, which is the one reason to check before divesting.
- **Placement data:** market growth **~2%/yr** (flat, low). Relative share **0.6×**: a follower, no scale advantage. Revenue **$25M**, ~11% of the portfolio.
- **Why this quadrant:** low growth, low share, a Dog. Likely thin margin given the lack of scale on the experience curve.
- **Recommended strategy: DIVEST or HARVEST**, after checking two strategic exceptions: (a) does the speaker line share components, channel, or brand with the earbuds in a way that makes it cheap to keep? (b) is any sub-segment quietly cash-positive? If neither holds, exit and free the capital. Confidence **Medium**.

## 3. Portfolio balance

**Is it self-funding? Barely, and not durably.**

- **Cash engine:** only the MP3 Cash Cow throws off reliable surplus today, but its market is declining ~12%/yr, so the engine is *shrinking under the portfolio*. There is no second cow.
- **Future:** no clean Star. The earbuds are the only unit that can become one, and they are currently *under-funded* relative to a leadership push.
- **Drains:** two Question Marks (earbuds need investment to win; the hub consumes cash with low odds) and one Dog (speakers) that ties up ~$25M of revenue and management attention for little return.

**Named imbalance: a portfolio with a melting engine and an unfinanced future.** The single cash source is in decline, while the one unit that could replace it (earbuds) is being treated as just another line rather than the priority. Left alone, in three years SoniqWave has a smaller cow, an earbuds line stuck at #2, a cash-burning hub, and a Dog.

**Cash-flow map (intended):**

```
  MP3 Cash Cow (harvest surplus)
        │
        ├──────────────▶  Wireless earbuds  (INVEST → push to share leader → future Cash Cow)
        │
        └──(small, capped)▶  Smart-home hub  (selective bet against a kill-criterion)

  Bluetooth speakers (Dog) ──▶ divest → frees ~$25M revenue base + capital, recycle into earbuds
```

## 4. Recommendations

### Per unit

| Unit | Quadrant | Call | One-line reasoning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wireless earbuds | Question Mark → Star | **INVEST (top priority)** | Only unit that can become the next Cash Cow; one share-rank away from Star. |
| MP3 player | Cash Cow (melting) | **HARVEST** | Fund the earbuds from it; don't reinvest in a declining niche. |
| Smart-home hub | Question Mark | **SELECTIVE / build-or-divest** | Small capped bet with a hard kill-criterion, else exit. |
| Bluetooth speakers | Dog | **DIVEST or HARVEST** | Exit unless synergy or a quiet cash-positive sub-segment justifies keeping it. |

### Portfolio-level reallocation (next-year budget)

1. **Concentrate R&D + marketing on earbuds to take share leadership.** This is the move that fixes the "no future" problem. Treat it as the portfolio's bet, not a line item.
2. **Harvest the MP3 cow to fund it.** Strip its R&D/marketing to maintenance level and redirect the surplus to earbuds.
3. **Cap the hub at a small fixed budget tied to an 18-month share milestone.** Build-or-divest decided up front, so it can't quietly bleed cash for three years.
4. **Divest the speaker Dog unless a synergy or cash-positive niche check passes**, and recycle the freed capital into the earbuds push.

**Resulting three-year shape (intended):** earbuds become a Star and then the dominant Cash Cow as TWS growth matures; the MP3 niche is harvested down gracefully; the hub is either a small second Star or cleanly exited; the speaker drag is gone. The portfolio moves from *melting-cow + unfinanced-future* to *one funded future Star backed by a harvested cow*: a smaller but coherent portfolio.

### Strategic exceptions to the naive verdict

- **Earbuds aren't a typical Question Mark.** The matrix's "invest selectively, expect uncertainty" rule understates them, at $120M and near-leadership they are effectively an under-funded Star. The naive Question-Mark caution would be a mistake here.
- **The MP3 "Cash Cow" is a trap if read as stable.** Its declining market means the surplus is finite and falling; treat it as a harvest, not a hold.
- **The speaker Dog may earn a stay of execution on synergy**, not on its own numbers, check shared components/channel/brand with earbuds before exiting.

---

## 5. Caveats

- **Market definition drives the result.** The earbuds' Question-Mark-vs-Star call turns on whether "the market" is all-TWS or premium-TWS; the hub looks worse under a broad "smart-home control" definition. State and stress-test each definition before acting.
- **Relative share is a crude proxy for advantage.** SoniqWave's 0.6× in speakers or 0.2× in hubs says little about whether either is *profitable*; the experience-curve assumption is weak in commoditized audio.
- **The matrix sees only two dimensions.** It ignores cross-unit synergy (earbuds ↔ speakers components, brand halo from a premium hub), unit profitability, and external forces (chip supply, platform lock-in in smart home). A Dog can be profitable; a Star can lose money.
- **Placements are a snapshot, not a forecast.** Growth rates and shares move; the melting cow is the clearest example.
- **All figures here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.** Use this matrix to structure the budget conversation, not to automate the cuts.
