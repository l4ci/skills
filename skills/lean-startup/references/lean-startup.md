# Lean Startup

Lean Startup (Eric Ries, *The Lean Startup*, 2011) is a method for building products and businesses under extreme uncertainty by maximizing validated learning per unit of time and money. Instead of writing a big plan and executing it, you treat the venture as a set of untested hypotheses and run the smallest experiments that test the riskiest ones, then decide whether to persevere or pivot.

## Core concepts

- **Leap-of-faith assumptions.** The beliefs the whole venture rests on. Two are primary:
  - **Value hypothesis:** that the product delivers real value to customers once they use it.
  - **Growth hypothesis:** that the product will reach more customers (how new customers are acquired and the venture grows).
- **Validated learning.** Progress is measured by what you have learned and proven about customers, not by features shipped or plans completed.
- **Build-Measure-Learn loop.** The core cycle: build the minimum needed to test a hypothesis, measure how customers actually behave, learn whether the hypothesis held, and repeat. The aim is to get through the loop quickly, so the loop should be designed backward, from the learning you need to the experiment that would produce it.
- **Minimum Viable Product (MVP).** The smallest thing that lets you run a full Build-Measure-Learn loop and start learning. Not a smaller product but a learning tool: a landing page, a concierge or "Wizard of Oz" service done manually behind the scenes, a single feature, a pre-sale. Build the least that answers the question.
- **Actionable metrics versus vanity metrics.** Actionable metrics tie to a specific hypothesis and a cause (cohort behavior, conversion, retention). Vanity metrics (total signups, page views, cumulative revenue) rise over time and feel good but do not test a hypothesis or guide a decision.
- **Innovation accounting.** Establish a baseline with an MVP, tune the engine toward the ideal, and then decide: persevere or pivot.
- **Pivot or persevere.** A pivot is a structured change in strategy to test a new fundamental hypothesis while keeping what has been learned (for example, changing the customer segment, the problem, the revenue model, or the channel). Persevere means the experiments support the current path. The decision is made on evidence at a regular cadence, not on hope.

## How to use it

1. **Surface the leap-of-faith assumptions.** Write down what must be true for the venture to work, especially the value and growth hypotheses. Make them concrete and falsifiable.
2. **Rank by risk.** Find the riskiest assumption: the one that is both most uncertain and most fatal if wrong. Test that first, not the easiest one.
3. **Design the smallest experiment.** Define the MVP or test that would produce evidence on that assumption, the specific customer behavior you will observe, and the actionable metric and threshold that would confirm or refute it. Decide the pass and fail lines before running it.
4. **Build, measure, learn.** Run the experiment, measure real behavior against the threshold, and state plainly what was learned.
5. **Decide: pivot or persevere.** On the evidence, continue, or pivot by naming which assumption changes and what new hypothesis the next loop tests.

## Common pitfalls

- **Testing the easy assumption** instead of the riskiest, fatal one.
- **MVP as a small product** rather than a learning tool, so it costs too much and delays learning.
- **Vanity metrics** that rise reassuringly but test nothing.
- **No pre-committed threshold**, so any result gets rationalized as success and no real decision is made.
- **Pivoting on a whim or refusing to pivot at all.** Both ignore the evidence; the discipline is deciding on data at a set cadence.
- **Confusing motion with progress.** Shipping features is not learning. Validated learning about customers is the unit of progress.
