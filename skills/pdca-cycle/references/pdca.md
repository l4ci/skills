# PDCA Cycle

PDCA (Plan, Do, Check, Act) is a lightweight, iterative loop for continuous improvement, also called the Deming cycle or Shewhart cycle. You plan a change and predict its effect, try it on a small scale, check the results against the prediction, and then act by adopting, adjusting, or abandoning, before running the loop again. Its strength is speed and repetition: small cycles that compound, not one big project.

PDCA is deliberately simpler than Six Sigma DMAIC. Use it for fast, frequent, lower-stakes improvement where heavy measurement is not warranted. Use DMAIC when the problem is large, costly, and demands statistical rigor.

## The four stages

### Plan
Identify the problem or opportunity, understand the current situation enough to act, and plan a specific change. State the objective, the change to try, the prediction (what you expect to happen and by how much), and how you will measure the result. A good Plan is small enough to test quickly and carries an explicit prediction, because the prediction is what makes the Check meaningful.

### Do
Carry out the planned change on a small scale: a pilot, a trial, one team, one shift, a limited run. Keep it small so a wrong idea is cheap. Record what actually happened during execution, including anything unexpected or any deviation from the plan.

### Check
Study the results against the prediction made in Plan. Did the change produce the expected effect? What did the data and the experience show, including surprises? This is the learning stage; honest comparison to the prediction, not to a hope, is what turns a trial into knowledge.

### Act
Decide based on what Check showed:
- **Adopt:** the change worked. Standardize it, roll it out more widely, and make it the new baseline.
- **Adjust:** it partly worked or revealed new information. Modify the change and run another cycle.
- **Abandon:** it did not work. Discard it, keep the learning, and plan a different change.
Then begin the next PDCA cycle from the new baseline. The loop never really ends; each turn raises the standard a little.

## How to use it

1. **Predict in Plan.** Write down what you expect before you act. Without a prediction, Check has nothing to compare against and the cycle teaches little.
2. **Keep Do small.** Small scale means cheap mistakes and fast loops. Resist turning a PDCA trial into a full rollout before Check.
3. **Check honestly.** Compare results to the prediction, not to wishful memory. Capture surprises; they are often the real learning.
4. **Always Act, then loop.** Make an explicit adopt, adjust, or abandon decision and start the next cycle. Standardize what works so gains stick.

## Common pitfalls

- **Plan with no prediction**, so Check cannot judge anything.
- **Do at full scale**, turning a cheap trial into an expensive commitment.
- **Skipping Check**, running change after change with no learning (the "Plan-Do-Plan-Do" anti-pattern).
- **Not acting**, leaving a successful trial un-standardized so the improvement never spreads or sticks.
- **One and done**, treating PDCA as a single project rather than a repeating loop.
