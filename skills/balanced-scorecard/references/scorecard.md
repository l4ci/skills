# The Balanced Scorecard: perspectives, structure, and the strategy map

Robert Kaplan and David Norton's framework (Harvard Business Review, 1992; *The Balanced Scorecard: Translating Strategy into Action*, 1996) translates a strategy into a balanced set of objectives and measures, so that a business is not steered by financial numbers alone. Financial measures are lagging indicators: they report results after the fact and say little about what produced them or what will produce them next. The scorecard pairs them with measures of customers, internal processes, and the organization's capacity to learn and improve, so that managers see the drivers of future performance alongside the record of past performance.

The unit of analysis is an **organization or business unit with a stated strategy**, not an industry. The scorecard does not invent strategy; it translates one that already exists. Its value depends entirely on the quality of that strategy. A scorecard built on a vague aspiration measures activity, not progress.

## The four perspectives

Each perspective answers one guiding question about the strategy.

### 1. Financial

**"To succeed financially, how should we appear to our shareholders?"**

The destination. Financial objectives define what success looks like in the language owners care about, and every other perspective is ultimately justified by its contribution here. Financial measures are lagging: they confirm whether the strategy is paying off.

Example measures: revenue growth, gross and operating margin, return on capital employed (ROCE), economic value added, cash flow, cost per unit, revenue per customer. The right ones depend on where the business is in its life cycle (a growth business measures revenue growth and market expansion; a mature one measures margin, cash flow, and return on capital).

### 2. Customer

**"To achieve our vision, how should we appear to our customers?"**

The value proposition. This perspective names the target customers and the markets, and the value the strategy promises them. Its measures split into outcome measures (largely lagging) and the value-proposition drivers that produce them (more leading).

Example outcome measures: customer satisfaction, retention, acquisition, market and account share, profitability per customer. Example value-proposition drivers: on-time delivery, product attributes, service responsiveness, brand and relationship measures. The value proposition is the choice of what to be good at (operational excellence, product leadership, or customer intimacy); the measures should track that choice, not all three at once.

### 3. Internal Process

**"To satisfy our shareholders and customers, which business processes must we excel at?"**

The engine. Given the customer value proposition above, this perspective identifies the few processes the organization must perform exceptionally well, and measures them. It looks across the whole value chain, not just existing operations.

Example measures: quality and defect rates, cycle and lead time, cost and productivity of key processes, on-time performance, new-product introduction time, post-sale service speed. Kaplan and Norton stress including the **innovation process** (identifying needs and developing offerings), not only the operations process (making and delivering today's products); a scorecard that measures only current operations optimizes the present and starves the future.

### 4. Learning & Growth

**"To achieve our vision, how will we sustain our ability to change and improve?"**

The foundation. This perspective measures the intangible assets that everything above stands on, in three groups: people (skills, training, satisfaction, retention), information systems (the data and tools staff need at the point of work), and organizational climate (motivation, alignment of incentives to strategy, culture). Its measures are the most leading of all: they move first and pay off later, which is exactly why they are the most often cut and the most often missed.

Example measures: employee skill coverage versus need, training hours, employee satisfaction and retention, information-systems coverage (share of staff with the data they need), strategic-awareness or alignment scores, suggestions made and implemented.

## The objective / measure / target / initiative structure

Within each perspective, the scorecard is built in four columns. Filling only some of them is a common failure.

- **Objectives**: what the strategy requires of this perspective, phrased as outcomes ("reduce order-to-delivery time"), not activities ("hold weekly logistics meetings"). A perspective has a few objectives, not a dozen.
- **Measures (KPIs)**: how progress on each objective is tracked. One or two per objective. A measure with no objective behind it is noise; an objective with no measure is a slogan.
- **Targets**: the level each measure must reach, and by when. A measure without a target cannot say whether you are winning. Targets should be grounded in benchmarks or a credible improvement trajectory, not picked to be comfortable.
- **Initiatives**: the actions, projects, or programs funded to move the measures toward the targets. Initiatives are how the scorecard connects to the budget and to action; objectives without initiatives are hopes.

## The strategy map and cause-effect logic

The four perspectives are not four separate scorecards. They are linked by **cause and effect**, and that chain of links is the **strategy map**. The map reads **bottom-up**:

```
        FINANCIAL          Revenue growth, margin, ROCE
            ▲               (the results the strategy is for)
            │ produces
        CUSTOMER            Satisfaction, retention, share
            ▲               (the value proposition that drives results)
            │ delivers
   INTERNAL PROCESS         Quality, cycle time, innovation
            ▲               (the processes that deliver the value)
            │ enables
  LEARNING & GROWTH         Skills, systems, culture, alignment
                            (the capabilities that drive the processes)
```

Read it as a chain of hypotheses: *if* we build these skills and systems (Learning & Growth), *then* we can run these processes better (Internal Process), *which* delivers this value to customers (Customer), *which* produces these financial results (Financial). Each arrow is a claim the organization is betting on and can later test against evidence. The map makes the strategy explicit, communicable, and falsifiable.

The map is what separates a Balanced Scorecard from a KPI dashboard. A dashboard is a list of metrics; a scorecard is a set of objectives wired together by a theory of how the business creates value.

## Leading versus lagging indicators

A complete scorecard balances the two:

- **Lagging indicators** are outcomes. They report what has happened: revenue, market share, customer satisfaction scores. They are easy to define and hard to argue with, but they tell you the result after you can no longer change it, and they say nothing about how it was produced.
- **Leading indicators** are drivers. They move earlier in the cause-effect chain and predict the lagging outcomes: training coverage, process cycle time, on-time delivery. They are specific to the strategy (a generic leading measure for one strategy is meaningless for another) and are where management still has leverage.

A scorecard heavy with lagging financial measures tells you the score after the game is over. The leading measures, mostly in Internal Process and Learning & Growth, are what you act on while the game is still on.

## The vital few

The discipline is to measure the few things that matter, not the many things that can be measured. Kaplan and Norton's working guide is roughly four to seven measures per perspective, around twenty in total. The pressure always runs the other way: every function wants its metric on the board, and easy-to-collect data crowds out hard-to-collect data that matters more. A scorecard with thirty or forty measures has no focus and communicates no strategy; everything is important, so nothing is. Prune ruthlessly, and prefer a strategically vital measure that is hard to collect over a trivial one that is easy.

## Pitfalls

- **A KPI dashboard with no link to strategy.** The most common failure: a wall of metrics with no objectives behind them and no strategy map connecting them. Measuring more is not the scorecard; the cause-effect logic is.
- **All lagging, all financial.** Keeping only financial outcome measures recreates the very problem the scorecard exists to fix. Without leading drivers in the other three perspectives, you are still steering by the rear-view mirror.
- **Too many measures.** Thirty KPIs measure nothing. Loss of focus is loss of the scorecard's purpose, which is to communicate and concentrate effort on the few things the strategy depends on.
- **Perspectives treated as silos.** Four independent lists with no cause-effect map is not a Balanced Scorecard. The links are the strategy; without them, each perspective optimizes locally and the strategy never adds up.
- **Measures chosen because they are easy, not because they matter.** Available data drives out important data. The right measure for a strategic objective is often the one nobody currently collects.
- **A scorecard built on a vague strategy.** "Grow", "be the best", "delight customers" cannot be translated into meaningful objectives. Sharpen the strategy first, or the scorecard inherits its emptiness.
- **No targets or no initiatives.** Objectives and measures with no targets cannot tell you whether you are winning; with no initiatives, nothing is funded to move them. The four columns stand together.
