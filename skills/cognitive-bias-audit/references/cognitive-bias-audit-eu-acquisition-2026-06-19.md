# Cognitive Bias Audit: $45M EU-Entry Acquisition

> _Illustrative example output for the `cognitive-bias-audit` skill. This acquisition is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Decision audited:** Acquire a smaller rival for **$45M** to enter the European market within 12 months.
- **Stakes:** $45M is ~one third of cash on hand. A failed integration would force layoffs and likely cost the CEO the board's confidence.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **What this is:** A bias audit inspects the *reasoning* behind a decision and shows where it is systematically exposed. It does **not** prove the conclusion wrong. A clean-reasoned deal can still fail and a biased one can still pay off.

The reasoning as captured: *"We've spent nine months on this deal; their team is excellent; our last acquisition went well; the founder we'd report to is convincing; projections show payback in three years assuming we hit their stated pipeline."*

---

## 1. Verdict

**This decision is heavily exposed, high enough that the deal should not close on the current reasoning without two structural fixes first.** Five of the six pieces of stated reasoning are bias tells rather than evidence: time already spent, a single prior success, a persuasive counterparty, and an unstressed three-year payback that rests entirely on *their* pipeline number. The reasoning contains almost no disconfirming evidence and no downside case, which is itself the strongest signal.

The two biases that most threaten the call:

1. **Sunk cost / escalation of commitment**: "nine months on this deal" is being used as a reason to proceed, not as a cost already gone. This is the bias most likely to push a marginal deal over the line.
2. **Base-rate neglect (with optimism/planning fallacy)**: the three-year payback assumes the target hits *their own* stated pipeline, against a cross-border M&A base rate where most deals miss synthesis targets. The single number the whole financial case rests on is the least stress-tested.

## 3. The dangerous few

### #1: Sunk cost / escalation of commitment (Severity 5, Confidence High)

**Why it threatens this call.** "We've spent nine months on this deal" is doing argumentative work it has no right to. Those nine months are gone whether or not you sign; the only question is whether $45M today buys $45M+ of future value. When time-in is cited as a reason to proceed, a *marginal* deal: one that would be declined on a cold look, gets pushed over the line. With a third of cash at stake and the CEO's standing attached to the outcome, the pull to close in order to vindicate the nine months (and the named reporting relationship) is exactly the escalation pattern. This is the single bias most able to convert "we should probably pass" into "we're signing."

**Prescribe, fresh-eyes restart test + pre-committed kill criteria.**
- **Who:** The CFO and one board member who have *not* worked the deal.
- **What:** Run the "would we start this today?" test in writing. Hand them the deal terms and target financials with the nine-month history and the founder relationship stripped out, and ask: *knowing only what we know now, would we open this acquisition at $45M?* If the answer is anything but a clear yes, the nine months are not a reason to continue.
- **Plus:** Before final negotiation, the deal team writes down the **kill conditions**: the pipeline-conversion floor, the integration-cost ceiling, the timeline slip, that would make you walk, and who has the authority to call it. Set them now, while the stakes are still abstract.

### #2: Base-rate neglect, carrying optimism bias (Severity 5, Confidence High)

**Why it threatens this call.** The entire financial case is one number: a three-year payback *"assuming we hit their stated pipeline."* That pipeline is the target's own projection, and the reasoning never asks how often acquired pipelines actually convert. Cross-border integrations routinely miss synthesis and revenue targets; treating the seller's number as the expected case, with no downside scenario anywhere in the reasoning, means the headline justification for $45M is untested. If real conversion lands well under plan, the three-year payback stretches past the point where it threatens the cash position and the CEO's board confidence. Base-rate neglect and optimism bias are the same wound here: the inside-view best case is standing in for the expected case.

**Prescribe, reference-class forecasting + pre-mortem.**
- **Who:** Corporate-development lead owns the reference class; the deal team runs the pre-mortem.
- **What (outside view):** Build a reference class of comparable cross-border, similar-size acquisitions and pull how their *acquired* pipelines actually converted against projection. Re-run the payback model off that class-adjusted conversion rate, not the seller's stated figure. Report payback as a **calibrated range** (e.g., P10/P50/P90), not a point.
- **What (pre-mortem):** Convene the deal team and assume it is 24 months later and the integration has failed. Each person writes down why, independently, before discussion, to keep the loudest voice and the consensus from steering it. The recurring failure modes become the diligence checklist and feed the kill criteria above.

*(Secondary, cheap to add:)* a **blind review** of the deal memo with the founder's persuasiveness and "excellent team" framing stripped out would directly knock down the halo and authority exposure (#3-tier biases), judge the deal on price, conversion, and integration cost alone.

## 4. Corrected read

Re-stated after accounting for the top biases, the decision is **not refuted, it is unproven on the current reasoning, and the reasoning overstates the case in two specific ways.**

**What the reasoning overstated:**
- The three-year payback is an *optimistic best case*, not the expected case. Off a reference-class conversion rate it likely lands later, and the reasoning gives no range, so the real risk to the cash position is invisible.
- "Their team is excellent," "the founder is convincing," and "our last acquisition went well" are doing more persuasive work than they support. A strong team and a charismatic seller are not facts about price or integration risk; one prior success is not a base rate.

**What the reasoning understated:**
- The downside. There is no scenario in which the pipeline underperforms, no integration-cost overrun, no currency/regulatory drag, none of the duller factors a cross-border, third-of-cash bet turns on.
- The cost of *not* walking away. Nine months and a named reporting line are quietly biasing the call toward yes.

**What survives the audit:** A genuinely strong target team and a real strategic logic for EU entry are not erased by the bias labels, they may well make this a good acquisition. The audit does not say "don't buy." It says the *case as reasoned* can't yet carry a $45M decision.

**How the call changes:** Do not sign on the current reasoning. Convert the deal from "close it because we're nine months in and the numbers work" to "close it **if** it clears a class-adjusted payback range and pre-set kill criteria, judged by people without skin in the nine months." Concretely: run the fresh-eyes restart test, rebuild payback off a reference class as a P10/P50/P90 range, run the pre-mortem, and write the kill conditions, *before* final negotiation. If the deal clears those, it was a good deal and you'll have proven it. If it doesn't, the nine months will have saved you from a worse loss than the nine months.

---

## 5. Caveats

- A bias audit inspects **reasoning, not facts**. It does not verify the target's pipeline, the valuation, or the integration plan; it shows where the *argument* is exposed.
- **Severity and confidence are judgment, not measurement.** The 1–5 scores and high/medium/low flags are calibrated estimates of how much each bias could distort *this* call, not data.
- An **unbiased process can still reach a wrong answer, and a biased one a right answer.** Debiasing improves the odds; it does not guarantee the outcome. This deal could clear every check above and still fail, or fail every check and still pay off.
- **Identifying a bias does not refute the conclusion.** Each flag marks where the process is exposed, which then has to be *tested* (the prescribed moves do that), not assumed wrong. "That's just sunk cost" is not by itself a reason to pass.
- **The audit can itself be motivated.** If anyone uses these labels to kill a deal they already disliked, that is motivated dismissal, the third failure mode. The corrected read above deliberately states what survives the audit for exactly this reason.
- All figures, names, and the scenario here are **illustrative** for documentation and were not researched.
