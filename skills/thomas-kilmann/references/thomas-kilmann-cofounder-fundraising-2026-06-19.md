# Thomas-Kilmann: Co-founder fundraising conflict

> _Illustrative example output for the `thomas-kilmann` skill. The co-founders and company are fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Conflict:** Series A timing. My co-founder wants to raise a large round now and grow fast; I want to stay lean and reach profitability first.
- **Parties:** Me (45%, "stay-lean") and my co-founder (45%, "raise-now"). Two other board seats are split, so the board cannot break the tie.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Context:** Equal-equity co-founders, four years in together; relationship matters a lot; no immediate time pressure; trust is generally high but the disagreement has simmered for months. My habitual style is **avoiding**.
- **Note:** No conflict mode is universally best. The TKI chooses an approach for *this* situation between *these* parties; it does not type either founder's personality.

---

## 1. Verdict

This is the textbook case for **Collaborating**, and almost nothing else fits. Both founders hold equal equity and a split board, so neither can impose an outcome (Competing fails on power). The stakes are high on *both* sides, the round shapes the company's risk profile for years, so neither can casually yield (Accommodating misreads the stakes). The relationship is a four-year partnership you both depend on, trust is still high, and there is no time gun to your head: the exact conditions under which working the problem jointly pays off. Compromising, "raise a medium round", is the **fallback**, not the answer, because a split-the-difference number nobody designed can be worse than either pure strategy.

Your habitual **avoiding** is the worst available choice here and is precisely what has let this simmer for months.

| Mode | Fit | One-line reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Collaborating** | **High** | High stakes both sides, equal power, relationship matters, time available: the full house. |
| Compromising | Medium | A viable fallback (a mid-size round), but reflexively splitting the difference forfeits a designed solution. |
| Competing | Low | Equal equity and a split board mean neither of you *can* win unilaterally; trying poisons the partnership. |
| Accommodating | Low | The stakes are too high for you to simply yield; doing so would also signal you can be rolled on strategy. |
| Avoiding | Low | Already the failing status quo: the issue has festered for months and a fundraise decision cannot drift. |

## 2. The situation

- **What each wants.** Co-founder: raise a large Series A now, trade dilution and burn for speed and market share. You: stay lean, reach profitability, raise later (or smaller) from a position of strength.
- **Stakes for you.** High. The capital strategy sets burn rate, hiring pace, and how much control and runway you keep. It is not a detail you can wave through.
- **Stakes for them.** High and symmetric. Your co-founder believes the growth window is now; to them, waiting is the existential risk.
- **Relationship.** Matters a great deal: four years, equal partners, no clean way to "win" without damaging the thing you both rely on to run the company.
- **Power balance.** Genuinely equal. 45/45 equity with a split board means neither side has a lever to force the outcome. *Unknown:* whether either remaining board member privately leans one way, worth confirming, because it would change the leverage read.
- **Time pressure.** Low, per your account. *Unknown / worth checking:* whether the co-founder perceives a hard window (a closing market, a competitor raising) that you don't, a perceived deadline on their side changes the negotiation even if no real one exists.
- **Trust and history.** Generally high, which is the asset that makes collaboration feasible. But the months of simmering are eroding it; the longer it avoids resolution, the more the trust premium decays.
- **Your habitual style.** Avoiding, recorded for the mismatch check in §5.

---

## 3. Mode fit

### Competing: Fit: Low

- **Fit, factor by factor.** Power: fails outright, 45/45 plus a split board means there is no authority to win with. Stakes: high for you, but also high for them, so a win/lose frame turns your partner into a loser on something they care about as much as you do. Relationship: high cost. Time: no emergency justifies a forced call.
- **Pros.** If you were certain you were right and the cost of losing was catastrophic, a hard stand at least makes your position unmissable.
- **Cons.** The overuse risk lands directly: you cannot actually prevail, so "competing" here means escalation without a path to victory, it hardens positions, spends the trust you'll need, and surrounds you with a resentful 45% partner.
- **Likely outcome.** Stalemate plus damage. You can block the large round, but you cannot impose the lean plan, and you've now made the next ten decisions harder.
- **Concrete move (if chosen).** "I will not approve a large round, and I'll use my equity and board position to block it.", Noted only to show why this is the wrong opener.

### Collaborating: Fit: High

- **Fit, factor by factor.** Stakes high on both sides → both concerns are too important to compromise away, the canonical trigger. Power equal → collaboration is the only mode that doesn't require leverage you don't have. Relationship high + trust still high → the investment is warranted and feasible. Time available → you can afford the slower, harder joint work.
- **Pros.** Surfaces what's *underneath* the positions: your co-founder's "raise now" is probably a fear of missing a window; your "stay lean" is probably a fear of losing control and runway. A solution can address both, e.g., a defined set of growth milestones that *trigger* the larger raise. Produces genuine buy-in, which a 45/45 partnership absolutely needs to function afterward.
- **Cons.** Costs time and emotional energy; the overuse risk is using "let's collaborate" to avoid ever deciding, a real danger given your avoiding habit. Set a decision deadline so collaboration doesn't become avoidance in nicer clothes.
- **Likely outcome.** A jointly designed capital plan (e.g., raise size and timing keyed to named metrics) that both can commit to, plus restored trust from having worked it openly.
- **Concrete move.** "We've both been circling this for months. Can we block three hours, put both our actual fears on the table, and design a capital plan neither of us would have written alone?"

### Compromising: Fit: Medium

- **Fit, factor by factor.** Power equal → the classic compromise setup (two equal parties, opposed positions). Stakes high → this is the argument *against* compromising first: the issue may deserve the full collaboration treatment, not a quick split. Time available → no pressure forcing an expedient settlement.
- **Pros.** Fast, fair-feeling, and reachable without deep alignment: "raise a medium round." A good **fallback** if collaboration stalls or you hit your deadline without a designed answer.
- **Cons.** The overuse risk is the trap here, a medium round nobody designed can be *worse* than either pure strategy: too much dilution to be lean, too little capital to actually go fast. Splitting the difference shortcuts the better solution collaboration would find.
- **Likely outcome.** A mid-size round that half-satisfies both and fully commits to neither strategy, acceptable as a fallback, disappointing as a first resort.
- **Concrete move.** "If we can't design something better by [date], let's settle on a round sized between our two numbers and revisit in two quarters."

### Avoiding: Fit: Low

- **Fit, factor by factor.** Stakes high both sides → avoiding leaves a high-stakes decision unmade. Time → "no pressure" is exactly what enables endless deferral. Relationship → the unaddressed conflict is already straining it.
- **Pros.** Narrow and real: a brief, *deliberate* pause to gather missing facts (the board members' leans, whether a real funding window exists) before the collaboration session can be useful. That is information-gathering, not avoidance.
- **Cons.** The overuse risk *is the current state*: the issue has festered for months, the decision is drifting by default, and you're walking on eggshells. Continued avoidance decays the trust that makes the good option possible.
- **Likely outcome.** The status quo, simmering resentment, a decision made by inaction (default = no raise), and a slow bleed of partnership trust.
- **Concrete move.** Only as a scoped pause: "Before we sit down, let me confirm where the other two board members actually stand, give me until Friday."

### Accommodating: Fit: Low

- **Fit, factor by factor.** Stakes for you → high, which is the disqualifier: accommodating fits when the issue matters far more to *them* than to you, and that isn't true here. Relationship → matters, which is the one pull toward yielding. Power → equal, so you aren't outmatched into yielding.
- **Pros.** Would end the standoff instantly and bank goodwill; defensible only if, on honest reflection, your co-founder's growth case is simply stronger than yours.
- **Cons.** The overuse risk is acute on a strategy-defining call: yield here and you signal you can be rolled on the decisions that matter most, and you lose track of the runway-and-control concerns that are legitimately yours.
- **Likely outcome.** The large round proceeds, your strategic voice shrinks, and resentment likely resurfaces at the next fork.
- **Concrete move.** "You may be right that the window is now, walk me through the case, and if it holds up I'll back the big raise." (Valid only as genuine open-mindedness inside collaboration, not as capitulation.)

## 4. Recommendation

**Primary: Collaborating. Fallback: Compromising.**

This is the situation collaboration was built for: two high-stakes positions, equal power so neither can force it, a relationship worth the investment, trust still (mostly) intact, and time to do the work. The fallback, if the joint session stalls or you hit a deadline without a designed answer, is a **structured compromise**: a mid-size round, not a reversion to avoidance.

**What to say and do:**

- **Open** by naming the cost of the status quo, not by restating your position: *"We've avoided deciding this for months and it's wearing on us both. I want us to actually solve it together."*
- **Ask** for the interest beneath their position: *why* raise large now? Probe for the real driver, a closing market window, a competitor, fear of being out-resourced. State yours honestly: control, runway, the risk of scaling before the model is proven.
- **Hold** your underlying concern (don't bet the company on unproven growth), but **don't hold** a specific round number as a position, numbers are negotiable, the concern is not.
- **Concede** readily on anything that serves their real interest at low cost to yours, e.g., agreeing to *prepare* fundraising materials now so you're ready to move fast the moment metrics justify it.
- **Design** a milestone-triggered plan: name the specific metrics (e.g., a revenue or retention threshold) that flip the decision from "stay lean" to "raise large," so the disagreement becomes a shared dashboard instead of a standoff.
- **Read the switch to fallback** by these signals: the conversation loops without new information, positions re-harden, or trust visibly drops. If you hit your pre-set deadline (recommend ~2-3 weeks) without a designed plan, take the structured compromise and schedule a revisit, do **not** let it slide back into avoidance.

**Default mismatch, flagged.** Your habitual style is **avoiding**, and it is mismatched here in the strongest way. The gap: avoiding is exactly what produced the months of simmering, and on a high-stakes, equal-power, time-available decision it is the lowest-fit mode of the five. What the situation demands is the opposite, to *initiate* the conversation you've been sidestepping and stay in it until there's a decision. The single highest-leverage thing you can do is notice the pull to defer and override it: book the session this week.

---

## 5. Caveats

- The TKI describes conflict *behavior in this situation*, not a fixed personality. Neither you nor your co-founder "is" a mode; you each choose an approach to this conflict.
- The recommendation is only as sound as the situational facts. Two are flagged unknown, the board members' actual leanings (which would change the power read) and whether your co-founder perceives a real funding *window* (which would change the time read). Confirm both before the session; either could shift the fit.
- The other party gets a vote. If your co-founder opens by *competing*, threatening to block, leverage the board, or force the issue, collaboration may not survive contact, and the fallback to a structured compromise (or a scoped, deliberate pause to gather facts) becomes the live plan.
- Choosing the right mode does not guarantee the outcome. Collaboration is the soundest *approach* to this conflict; it still depends on both founders being willing to put their real fears on the table.
