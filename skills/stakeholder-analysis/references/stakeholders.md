# Stakeholder analysis: axes, quadrants, and engagement

Aubrey Mendelow's power-interest grid (Mendelow, 1991) maps the people and groups who affect or are affected by an initiative, so engagement effort lands where it changes the outcome rather than spreading evenly across everyone. The grid plots each stakeholder on two axes and sorts them into four quadrants, each with its own engagement strategy. Beyond the grid, a usable analysis also reads each stakeholder's current stance, what they want, what they fear, and who they sway.

A stakeholder is any individual or group with a stake in the initiative: those who hold formal authority over it, those who supply resources or do the work, those whose work it changes, and those it affects downstream. Internal (sponsors, teams, functions) and external (customers, regulators, partners, the public) both count.

## The two axes

The grid rests on two distinct judgments. Keeping them separate is the whole point of the framework; collapsing them is the most common error.

### Power

Power is the stakeholder's ability to influence the outcome: to advance it, slow it, or stop it. It comes from authority over the decision, control of budget or resources the initiative needs, control of the people who do the work, a veto (formal or de facto), or the standing to make others act. Assess it by asking: if this stakeholder pushed against the initiative, how much could they actually do to it? A stakeholder can have high power they are not currently using.

Rate power 1 (negligible) to 5 (can decide the outcome alone). Anchor the score in a concrete lever, not a job title: a senior name with no control over this initiative's resources or decisions is not high-power for this initiative.

### Interest

Interest is how much the stakeholder cares about or is affected by the outcome. It comes from how directly the initiative changes their work, budget, status, or goals, and from how much they have at stake in which way it goes. Assess it by asking: how much does this stakeholder stand to gain or lose, and how closely are they watching? High interest can be positive (they want it to succeed) or negative (they want to stop it); interest measures intensity of attention, not direction. Direction is captured separately, by stance.

Rate interest 1 (indifferent) to 5 (this is central to their concerns). A stakeholder who is barely affected today can become high-interest the moment the initiative touches something they care about, which is why the grid is a snapshot, not a fixture.

Power and interest are independent. A regulator may have high power and, until provoked, low interest. A frontline team whose job the change reshapes may have high interest and little formal power. Score each axis on its own evidence.

## The four quadrants

Crossing the two axes gives four quadrants, each with a distinct engagement strategy.

| | Low interest | High interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High power** | **Keep satisfied** | **Manage closely** |
| **Low power** | **Monitor** | **Keep informed** |

- **Manage closely (high power, high interest) -- the key players.** These stakeholders can decide the outcome and care deeply about it. Involve them, co-create with them, keep them fully engaged, and bring them into decisions. Most engagement effort belongs here. A key player who turns into a blocker is the gravest threat to the initiative; a key player who is a committed supporter is its strongest asset.
- **Keep satisfied (high power, low interest).** They can block or unblock the initiative but are not currently paying close attention. Meet their needs and keep them comfortable, without overloading them with detail they have not asked for. The risk is that something pulls their interest up and they intervene late from a position of power; satisfied stakeholders move quietly to "manage closely" if disturbed. Watch for what could raise their interest.
- **Keep informed (low power, high interest).** They care, often intensely, but cannot drive the outcome. Communicate with them, answer their questions, and recruit them as advocates and as sources of ground-truth information. Well handled, they build support and surface problems early. Neglected, they become a vocal, organized opposition that borrows power from others.
- **Monitor (low power, low interest).** Minimal effort. Keep them informed through general channels and watch for changes in either axis that would move them into a quadrant that needs more. Do not spend scarce engagement effort here; over-attending the monitor quadrant is a real and common waste.

The quadrant sets the *amount and kind* of engagement. It does not say whether the stakeholder is for or against you. That is stance.

## Stance, interests, and the influence network

The grid positions stakeholders; these three dimensions explain them.

### Stance

Where does the stakeholder currently stand toward the initiative?

- **Supporter** -- wants it to succeed and will act to help.
- **Neutral** -- not committed either way; persuadable.
- **Blocker** -- wants to stop, slow, or reshape it, actively or passively.
- **Unknown** -- not yet assessed; treat as a gap to close, not as neutral.

Stance is a current reading, not a fixed trait, and it is the lever engagement pulls on. Assess it from evidence (what they have said and done), not from assumption. Assuming a powerful stakeholder is on board because they have not objected is how initiatives discover a blocker too late.

### Interests and concerns

For each stakeholder, name what they actually want from the situation (their underlying interest, which is usually not the same as their stated position) and what they fear or stand to lose. Engagement works by addressing these, not by repeating the case for the initiative. A blocker whose real concern is being made to look unprepared in front of their own boss is moved by a different lever than one who fears losing headcount.

### Influence network

Stakeholders are not independent. Map who sways whom: who does a powerful-but-low-interest sponsor listen to, which respected peer can move a bloc of neutrals, which blocker is actually voicing someone else's objection. Informal influence (the engineer everyone trusts, the long-tenured manager whose nod others wait for) often matters more than the formal org chart and is the part a naive analysis misses. The highest-leverage move is frequently not engaging a stakeholder directly but engaging the person who influences them.

## Running engagement

The analysis exists to change positions, not only to record them. With stances and the influence network mapped, the engagement plan works the gradients:

- **Move blockers toward neutral.** You rarely flip a committed blocker into a supporter directly. Aim first to neutralize the objection: understand the real concern, address what you can, and remove the reason to fight. A blocker who can be made indifferent is often enough.
- **Move neutrals toward support.** Neutrals are the most movable mass and often decide the outcome. Give them reasons to engage, route them through influencers they already trust, and convert the high-interest ones into advocates.
- **Hold and deepen supporters.** Keep existing supporters engaged and equipped; a supporter who feels taken for granted drifts to neutral. Use high-power supporters to sway the stakeholders you cannot reach directly.
- **Keep the high-power, low-interest group satisfied.** Pre-empt the late intervention. Give them what they need to stay comfortable so their interest never spikes adversely.
- **Sequence by leverage.** Prioritize the relationships that shift the most: a high-power blocker, an influencer who controls a bloc of neutrals, a key player whose stance is still unknown. Effort follows leverage, not headcount.

Re-run the reading as the initiative moves. Positions shift with each decision and announcement; a snapshot taken at kickoff is stale by the first milestone.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating power and interest as the same thing.** They are independent axes. A loud, highly-interested stakeholder with no real lever is not high-power; a quiet sponsor who controls the budget is. Conflating them puts effort in the wrong quadrant.
- **Assuming stance instead of checking it.** "They're senior, they must be on board" and "they complained once, they're a blocker" are both guesses. Read stance from what stakeholders have actually said and done, and mark unknowns as unknown.
- **Treating the map as static.** Power and interest and stance all move. A satisfied stakeholder whose interest spikes becomes a key player overnight; a supporter who is neglected drifts. A one-time snapshot misleads the moment the initiative progresses.
- **Ignoring informal influence.** The org chart is not the influence network. Missing who actually sways whom means engaging the visible stakeholder and losing to the invisible one behind them.
- **Over-engaging the monitor quadrant.** Low-power, low-interest stakeholders absorb effort that the key players need. Minimal contact is the correct strategy, not neglect to feel guilty about.
- **Mapping individuals when the unit is a group, or vice versa.** Sometimes a faction moves as one and should be a single stakeholder; sometimes "the leadership team" hides a supporter and a blocker who must be split apart. Choose the grain that captures the real lines of power and interest.
