# COM-B and the Behaviour Change Wheel: components and intervention mapping

COM-B (Michie, van Stralen, and West, 2011) holds that a behaviour occurs only when three conditions are met at once: the actor has the **Capability** to perform it, the **Opportunity** for it to happen, and the **Motivation** to do it more than any competing behaviour. Remove any one and the behaviour stops. The diagnostic job is to find which component is the binding constraint, because the intervention must target that component. Motivating someone who lacks the opportunity fails; teaching someone who already knows but lacks a cue fails.

## B: the behaviour, defined specifically

Before any component is assessed, the behaviour must be pinned down: **who** does **what**, **when**, and **where**. "Be healthier" is not a behaviour. "Office workers take the stairs instead of the lift when going up one or two floors during the workday" is. A vague behaviour cannot be diagnosed, because each component is judged against a concrete action by a concrete actor. Name the target behaviour and the target actor before anything else.

## The three components and their six sub-components

### Capability — does the actor have what it takes?

- **Physical capability**: physical skills, strength, stamina, dexterity. Can the actor physically do it?
- **Psychological capability**: knowledge, cognitive skills, attention, memory, comprehension, self-regulation. Does the actor know what to do, how, and can they think it through and regulate themselves to do it?

### Opportunity — does the world allow it?

- **Physical opportunity**: the environment, time, resources, money, materials, cues, accessibility. Does the situation make the behaviour possible and prompt it?
- **Social opportunity**: social norms, culture, what others do and expect, interpersonal cues and language. Does the social environment permit and prompt it?

### Motivation — does the actor want to, more than the alternatives?

- **Reflective motivation**: beliefs about consequences, intentions, goals, identity, plans, evaluations. Does the actor intend and value doing it?
- **Automatic motivation**: habits, emotions, impulses, desires, reinforcement, associations. Do the actor's automatic processes pull toward or against it?

## Mapping deficits to the nine intervention functions

The Behaviour Change Wheel routes each component deficit to the intervention functions that can plausibly close it. Targeting the wrong function — the classic error of reaching for Education or Persuasion when the gap is in Opportunity — wastes effort and discredits the change. The nine functions:

| Function | What it does | Primary deficits it addresses |
|---|---|---|
| **Education** | Increase knowledge and understanding | Psychological capability |
| **Training** | Impart skills | Physical capability, psychological capability |
| **Persuasion** | Use communication to induce feeling or stimulate action | Reflective motivation, automatic motivation |
| **Incentivisation** | Create expectation of reward | Reflective motivation, automatic motivation |
| **Coercion** | Create expectation of cost or punishment | Reflective motivation, automatic motivation |
| **Environmental restructuring** | Change the physical or social context | Physical opportunity, social opportunity, automatic motivation |
| **Restriction** | Use rules to reduce competing behaviours | Physical opportunity, social opportunity |
| **Modelling** | Provide an example to aspire to or imitate | Social opportunity, automatic motivation |
| **Enablement** | Increase means or reduce barriers beyond the above | Physical capability, psychological capability, physical opportunity, social opportunity |

Read deficit to function, not the reverse:

- **Physical capability gap** → Training, Enablement.
- **Psychological capability gap** → Education, Training, Enablement.
- **Physical opportunity gap** → Environmental restructuring, Enablement, Restriction.
- **Social opportunity gap** → Environmental restructuring, Restriction, Modelling, Enablement.
- **Reflective motivation gap** → Persuasion, Incentivisation, Coercion.
- **Automatic motivation gap** → Modelling, Environmental restructuring, Persuasion, Incentivisation, Coercion.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming motivation is the problem.** The most common and most expensive error. People who fail to perform a behaviour are usually willing but lack the capability or the opportunity. Check all three before concluding the gap is motivation.
- **Designing education when the gap is environmental.** Telling people what they already know does nothing if the barrier is a missing cue, a locked door, time, or money. Knowledge is one sub-component of one component.
- **Defining the behaviour too vaguely to diagnose.** If the behaviour is a goal rather than a specific action by a specific actor, no component can be assessed and the diagnosis collapses.
- **Stopping at the binding constraint.** Closing one gap can reveal another that was masked behind it. Note secondary constraints so the intervention does not stall after the first barrier falls.
- **Mapping function to deficit by intuition.** Use the mapping above. Persuasion does not fix a capability gap; training does not fix a motivation gap.
