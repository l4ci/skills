# Design Thinking

Design Thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation: understand the people you are designing for, frame the right problem, generate many options, make ideas tangible cheaply, and learn from real feedback. It is iterative, not linear. Insights from any stage send you back to an earlier one, and that looping is the method working, not failing.

The version here uses the five-mode framing popularized by the Stanford d.school and IDEO: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. They are modes of work, not strict sequential steps.

## The five modes

### Empathize
Understand the people you are designing for, their needs, behaviors, and context, without assuming you already know. Methods: interviews, observation, immersion, and capturing what people say, do, think, and feel. The goal is genuine insight into real human needs, not opinions about a solution. Watch for designing for yourself instead of the user.

### Define
Synthesize the empathy work into a sharp, human-centered problem statement: a specific user, their real need, and the insight behind it. Often written as a point-of-view ("[user] needs [need] because [insight]") and turned into "How Might We" questions that open the solution space without prescribing a solution. A good Define is narrow enough to act on and wide enough to allow many answers. This mode connects to dedicated problem-framing methods.

### Ideate
Diverge: generate a large quantity of ideas across the How Might We questions before converging on any. Quantity and range first, judgment later; defer criticism, welcome odd ideas, build on others. Use varied prompts and techniques so the ideas are genuinely different rather than ten versions of one. Then converge: cluster, and select a few to take forward against the user need.

### Prototype
Make ideas tangible as cheaply and quickly as possible: sketches, paper, storyboards, role-play, a clickable mockup, a fake landing page. A prototype exists to learn, so build the least that will answer the open question. Avoid falling in love with a polished prototype before it has been tested.

### Test
Put prototypes in front of real users and learn. Observe and ask; let them use it rather than pitching it to them. The aim is to find what is wrong while it is still cheap to change, so look for disconfirming feedback, not applause. Feed what you learn back into the relevant mode: refine the prototype, reframe the problem, or return to empathy.

## How to use it

1. **Lead with the user, always.** Every mode is anchored in a real person's need. The moment the work becomes about the solution rather than the user, it has drifted.
2. **Diverge before you converge.** Separate generating options from judging them, in both Ideate and the rest. Collapsing the two kills range.
3. **Loop deliberately.** Decide which mode each new insight sends you back to. Progress is measured in learning, not in finishing stages.
4. **Make it tangible early.** Prototype and test sooner than feels comfortable. Cheap learning beats long planning.

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping Empathize**, so the work solves an assumed problem for an imagined user.
- **Converging too early**, choosing the first plausible idea before diverging.
- **Prototypes too polished**, expensive to build and hard to abandon, which biases testing toward defending them.
- **Testing as selling**, seeking validation instead of disconfirmation.
- **Treating the modes as a one-way checklist** rather than an iterative loop.
