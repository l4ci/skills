# Three Horizons: Lumen Books

> _Illustrative example output for the `three-horizons` skill. Lumen Books is a fictional company; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Organization:** Lumen Books, a mid-sized trade publisher whose revenue is mostly print.
- **Growth ambition:** Double revenue within five years (≈£42M → ≈£84M).
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Horizons are maturity stages, not calendar time.** A long-dated bet that is already winning customers is Horizon 2, not Horizon 3; an old product line that still produces the cash is Horizon 1 regardless of its age. The publishing industry is shifting to digital faster every year, which **compresses** all three horizons into a shorter span than a slow industry would allow.

---

## 1. Horizon map

Every named initiative placed by **maturity stage**. Value is illustrative annual contribution; investment is illustrative annual spend; risk reads as certainty of return.

| Initiative | Horizon | Role | Value (illustrative) | Investment | Risk / certainty |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Print trade list | **H1: Core** | Cash engine; funds everything else | ~£36M revenue, ~£6.5M operating cash | ~£3M (maintenance, advances) | Low risk, high certainty: but structurally declining ~3–5%/yr |
| Audiobook imprint (yr 2) | **H2: Emerging** | The credible next core | ~£4.2M revenue, near break-even | ~£1.1M (catalog, narration, marketing) | Medium: growing fast, not yet profitable at scale |
| Subscription e-reading app (beta) | **H2: Emerging (early)** | A second engine, unproven economics | ~£0.3M (beta/pilot revenue) | ~£0.9M (engineering, content licensing) | Medium-high: demand signal real, unit economics unproven |

**At a glance:** ~89% of revenue and the entire cash engine sit in H1. H2 holds two builds at very different stages. H3 holds a single small option: the thinnest part of the portfolio.

---

## 2. Per-horizon detail

### Horizon 1: the Core (print trade list)

**Initiatives.** The print trade list is the whole of H1: frontlist new titles plus a deep, long-tail backlist. It produces ~£36M of the ~£40.5M total revenue and effectively all the distributable cash.

**Health.** Healthy *as a cash engine*, fragile *as a future*. Margins are intact and the backlist is a durable annuity, but category-level print trade volume is drifting down ~3–5% a year and that is unlikely to reverse. The risk is not collapse; it is slow erosion that a doubling ambition cannot be built on. H1 is over-funded relative to its growth potential and is the source of the tyranny-of-the-core pull described in Section 4.

**How it should be managed.** Run it for **margin and cash yield, not growth**: optimize print runs and returns, defend the backlist annuity, raise operating efficiency, and **ring-fence the cash it throws off** so a fixed share routes to H2 and H3 rather than being recycled into more of the same. Metrics: operating margin, backlist contribution, cash conversion, return rates. Do not measure it on innovation or top-line growth, that is not its job.

### Horizon 2: the Emerging (audiobook imprint; e-reading app)

**Initiatives.**
- **Audiobook imprint (year 2)**: the strongest H2 asset. ~£4.2M revenue, growing fast, near break-even. It has graduated from "experiment" to "business being built": real customers, real catalog, a path to profit. This is the **most credible next core**.
- **Subscription e-reading app (beta)**: earlier-stage H2. A genuine demand signal but unproven unit economics (content-licensing cost vs. subscription retention). Sits at the H3→H2 boundary; it is in H2 because it is already acquiring paying users, not because of calendar age.

**Health.** This is the **best-balanced part of the portfolio**, and it is the part most likely to be starved. Two builds at complementary stages is good. But H2's defining trap is that it needs **patient build capital** precisely when H1's certain returns shout loudest for the same money. Underfund H2 now and the doubling ambition has no engine in years 3–5.

**How it should be managed.** Fund for **growth and a path to scale economics, not current profit**. The audiobook imprint should be capitalized to compound (catalog depth, marketing) and held to revenue growth, subscriber/listener growth, and *trajectory* to profitability, not this year's P&L. The e-reading app should be held to **unit-economics validation** (CAC, retention, content-cost-per-active) before any scale spend; it earns more capital only by clearing those gates. Governance: separate from H1's annual budget cycle, reviewed on milestones, protected from raids by the core.

### Horizon 3: the Options (AI-narrated backlist experiment)

**Initiatives.** A single option: AI narration of backlist titles that are uneconomic to record with human narrators. ~£0 revenue, ~£0.15M spend, high uncertainty on quality, rights, and customer acceptance.

**Health.** **Thin, and that is the headline finding.** One option is not a portfolio of options; it is a single bet dressed as a future. A publisher chasing a 5-year doubling against a digitizing market should be running several cheap H3 probes (new formats, direct-to-reader channels, rights/IP licensing plays, community or education products), killing most and graduating the few that work. With one option, the H3→H2 pipeline can produce at most one graduate, and only if that exact bet happens to land.

**How it should be managed.** Fund for **learning, not profit**. Keep each option cheap, time-boxed, and judged on validated learning and option value, "did we cheaply learn whether this could become a business?", never on near-term revenue or certainty. Applying H1 profitability metrics here would kill the option before it taught anything. The right move is to **widen H3**: add two or three more low-cost probes so the future rests on a spread of bets, not one.

---

## 3. Balance and pipeline

**Does the organization run all three horizons at once?** Partly. All three are *present*, which is better than most incumbents, but the balance is skewed and the H3 end is too thin to call the future genuinely funded.

**Pathology: the tyranny of the core (early-stage, not yet acute).** H1 holds ~89% of revenue, the entire cash engine, and the gravitational pull on every budget conversation. H2 is real but exposed to being raided whenever the core's certain returns make a louder claim. H3 has been allowed to shrink to a single experiment. This is the classic pattern, the measurable present mortgaging the unmeasured future, caught **before** it has hollowed out the pipeline, which is the good news.

**Pipeline trace (should flow inward H3 → H2 → H1):**
- **H1 inflow:** the audiobook imprint is on a credible track to mature into core revenue within 2–3 years. This link is **working**.
- **H2 inflow:** only the AI-narration option could graduate from H3, and it is unproven. This link is **weak**: H2 is being fed by essentially one early-stage option.
- **H3 origination:** **the break in the pipeline.** With a single option, there is little raw material to refill H2 once the current builds mature. The cliff is not in year 2; it is in years 4–6, when H1 has eroded further, the audiobook imprint has become the new core, and nothing fresh has graduated behind it.

**Net:** the front of the pipeline (H2→H1) is healthy; the **back of the pipeline (H3→H2) is starved**. That is precisely the shape that looks fine for three years and then runs out of future.

## 4. Allocation and management

**Shift, illustratively, from "defend the core" toward "feed the pipeline."** Indicative reallocation of discretionary investment (not core maintenance):

| Horizon | Rough current split | Suggested direction | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | ~60% | **↓ ~45%** | Run for cash and margin; stop recycling surplus into more core |
| H2 | ~35% | **↑ ~42%** | Capitalize the audiobook imprint to compound; gate-fund the app |
| H3 | ~5% | **↑ ~13%** | Widen from one option to three or four cheap probes |

The point is not the exact numbers; it is to **ring-fence a fixed slice of H1's cash for H2 and H3 by policy**, so the future is funded *first* and is not the residual left after the core has taken what it wants.

**Manage each horizon by its own rules: one set of metrics across all three would destroy the future or waste the core:**

| Horizon | Funding model | Success metrics | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **H1** | Steady operating budget | Operating margin, cash conversion, backlist contribution, returns | Annual budget; efficiency-driven |
| **H2** | Staged growth capital | Revenue/subscriber growth, trajectory to profit, retention | Milestone reviews, separate from core P&L, raid-protected |
| **H3** | Small, time-boxed option bets | Validated learning, option value, cost-to-learn | Portfolio of cheap probes; kill fast, scale rarely |

---

## 5. Verdict

**Is the future funded? Not yet: but the fix is cheap and the window is open.** Lumen Books is in better shape than a typical print incumbent: it has a genuine H2 in the audiobook imprint, a credible second H2 bet in the e-reading app, and at least one H3 option. The H2→H1 pipeline works. What is missing is the **back of the pipeline**: a single H3 option cannot refill H2 once today's emerging businesses mature, and that produces a future cliff in years 4–6 that a 5-year doubling ambition cannot survive.

**The few moves that matter most:**

1. **Ring-fence H1 cash for the future by policy.** Commit a fixed share of the core's surplus to H2 and H3 before the annual budget fight, so the future is funded first, not last. This is the single highest-leverage move.
2. **Capitalize the audiobook imprint to compound.** It is the credible next core; fund it for growth and judge it on trajectory, not this year's profit.
3. **Gate-fund the e-reading app on unit economics.** Prove CAC/retention/content-cost before any scale spend; more capital is earned by clearing gates.
4. **Widen H3 from one option to three or four.** Add cheap, time-boxed probes so the future rests on a spread of bets. An H3 of one is the report's central finding.
5. **Run H1 for cash, not growth.** Optimize margin and the backlist annuity; stop recycling its surplus into more of the same.

Doubling revenue in five years will not come from the print core, it will come from H2 maturing into the new core while a healthy H3 refills the pipeline behind it. The balance is fixable now, cheaply; left another two or three years, the thin H3 hardens into a cliff.

---

## 6. Caveats

- **Horizon placement is a judgment about maturity, not age.** Reasonable people could place the e-reading app at the H3→H2 boundary differently; the call here rests on it already acquiring paying users.
- **The model shows balance, not winners.** Three Horizons says *whether the portfolio funds its future*, not *which specific bet pays off*. It cannot tell you the AI-narration option will work, only that one option is too few.
- **Industry pace changes the spacing.** Publishing's accelerating digital shift compresses the horizons; in a slower industry the same map would buy more years before the H3 cliff bit.
- **All figures here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.**
