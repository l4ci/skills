# BCG Growth-Share Matrix

The BCG matrix (Bruce Henderson, Boston Consulting Group, 1970) is a portfolio tool. It places each business unit or product line on two axes to decide where to invest, hold, harvest, or divest, and to keep the portfolio's cash flows in balance.

## The two axes

- **Market growth rate (vertical):** a proxy for how much cash a unit consumes and how attractive its market is. High-growth markets demand investment to keep up. The high/low dividing line is commonly set at about 10% annual growth, or at the average growth of the markets in the portfolio. State the threshold used.
- **Relative market share (horizontal):** a proxy for competitive strength and cash generation, via the experience curve (higher share tends to mean lower unit cost). Defined as the unit's market share divided by the largest competitor's share. A value above 1.0 means the unit is the market leader. The dividing line is set at 1.0 (sometimes 1.5). Note: the axis is conventionally drawn with high share on the left.

Size each unit by its revenue (drawn as a bubble) so the portfolio's weight is visible.

## The four quadrants

- **Stars (high growth, high share):** leaders in growing markets. They generate cash but also consume it to hold their lead. Strategy: invest to hold or grow share. Tomorrow's cash cows if the market matures while they keep the lead.
- **Cash Cows (low growth, high share):** leaders in mature markets. They generate far more cash than they need. Strategy: milk them efficiently, defend the position, and use the surplus to fund Stars and selected Question Marks.
- **Question Marks (high growth, low share):** also called Problem Children. In attractive markets but not winning. They consume cash and their future is uncertain. Strategy: invest selectively to build share in the ones that can become Stars, and divest the rest. The hardest quadrant to judge.
- **Dogs (low growth, low share):** weak position in unattractive markets. Typically generate little cash. Strategy: divest or harvest, unless they serve a strategic purpose (blocking a competitor, completing a product line) or are quietly cash-positive.

## The underlying logic

The portfolio should be self-funding over time: Cash Cows fund Stars and the Question Marks worth backing, Stars mature into Cash Cows, and Dogs are released. A portfolio that is all Dogs and Question Marks has no engine; one that is all Cash Cows has no future. Balance across the quadrants and the implied cash flows is the real output, not just the placement.

## Common pitfalls and critiques

- **Market definition drives everything.** Define a market narrowly and a unit looks like a leader; define it broadly and the same unit looks weak. State the market definition for each unit.
- **Relative share is a crude proxy.** High share does not always mean profitability or advantage, especially where the experience curve is weak.
- **Only two dimensions.** The matrix ignores synergies between units, profitability differences, and external forces. A "Dog" can be profitable and a "Star" can lose money.
- **High growth is not automatically good** if winning share there is too costly.

Carry these into the analysis: use the matrix to structure the portfolio conversation, not to issue automatic verdicts.
