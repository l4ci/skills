# Five Dysfunctions: Product Leadership Team

> _Illustrative example output for the `five-dysfunctions-of-a-team` skill. This team is fictional; every behavior, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these observations._

- **Team:** Product leadership team, VP of Product plus seven directors (Growth, Core, Platform, Mobile, Data, Design, and Product Ops) who set the roadmap and own delivery.
- **Surface complaint:** "The last three roadmap commitments all slipped and no one was held accountable. We need more accountability." Diagnosis requested to find why.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Reading rule:** The five dysfunctions are a hierarchy, read bottom to top. The lowest broken layer is the binding constraint; everything above it inherits the crack. The score table is not a sum, the diagnosis is the bottom-up read.

---

## 1. Verdict

**The binding constraint is the base layer: Absence of Trust (severe).** The team has no vulnerability-based trust. In meetings, directors guard their own areas, never expose a slipping deliverable until it is already late, and route real objections through the VP or each other in side-channels instead of saying them in the room. Every layer above inherits this break. The "accountability problem" the VP named is real as a symptom, but it sits three layers up the pyramid, installing accountability rituals now would fail, because the team cannot yet do the trust, conflict, and commitment that accountability rests on.

| # | Layer (base first) | Severity | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Absence of Trust | **Severe** | High |
| 2 | Fear of Conflict | **Severe** | High |
| 3 | Lack of Commitment | **Moderate–Severe** | High |
| 4 | Avoidance of Accountability | **Severe** | High |
| 5 | Inattention to Results | **Moderate** | Medium |

The first broken layer reading upward is **layer 1 (Trust)**. That is where the diagnosis centers and where the rebuild starts, regardless of the fact that the complaint pointed at layer 4.

---

## 2. The pyramid, layer by layer

### Layer 1: Absence of Trust (base) · Severity: Severe · Confidence: High

**Verdict:** Present and severe. There is no vulnerability-based trust: no director is willing to expose a mistake, a slip, or a need for help in front of peers. This is the foundation, and it is missing.

**Tells observed:**
- **Members hide weaknesses and conceal failures.** The Platform and Mobile directors each knew their lanes were behind two to three sprints before the last roadmap review, but neither surfaced it until the slip was unrecoverable. Bad news travels late, and only when it can no longer be hidden.
- **Refuse to ask for help or offer it outside their own area.** When Data was visibly underwater last quarter, no peer offered capacity; the Data director did not ask. Each director defends their headcount and roadmap as private property.
- **Hesitate to give feedback.** Directors do not critique each other's plans in the room. Concerns are real but unspoken: they reappear later as the side-channel disagreements.
- **Guard their reputations.** Status updates are framed to look green; risks are softened. The meeting is a performance of competence, not a working session.

**Tells ruled out:**
- **Open grudges / overt interpersonal hostility.** Not observed. The surface is polite, even warm. This is the trap: the absence of conflict reads as harmony, but it is the *avoidance* that signals the missing trust, not visible animosity.
- **Dreading time together.** No evidence the team avoids each other socially; the failure is specifically about professional exposure, not personal dislike.

**Why high confidence:** The late-surfacing-slip behavior is concrete and repeated across at least two directors and two quarters, and it is corroborated by the side-channel routing the VP described.

---

### Layer 2: Fear of Conflict · Severity: Severe · Confidence: High

**Verdict:** Present and severe. The team substitutes artificial harmony for the productive debate it needs to make good roadmap calls.

**Tells observed:**
- **Boring, guarded meetings; no productive debate.** Meetings are "polite and quiet." Roadmap proposals pass without a hard question. Nobody pushes on a peer's sequencing or resourcing.
- **Back-channel politics where issues are not aired openly.** The single loudest tell: real disagreements happen in side-channels *afterward*, DMs, hallway conversations, one-on-ones with the VP, never in the meeting that was supposed to settle them.
- **Important topics avoided.** Cross-team dependencies (Platform vs. Mobile, Growth vs. Core) are the recurring source of slips, yet the trade-offs between them are never fought out in the room.
- **The same issues raised again and again.** Because debates resolve in side-channels and not in the open, the same prioritization fights recur each planning cycle without ever closing.

**Tells ruled out:**
- **Personal attacks.** The back-channel activity is political and avoidant, not hostile, people work around each other rather than at each other. (Worth re-checking as trust returns; suppressed conflict can surface sharp before it turns productive.)

**Why high confidence:** "Real disagreements happen in side-channels afterward" is a direct, specific observation of displaced conflict, the defining tell of this layer.

---

### Layer 3: Lack of Commitment · Severity: Moderate–Severe · Confidence: High

**Verdict:** Present. The roadmap commitments are nominal, not genuine. Directors leave the room without true buy-in because they were never heard, they never aired their objection in the open.

**Tells observed:**
- **Ambiguity about direction and priorities.** "Agreed" roadmap items mean different things to different directors leaving the room. Because the dependency trade-offs were never fought out (layer 2), each director privately weights priorities by their own area.
- **Fence-sitting.** Directors nod to commitments they quietly do not believe their team can hit on the stated timeline, and then the slip "surprises" no one.
- **Decisions effectively revisited.** Not openly re-litigated in the meeting, but undone in execution: a director who didn't buy in simply deprioritizes the item against their own work.

**Tells ruled out:**
- **Endless over-analysis / windows missed by deliberation.** Not the failure mode here. This team *does* nominally decide, quickly and quietly. The problem is that the decisions are hollow, not that they take forever. (Score held at moderate–severe rather than full severe for this reason.)

**Why high confidence:** Three consecutive commitments slipped with no advance flag, the signature of buy-in that was never real, made possible by debate that never happened (layer 2).

---

### Layer 4: Avoidance of Accountability · Severity: Severe · Confidence: High

**Verdict:** Present and severe. This is the layer the VP feels most acutely. There is no peer-to-peer accountability; the VP is the sole and exhausted source of discipline.

**Tells observed:**
- **The leader carrying the entire burden of discipline.** "The VP ends up making every hard call alone." When a deliverable slips, the VP is the only one who confronts it, peers stay silent.
- **Missed deadlines and deliverables.** Three roadmap commitments in a row, with no one held to account by anyone other than (belatedly) the VP.
- **Peers unwilling to confront one another.** No director has called out another director's slip, even when one team's miss cascaded into their own delay. They route the grievance to the VP instead.
- **Mediocrity tolerated.** A chronically slipping lane is absorbed by the group quarter after quarter without peer challenge.

**Tells ruled out:**
- **Resentment among members holding different standards.** Some likely exists (the side-channel traffic hints at it), but it has not surfaced as open friction over standards, so it is not scored as a primary tell.

**Why high confidence:** "The VP makes every hard call alone" plus three unchallenged slips is the textbook profile of accountability that has collapsed onto the leader.

---

### Layer 5: Inattention to Results · Severity: Moderate · Confidence: Medium

**Verdict:** Present but the least independently broken. Directors privately optimize for their own area's goals over the team's shared roadmap, but there is not yet evidence of ego-driven status-seeking or that the team is bleeding talent.

**Tells observed:**
- **Members focused on their own department's goals over the team's.** Each director defends their lane's roadmap and headcount; the collective roadmap is treated as the sum of seven private plans, not one shared scoreboard.
- **Silos.** Cross-team dependencies are where everything breaks, and no one owns the seams between areas.

**Tells ruled out:**
- **Losing achievement-oriented members / stagnation.** No reported attrition or visible decline; this is why the score is moderate, not severe.
- **Ego and personal status over team.** The failure looks more like *departmental* goal-substitution than individual glory-seeking. Worth watching, not yet a primary driver.

**Why medium confidence:** Results-layer tells are partly inferred from the silo behavior rather than directly observed (no scoreboard data, attrition figures, or stated career-over-team behavior in the intake). Thinner evidence, lower confidence.

## 3. The cascade

Read bottom to top, the breaks are not five separate problems. They are one break, **no vulnerability-based trust**: surfacing four times as it climbs the pyramid:

1. **Trust → Conflict.** Because no director will expose a doubt or a weakness in front of peers (layer 1), they cannot risk open disagreement. So the debate that *should* happen in the roadmap meeting gets displaced into side-channels (layer 2). The "quiet, polite meeting" is not harmony; it is the visible shape of the missing trust.

2. **Conflict → Commitment.** Because the real objections were never aired and resolved in the room (layer 2), directors never felt *heard*, and a person who was not heard does not truly buy in. So the commitments are nominal (layer 3): everyone nods, no one owns it, and the slip is baked in before the meeting ends.

3. **Commitment → Accountability.** Because there was never genuine, shared commitment to a clear standard (layer 3), there is no agreed line for peers to hold each other to. With no shared standard, peer accountability has nothing to stand on, so all discipline collapses onto the one person willing to confront: the VP (layer 4).

4. **Accountability → Results.** Because no one is held to the collective standard (layer 4), each director safely drifts back to optimizing their own area's goals, and the shared roadmap fragments into silos (layer 5).

**Reconciling the surface complaint.** The VP asked for *more accountability*, and layer 4 is genuinely severe, so the instinct points at a real symptom. But accountability is the fourth layer up, and **it cannot be installed directly on this team.** Peer accountability requires a shared commitment to hold each other to (layer 3); that commitment requires having fought the trade-offs out openly (layer 2); and that open conflict requires the trust to expose a doubt without it being used against you (layer 1). All three preconditions are missing. Roll out accountability rituals now, published scorecards, peer progress reviews, "who owns this slip" reviews, and they will harden into another polite performance, with the real reckonings still happening in the VP's DMs afterward. **The accountability problem is the trust problem, three layers up, showing through.** Treat it where it lives.

---

## 4. Interventions and sequence

Rebuild from the base. Do not move up a layer until the one below shows the success signal.

### Step 1: Repair the base: Absence of Trust *(start here)*

The interventions, drawn from the trust layer and fitted to what the analysts saw:

- **The VP models vulnerability first.** This is the non-negotiable first move. The VP names a real mistake of their own, a roadmap call they got wrong, a place they need help, in the room, before asking anyone else to. The team will not expose weakness until the leader does, and the current pattern (VP as sole hard-caller) actively signals the opposite.
- **Personal-histories exercise.** A low-risk opening: each director shares a few non-threatening personal facts (where they grew up, an early job, a formative challenge). It costs little and starts dismantling the "competent stranger" posture.
- **Team-effectiveness exercise.** Each director names one genuine contribution each peer makes and one area where that peer could do more for the team. This forces the first low-stakes reps of the feedback the team currently never gives.
- **Behavioral profiling (DiSC or similar).** Gives the team a shared, non-judgmental language for difference, so a hard exchange later reads as "that's a high-D pushing for a decision," not "they're attacking me."

**Success signal before moving up:** a director surfaces a slipping deliverable, or asks a peer for help, *in the room and early*, before it is forced into the open. When bad news starts arriving on time, trust is returning.

### Step 2: Build Fear of Conflict into productive conflict

- **Mine for buried disagreements.** The VP (or a facilitator) explicitly drags the side-channel objections into the meeting: "I've heard three of you have concerns about the Platform–Mobile sequencing, let's have it here, now."
- **Real-time permission.** When a hard exchange starts, the VP names it as good, "this is exactly the debate we need, keep going", so the team learns conflict about ideas is productive, not dangerous.
- **Assign a devil's advocate** for roadmap decisions, rotating the role, so contrarian views are someone's explicit job rather than a personal risk.

**Success signal:** the dependency trade-offs get fought out in the meeting and the side-channel traffic drops. The same prioritization fight stops recurring every cycle because it now actually closes.

### Step 3: Convert debate into real Commitment

- **End every meeting with an explicit decision review.** State out loud what was decided, who owns it, and what gets communicated downward, and have each director confirm. Ambiguity is the enemy.
- **Disagree and commit.** Make it explicit: a director whose objection was heard and overruled still commits to the decision. Heard, not necessarily agreed.
- **Hard deadlines, honored.** Run worst-case scenarios on risky items to lower the fear of being wrong, then commit to dates and hold them.

**Success signal:** "agreed" means the same thing to all eight people leaving the room, and commitments stop silently unraveling in execution.

### Step 4: Restore peer Accountability *(the layer the VP originally asked for)*

Only now does the fix the VP wanted become possible:

- **Publish goals and standards** so expectations are explicit and no slip can hide behind ambiguity.
- **Lightweight, regular peer progress reviews** against the published standard, peers discuss performance, not the VP alone.
- **Team rewards, not purely individual ones,** so directors have a real stake in each other's delivery.
- **The VP becomes the arbiter of last resort,** stepping in only when the peer mechanism fails, the opposite of today's "every hard call alone."

**Success signal:** a director confronts a peer's slip *directly,* and the VP's DM inbox of grievances goes quiet.

### Step 5: Reorient to collective Results

- **A visible, shared scoreboard** of the team's roadmap outcomes, so the collective result is unambiguous and silos have nowhere to hide.
- **Tie recognition to the team's results,** with the VP reserving reward for those who advance the shared roadmap over their own department's wins.

**Success signal:** directors trade resources across lanes to hit the shared roadmap, instead of defending their own.

---

## 5. Caveats

- The Five Dysfunctions model is a **lens, not a measurement.** The severity scores are a structured read of the behaviors reported, not metrics.
- **Thin intake yields a thin diagnosis.** This diagnosis rests on the handful of behaviors in the brief (quiet meetings, side-channel conflict, three slips, the VP carrying every call). The trust and conflict layers are well-evidenced; the **results layer is the weakest-supported** and was partly inferred, worth confirming with scoreboard and attrition data before acting on Step 5.
- It diagnoses **team dynamics, not individual capability or fit.** Nothing here says any director is a bad operator; the pattern is structural.
- A clean diagnosis **names the binding constraint; it does not by itself rebuild the team.** The sequence above is the work, and it takes months, not a workshop.
- The hierarchy is a **strong heuristic, not a law.** It is possible a higher layer is independently broken, watch the accountability layer (4) as trust is repaired, since the VP's solo-arbiter habit could persist out of reflex even once the foundation returns. If accountability does not recover once layers 1–3 are stable, treat it as an independent break.
