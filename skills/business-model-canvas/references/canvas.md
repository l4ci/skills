# Business Model Canvas: the nine building blocks

Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur's framework (*Business Model Generation*, 2010) describes how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value through nine building blocks. Together they form a single picture of a business model on one page. The point is not to fill in nine boxes but to test whether they fit each other: a coherent model is one where the blocks reinforce each other, and a broken model is one where they contradict.

The unit of analysis is a **business model** for a defined business, product line, or venture, not a company's whole strategy and not a business plan. One company can run several models; if the business has more than one, model each separately or state which one you are modeling.

## The two sides and the hinge

The canvas splits down the middle:

- **Right side (desirability, value to the customer).** Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams. This side answers who you serve, what you offer them, how you reach them, and what they pay.
- **Left side (feasibility and efficiency).** Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, Cost Structure. This side answers what it takes to deliver the value and what it costs.
- **Value Propositions is the hinge.** It sits in the center because it links the two sides: the right side exists to deliver the value proposition to a segment, and the left side exists to produce it.

**Viability** is the test across both sides: do the Revenue Streams plausibly exceed the Cost Structure over time. A model can be desirable and feasible and still fail if it cannot capture more value than it spends.

## Facts versus assumptions

Every block contains a mix of what is known and what is believed. Mark each material claim as a **fact** (evidence: real figures, signed contracts, observed behavior) or an **assumption** (a hypothesis still to be tested). The riskiest assumptions, usually on the right side (will this segment want this and pay for it), are what a young business model should test first. Treating an assumption as a fact is the most common way a canvas misleads.

## 1. Customer Segments

The distinct groups of people or organizations the business creates value for. Segments differ when their needs differ, when they require different value propositions, channels, or relationships, or when they have substantially different profitability or willingness to pay.

Key questions:
- For whom are we creating value? Who are the most important customers?
- Are these one segment or several distinct ones, and on what basis do they differ?
- What is the segment archetype: mass market, niche, segmented, diversified, or a multi-sided platform (where two or more interdependent segments are both needed, for example users and advertisers)?
- How large is each segment and how reachable is it?

A common error is treating "everyone" as the segment. Push for the specific group whose problem is acute enough to act and pay.

## 2. Value Propositions

The bundle of products and services that creates value for a segment by solving its problem or satisfying its need. This is the reason a customer chooses you over an alternative or over doing nothing.

Key questions:
- What value do we deliver, and which customer problem or need does it address?
- Which jobs is the customer trying to get done, and which pains do we relieve or gains do we create?
- What is the source of the value: newness, performance, customization, design, brand, price, cost reduction, risk reduction, convenience, accessibility, "getting the job done"?
- Why this rather than the substitute or the status quo? What is the wedge?

The value proposition must map to a specific segment. A value proposition with no clearly named segment, or a segment with no distinct value proposition, is the first sign of an incoherent model.

## 3. Channels

How the business reaches its segments to communicate with them and deliver the value proposition. Channels run through five phases: **awareness** (how customers learn about the offer), **evaluation** (how they assess it), **purchase** (how they buy), **delivery** (how the value reaches them), and **after-sales** (support after purchase).

Key questions:
- Through which channels do our segments want to be reached?
- Which channels work today, which are most cost-efficient, and which integrate best with customer routines?
- Are channels owned or partner, direct (sales force, web, own stores) or indirect (wholesalers, partner stores)?
- Is each of the five phases covered, or are there gaps (for example strong awareness but weak after-sales)?

Channels must fit the segment. A high-touch enterprise segment reached only through a self-serve website, or a mass-market consumer segment reached only through a field sales force, is a mismatch.

## 4. Customer Relationships

The type of relationship the business establishes with each segment, driven by the goals of acquisition, retention, and upselling (boosting sales).

Key questions:
- What kind of relationship does each segment expect: personal assistance, dedicated personal assistance, self-service, automated services, communities, or co-creation?
- Which relationships have we established, how costly are they, and how are they integrated with the rest of the model?
- Is the relationship type tuned for acquisition, for retention, or for growing revenue per customer, and does that match the segment's economics?

Relationships must fit both the segment and the revenue logic. Dedicated personal assistance for a low-value, high-volume consumer segment will not pay for itself; pure self-service for a complex, high-value enterprise sale will lose deals.

## 5. Revenue Streams

The cash the business generates from each segment by capturing some of the value it creates. There are two basic types: **transaction revenues** from one-time payments, and **recurring revenues** from ongoing payments (continued delivery or after-sale support).

Key questions:
- For what value is each segment willing to pay, what do they pay today, and how would they prefer to pay?
- What is the revenue mechanism: asset sale, usage fee, subscription fee, lending/renting/leasing, licensing, brokerage fee, or advertising?
- What is the pricing mechanism: fixed (list price, product-feature-dependent, segment-dependent, volume-dependent) or dynamic (negotiation, yield management, real-time market, auction)?
- How much does each stream contribute, and which segment pays for what (especially in multi-sided models, where one segment may subsidize another)?

A value proposition that customers love but will not pay for is a revenue-stream failure, not a value failure. Name what each segment actually pays, not what you hope they would.

## 6. Key Resources

The most important assets required to make the model work: to offer the value proposition, reach markets, maintain relationships, and earn revenue.

Key questions:
- What key resources do our value propositions, channels, relationships, and revenue streams require?
- Which categories matter most: physical (facilities, equipment, networks), intellectual (brands, patents, IP, data, partnerships), human (talent, especially in knowledge and creative work), or financial (cash, credit lines, capital)?
- Are these resources owned, leased, or acquired from partners?

Key resources should trace directly to delivering a value proposition or running the right side. A resource that no block depends on does not belong on the canvas.

## 7. Key Activities

The most important things the business must do well for the model to work.

Key questions:
- What key activities do our value propositions, channels, relationships, and revenue streams require?
- Which category dominates: production (designing, making, delivering a product), problem-solving (new solutions to individual customer problems, as in consulting and services), or platform/network (managing a platform, matchmaking, software, brand)?
- Which activities are genuinely core versus those better left to partners?

Like resources, activities must earn their place by delivering value. The canvas captures the few activities that are decisive, not an operational task list.

## 8. Key Partnerships

The network of suppliers and partners that make the model work. Four types: strategic alliances between non-competitors, coopetition (partnerships between competitors), joint ventures to develop new businesses, and buyer-supplier relationships to assure reliable supply.

Key questions:
- Who are our key partners and key suppliers, and which key resources do we acquire from them?
- Which key activities do partners perform?
- What is the motivation: optimization and economy of scale (outsourcing, sharing infrastructure), reduction of risk and uncertainty, or acquisition of particular resources and activities the business does not own?

Partnerships should reduce the resources and activities the business must own. A partnership listed with no clear motivation, or one that duplicates what the business already does well in-house, signals a model that has not been thought through.

## 9. Cost Structure

The most important costs incurred to operate the model. All blocks on the left side drive cost; this block makes the cost picture explicit.

Key questions:
- What are the most important costs inherent in our model, and which key resources, activities, and partnerships are most expensive?
- Is the model **cost-driven** (minimize cost wherever possible, lean structure, low-price value proposition, maximum automation) or **value-driven** (focused on value creation, premium propositions, less concerned with cost)?
- What is the mix of fixed costs (salaries, rents, facilities that hold regardless of volume) and variable costs (which scale with output)?
- Where do economies of scale (unit cost falls as output rises) or economies of scope (the same operations support a broader range) apply?

The cost structure must be consistent with the value proposition and revenue model. A low-price, cost-driven value proposition built on an expensive, value-driven cost base will not be viable.

## Reading the model as a whole

Filling the nine blocks is the start. The analysis is in checking fit across them:

- **Right-side coherence.** Does each value proposition map to a named segment, and each segment to a value proposition? Do the channels and customer relationships fit each segment's expectations and economics? Do the revenue streams capture value from the segments that the value propositions serve?
- **Left-side sufficiency.** Do the key resources, activities, and partnerships actually produce and deliver the value propositions? Is anything required to deliver the value missing, and is anything listed that nothing depends on?
- **Viability across the sides.** Do the revenue streams plausibly exceed the cost structure over a realistic horizon, given the volumes the segments and channels can support? Is the cost structure (cost-driven or value-driven) consistent with the pricing and the value proposition?
- **The story.** A sound model can be told as one sentence: this business serves *these segments* with *this value*, reaches them *this way*, earns through *these streams*, and produces the value with *these resources, activities, and partners* at *this cost*, such that revenue exceeds cost. If the sentence does not hold together, the model does not either.

## Common pitfalls

- **Listing without testing fit.** The canvas becomes nine lists that never get checked against each other. Most of the value is in coherence, not completeness.
- **An incoherent model.** Blocks contradict each other: a premium value proposition on a cost-driven structure, a high-touch relationship for a low-value segment, channels that cannot reach the named segment, revenue streams that no segment is shown to pay.
- **Confusing the canvas with a business plan.** The canvas is a one-page model of how value is created, delivered, and captured. It is not a financial plan, a forecast, an org chart, or an execution roadmap. It frames the bets; it does not size or schedule them.
- **Treating assumptions as facts.** The riskiest parts of a new model (that a segment exists, has the problem, and will pay) are assumptions until tested. A canvas that presents hopes as established facts hides exactly the risk the business most needs to see.
- **Modeling "everyone" or "everything".** Vague segments, value-for-all propositions, and kitchen-sink resource and activity lists drain the canvas of meaning. The discipline is to name the few things that decide the model.
