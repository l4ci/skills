# McKinsey 7-S: Northwind Logistics (Operations Division)

> _Illustrative example output for the `mckinsey-7s` skill. Northwind Logistics is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

- **Organization:** Northwind Logistics, a 600-person regional freight carrier; this analysis scopes the **operations division** (dispatch, line-haul, terminal handling, ~410 of the 600 heads).
- **Purpose:** Diagnose why the post-acquisition integration is stalling and recommend the realignment it needs.
- **Target change:** Northwind acquired **Cardinal Express** (180 people) four months ago. Intended future state: **one merged operations team on a single shared dispatch system**. Since close, on-time delivery has fallen and Cardinal staff are leaving.
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Mode:** Current-versus-desired gap analysis (change context).

## 2. Per-element detail

### Strategy (hard)

**Current.** The acquisition logic is coherent and was the right call: Cardinal overlapped Northwind on ~40% of lanes in the I-5 corridor, and buying it lets Northwind retire duplicate line-haul, raise load factors, and convert a price competitor into captured density. Management frames the win as "density and margin."

**Desired.** Unchanged at the corporate level: but the operations division needs the strategy *translated into an operating plan*: which lanes merge when, which terminals consolidate, what the target cost-per-mile is post-integration. That translation was never written.

**Gap (medium).** The strategy is not wrong; it is **un-operationalized**. There is a deal thesis and a synergy number ($6.2M/yr in claimed lane savings) but no sequenced plan for how operations gets there. Every other element is improvising against a goal nobody specified at the operating level.

**Evidence.** Deal memo and synergy model exist; no integration operating plan in the ops shared drive. Dispatch leads on both sides describe "we were told we'd merge, not how." **Tension already visible with Structure and Systems**: both are stuck waiting for a plan that doesn't exist.

### Structure (hard)

**Current.** On paper, one operations division under VP Ops Dana Reyes. In practice, **two intact hierarchies**: Northwind dispatch under its three regional desk leads, Cardinal dispatch still under its surviving ops manager reporting on a dotted line to Reyes, who is simultaneously running the legacy network. Decision rights at the seam are undefined: when a Cardinal load needs a Northwind tractor, nobody owns the call.

**Desired.** A single dispatch leadership layer, regional desks that own merged territory end to end, and one accountable owner for cross-network moves.

**Gap (large).** The merge was announced, not executed. The "dotted line" is the tell, it preserves both chains of command and creates a coordination vacuum exactly where loads cross between the legacy networks.

**Evidence.** Two org charts still in use; a single "future state" slide with no transition dates. Reyes's calendar shows ~70% on legacy issues. **Tension with Systems** (no shared platform to coordinate across the structural seam) **and Style** (top-down redraw with no Cardinal input). Confidence high.

### Systems (hard)

**Current.** The two dispatch/TMS platforms run in parallel: Northwind's **Helm** and Cardinal's **RouteOne**. They do not talk. Cross-network loads are reconciled by **manual spreadsheet handoff** twice a day, which is where the on-time misses concentrate. KPIs are defined differently on each side (Northwind measures on-time to appointment; Cardinal measured on-time to the customer's informal expectation), so even the dashboards disagree about whether service is slipping.

**Desired.** One shared dispatch system, one SOP set, one KPI definition, the literal central promise of the integration.

**Gap (critical).** This is the load-bearing failure. The merger's value and the merged team's daily work both depend on a single system that has not been built or chosen (Helm vs. RouteOne vs. new is still undecided at month four).

**Evidence.** Two live platforms; the twice-daily reconciliation spreadsheet; on-time delivery down ~9 points (94% → 85%) since close, with misses clustered on cross-network loads. **Tension with Strategy** (no operating plan to drive the system decision) **and Staff** (Cardinal dispatchers, who know RouteOne, are the ones leaving). Confidence high.

### Shared Values (soft, center)

**Current.** Northwind's lived value is **process and safety discipline**: follow the SOP, log everything, no cowboy dispatch. Cardinal's lived value was **speed and customer-first improvisation**: fix the customer's problem now, paperwork later. Both worked in their own contexts. Post-close there is **no merged statement of what the combined division stands for**, so each side reads the other through its own value and finds it wanting: Northwind sees Cardinal as sloppy; Cardinal sees Northwind as bureaucratic and slow.

**Desired.** One articulated culture that explicitly keeps Cardinal's responsiveness *inside* Northwind's discipline, "fast, and accountable", rather than letting one value silently win.

**Gap (large).** Untouched. Nobody has named the combined values, so the default is that Northwind's process culture is being imposed by sheer headcount, which is precisely what Cardinal staff are leaving over.

**Evidence.** No merged values/charter document; town-hall language is all Northwind framing ("align to our standards"). Exit-interview themes from departing Cardinal staff: "rules over customers," "no say." Confidence medium (soft, judgment-based). **This element is at the center, its drift shows up in Style, Staff, and Skills simultaneously.**

### Skills (soft)

**Current.** As an organization, Northwind is genuinely good at **network optimization, capacity planning, and compliance**. Cardinal was good at **last-mile responsiveness and account rescue**: the scrappy recovery of a late or at-risk shipment that retains a customer. These are complementary, and capturing Cardinal's responsiveness was part of the implicit thesis.

**Desired.** A merged division fluent in both: optimized network *and* responsive recovery, with dispatchers cross-trained across the two playbooks.

**Gap (large, and widening).** Cardinal's distinctive skill lives in the heads of its dispatchers and account managers, and those are the people leaving. Each departure is an irreversible withdrawal of the exact capability the acquisition was partly meant to buy. No cross-training has started.

**Evidence.** No capability map of the merged team; no cross-training program. Account-rescue response times on former Cardinal accounts have lengthened since two senior Cardinal dispatchers left. **Tension with Staff** (the skill leaves when the staff leaves) **and Style** (a directive style isn't extracting or honoring Cardinal know-how). Confidence medium.

### Style (soft)

**Current.** Northwind leadership is **directive and metric-driven**: decisions made at the top, communicated down, fast on numbers, slow on consultation. The integration has been run in exactly this mode: Northwind executives set the direction, Cardinal managers were briefed not consulted. Cardinal's culture had been **participative and hands-on**, so its people experience the new style as both demotion and silencing.

**Desired.** Visibly **joint** leadership of the integration, Cardinal managers with real decision authority over the merged dispatch, not just seats in the room.

**Gap (large).** Style is not a backdrop here; it is an active cause. The top-down handling is what converts cultural difference into attrition.

**Evidence.** Integration steering committee is 6 Northwind, 1 Cardinal. No Cardinal manager owns a workstream. Departing managers cite "no authority, no voice." Confidence medium. **Tension with Shared Values** (directive style enforces Northwind's value by default) **and Staff** (style drives the leaving).

### Staff (soft)

**Current.** 410 operations heads post-close. **Cardinal attrition ~22% in four months**: including 2 of 5 Cardinal operations managers and a cluster of senior dispatchers. No retention package, no integration roles defined for surviving Cardinal staff, no clarity on who keeps their job when the systems and structure merge. The uncertainty itself accelerates departures.

**Desired.** A stable, retained, cross-staffed merged team with defined roles and a credible reason for Cardinal people to stay.

**Gap (critical).** The people are the integration. Losing them at this rate hollows out both the Skills element and any chance of a smooth Systems cutover (RouteOne expertise is walking out).

**Evidence.** HR attrition data (22% vs. Northwind baseline ~7%); no retention bonuses issued; org-design uncertainty unresolved at month four. Confidence high. **Tension with nearly everything**: Style, Shared Values, Skills, and Systems all bleed through Staff.

## 3. Alignment analysis

The framework's payoff is the fit between elements. Below, the consequential pairs are scored as **reinforcing (+)**, **neutral (○)**, or **conflicting (–)**, with emphasis on each element vs. Shared Values, Strategy vs. Structure/Systems, and Style/Staff vs. the Skills the strategy needs.

### Alignment matrix (operations division, post-close)

| | Strat | Struct | Sys | Values | Skills | Style | Staff |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** |: | – | – | ○ | ○ | – | – |
| **Structure** | – |: | – | – | ○ | – | – |
| **Systems** | – | – |: | – | – | ○ | – |
| **Shared Values** | ○ | – | – |: | – | – | – |
| **Skills** | ○ | ○ | – | – |: | – | – |
| **Style** | – | – | ○ | – | – |: | – |
| **Staff** | – | – | – | – | – | – |: |

Read the matrix as "does element row reinforce or fight element column." It is almost entirely negative: the signature of a stalled integration where no element has been realigned to the others.

### The fit, grouped

**Reinforcing / latent (the assets):**
- **Strategy ↔ Skills (○→+ potential):** Northwind's optimization skill and Cardinal's responsiveness are genuinely complementary; the strategy *could* be served by their combination. This is the one strong latent fit, and it is decaying as Skills leave.
- Nothing else currently reinforces; the rest of the system is pulling apart.

**Conflicting (the damage):**
- **Strategy – Structure / – Systems:** A strategy of "merge for density" sits on top of two intact hierarchies and two un-merged systems. The wiring cannot deliver the plan because the plan was never operationalized into the wiring.
- **Structure – Systems:** A dotted-line, two-hierarchy structure coordinated by manual spreadsheets across two platforms, the structural seam and the systems seam are the *same* seam, and it is exactly where on-time delivery is failing.
- **Shared Values – Style / – Staff / – Skills:** With no merged values, Northwind's process culture wins by default; a directive Style enforces it; Cardinal Staff leave over it; and Cardinal Skills leave with them. This is the central rot, Shared Values is conflicting with four soft/hard elements at once.
- **Style – Skills / – Staff:** A top-down style neither extracts Cardinal's tacit responsiveness skill nor retains the people who hold it.
- **Staff – everything:** Attrition is the symptom through which every other misalignment expresses itself.

The diagnosis the matrix forces: this is **not a systems-integration problem with a culture side-effect.** It is a **Shared-Values vacuum** that, left unfilled, let a directive Style impose one culture, which is draining Staff and Skills, while the hard elements (Structure, Systems) stall for lack of an operationalized Strategy. The hard and soft failures are reinforcing each other.

## 4. Key misalignments (ranked)

Ranked by impact on the merger's success. Each fix names the **element to change** and the **knock-on adjustments** it forces, because in 7-S no fix is local.

### 1. Staff attrition is irreversibly draining Cardinal's Skills (Staff – Skills, – Strategy)
**Consequence.** Every senior Cardinal dispatcher who leaves removes the responsiveness capability the acquisition partly paid for and the RouteOne knowledge a clean system cutover needs. At ~22% in four months this is compounding and one-way.
**Fix (Staff).** Issue retention packages to the named-critical Cardinal dispatchers and the remaining ops managers *this week*; define integration roles so survivors know they have a future.
**Knock-on.** Forces **Structure** (you must name the roles you're retaining people *into*), **Style** (retention is hollow unless those people get real authority, see #2), and **Skills** (retention only preserves capability if cross-training starts before the next departure). A retention bonus with no role and no voice will fail.

### 2. A Shared-Values vacuum + directive Style is imposing one culture (Values – Style – Staff)
**Consequence.** Without a stated merged culture, Northwind's process value wins by headcount; the directive style enforces it; Cardinal experiences erasure and leaves. This is the engine of #1.
**Fix (Shared Values, then Style).** Name the combined value explicitly, "responsive *and* accountable", and rebalance the integration leadership so Cardinal managers own real workstreams (target steering committee to near-parity, not 6:1).
**Knock-on.** Forces **Style** to shift from directive to joint (hard, because it is the genuine leadership habit), **Staff** (gives survivors the voice retention money alone can't buy), and **Skills** (a culture that values responsiveness will protect the skill that delivers it). Values is the center, moving it ripples everywhere, which is the point.

### 3. Two systems across two hierarchies are breaking on-time delivery (Systems – Structure, – Strategy)
**Consequence.** The manual twice-daily reconciliation at the platform seam is where the 9-point on-time drop lives. It worsens with every RouteOne expert who leaves.
**Fix (Systems).** Make the platform decision now (the un-operationalized Strategy must finally choose Helm / RouteOne / migrate) and define one KPI standard so both sides measure the same service.
**Knock-on.** Forces **Strategy** (write the operating plan that sequences the cutover, see #4), **Structure** (a single system needs a single dispatch leadership to run it, not a dotted line), and **Staff/Skills** (retain RouteOne knowledge *before* cutover, or the migration loses its guides, ties back to #1).

### 4. Strategy was never operationalized for the division (Strategy – Structure – Systems)
**Consequence.** No sequenced integration plan means Structure, Systems, and lane consolidation all improvise against an unstated target; the $6.2M synergy is unscheduled and slipping.
**Fix (Strategy).** Write the operations integration plan: lane-merge sequence, terminal consolidation dates, target cost-per-mile, system-cutover milestones, with a single accountable owner.
**Knock-on.** Forces **Structure** (collapse the dotted line into one accountable dispatch org to execute the plan) and **Systems** (the plan picks the platform and sets the cutover date). Without this, fixes #1–#3 lack a destination.

### 5. Structure preserves two chains of command at the seam (Structure – Systems – Strategy)
**Consequence.** Undefined decision rights for cross-network loads create the coordination vacuum that the spreadsheet papers over and that on-time misses fall into.
**Fix (Structure).** Collapse to one dispatch leadership with regional desks owning merged territory; give cross-network moves a single owner.
**Knock-on.** Forces **Staff** (this defines whose job is whose, must be paired with #1 retention so the restructure doesn't read as a purge) and **Style** (who gets the merged desk-lead roles is the most visible signal of whether leadership is joint or a takeover, ties to #2).

## 5. Verdict and next steps

**Overall coherence: low, and self-reinforcing in the wrong direction.** Four months in, not one of the seven elements has been realigned to the others. The matrix is almost wholly negative. The integration is reading, to Cardinal's people, correctly, as an absorption, not a merger: Northwind's structure, systems, values, and style are all simply persisting, and Cardinal's contribution (its responsiveness skill, carried by its staff) is being lost rather than captured. On-time delivery is the visible symptom; attrition is the mechanism; the **Shared-Values vacuum is the root**.

**The realignment is sequenced, soft-first.** The instinct is to fix the hard problem (one system) first. That is wrong here, because the system cutover *depends on* the people (RouteOne knowledge) who are leaving over the soft failures. Sequence:

1. **Stop the bleed (Staff + Style + Values), this week.** Retention for named-critical Cardinal staff *coupled with* real workstream authority and an explicitly stated combined value. These three move together or not at all, money without voice fails, voice without a stated culture is performative. This buys the time everything else needs.
2. **Operationalize the Strategy (week 2–3).** Write the ops integration plan with one accountable owner; it sets the destination for structure and systems.
3. **Collapse the Structure (week 3–4).** One dispatch leadership, merged regional desks, single owner for cross-network loads, paired with the retention from step 1 so it doesn't read as a purge.
4. **Cut over the Systems (month 2–4).** Decide the platform, standardize the KPI, migrate, while the retained RouteOne experts are still present to guide it.
5. **Cross-train Skills (continuous).** Lock in the complementary capability so it survives any further turnover.

For this merger to succeed, the soft elements must be realigned *before* the hard cutover, not after. The deal thesis is sound; the integration is failing on execution, and the failure is fixable only if it is treated as a 7-S realignment rather than an IT project.

---

## 6. Caveats

- 7-S is a **structured snapshot**, not a forecast. It maps the fit at month four; it does not predict the on-time number after a fix.
- The **soft elements (Shared Values, Skills, Style, Staff) are judgment, not measurement.** They carry medium confidence here and rest on exit-interview themes and observed behavior, not metrics. They are also where this diagnosis concentrates, so they are the assessments most worth pressure-testing with real culture and capability data before acting.
- **Alignment is necessary but not sufficient.** A perfectly aligned operations division can still lose to fuel costs, a freight-demand downturn, or a competitor, 7-S explains internal coherence, not external fate.
- All figures (attrition rate, on-time points, synergy dollars, headcounts) are **illustrative for documentation** and were not researched.
