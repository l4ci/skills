# business-model-canvas

Runs a Business Model Canvas of a company, product, or venture, mapping how it creates, delivers, and captures value. The canvas comes from Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur (*Business Model Generation*, 2010) and lays a business model out on one page in nine building blocks: Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, and Revenue Streams on the right side, where value reaches the customer; Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, and Cost Structure on the left, where the value gets produced. Value Propositions sits in the middle and joins the two halves.

This skill runs the analysis in parallel. Nine analyst subagents each take one block, describe it for the specific business with real evidence, and mark every claim as a fact or an assumption still to be tested. A synthesis pass then checks whether the blocks fit: whether the value propositions match the segments, whether channels and relationships suit each segment, and whether the revenue streams plausibly outrun the cost structure. It tells the model as one story and points to the weakest links. The output is a saved markdown report with the canvas laid out block by block, a verdict on coherence and viability, and the riskiest assumptions called out.

The framework fails in predictable ways, and the skill is built around them. People fill the nine boxes and never check that the boxes agree, so a premium offer ends up bolted to a bargain cost base or a high-touch relationship to a low-value segment. People also write down what they wish were true as if it were settled, which buries the very risk a young model most needs to see. Separating fact from assumption in each block, and ranking the contradictions in synthesis, is how the skill surfaces both. It also holds the line that a canvas is a model of the business, not a plan for it: no forecasts, no roadmaps, just whether the model hangs together.
