# Impact vs. Feasibility Matrix

A prioritization tool that plots a set of options (initiatives, ideas, features, projects) on two axes and sorts them into four quadrants to decide what to do first, what to plan, and what to drop. Also known as the impact-effort matrix (feasibility is the inverse of effort and risk).

## The two axes

- **Impact (vertical):** how much an option advances the objective if done. The definition of impact must be stated up front and tied to the objective (revenue, users, risk reduction, strategic fit, whatever the goal is). Without a stated definition, scores are arbitrary.
- **Feasibility (horizontal):** how readily the option can be done, given effort, cost, time, technical difficulty, dependencies, and risk. High feasibility means low effort and low risk.

## The four quadrants

- **High impact, high feasibility: Quick wins.** Do these first. Maximum return for the least difficulty. The portfolio's momentum.
- **High impact, low feasibility: Major projects.** Worth doing but hard. Plan them properly, sequence them, and break them down; do not start them casually or all at once.
- **Low impact, high feasibility: Fill-ins.** Easy but marginal. Do them only with spare capacity, and beware letting easy work crowd out the major projects.
- **Low impact, low feasibility: Thankless tasks.** Avoid or drop. Hard and not worth it. The clearest output of the exercise is usually permission to stop doing these.

## How to use it

1. **Define the axes first.** State exactly what impact means for this objective and what drives feasibility (effort, cost, risk, dependencies). Set these before scoring so every option is judged on the same scale.
2. **Score every option** on impact (1 to 5) and feasibility (1 to 5), each with a one-line rationale and the evidence or assumption behind it. Note dependencies between options, since one option's feasibility can hinge on another being done first.
3. **Place and read the quadrants.** Assign each option to a quadrant from its scores.
4. **Sequence, do not just sort.** Quick wins first to build momentum and free capacity, major projects planned and staged, fill-ins as capacity allows, thankless tasks dropped. Respect dependencies: a low-feasibility enabler may need to come before a quick win that depends on it.

## Common pitfalls

- **Undefined axes.** "Impact" and "feasibility" mean different things to different people; unstated, the matrix just encodes bias. Define them.
- **Optimism on feasibility.** Effort and risk are routinely underestimated. Probe dependencies and unknowns rather than taking the first guess.
- **Ignoring dependencies.** Options are rarely independent; sequencing must respect what enables what.
- **Fill-in creep.** Teams gravitate to easy, low-impact work because it feels productive, starving the high-impact projects. Name this risk.
- **Treating the score as the decision.** The matrix structures the call; strategic must-dos and hard constraints can still override a quadrant.
