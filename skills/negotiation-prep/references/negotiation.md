# Principled negotiation: principles, BATNA, ZOPA, and pitfalls

Principled (interest-based) negotiation comes from Roger Fisher and William Ury, *Getting to Yes* (1981). It rejects the choice between hard bargaining (win at any cost, wreck the relationship) and soft bargaining (concede to keep the peace) and decides the substance on its merits instead. The whole discipline is preparation: you settle interests, alternatives, and standards before you are in the room, not while you are reacting in it.

## The four principles

1. **Separate the people from the problem.** Negotiators are people first, with egos, fears, and a relationship at stake. Attack the problem, not each other. Deal with emotions and misperceptions directly and openly, so they do not contaminate the substance. A concession on the merits is not a concession to a person.
2. **Focus on interests, not positions.** A position is a single answer someone has settled on; an interest is the need, fear, or desire underneath it. Positions collide; interests often do not. Behind opposed positions lie shared and compatible interests as well as conflicting ones. Ask "why" and "why not" to surface them.
3. **Invent options for mutual gain.** Before deciding, generate possibilities. Separate inventing from deciding (do not judge while brainstorming), widen the options on the table rather than search for a single answer, look for shared gain, and find ways to make the other side's decision easy. A larger pie before it is divided.
4. **Insist on objective criteria.** When interests conflict directly, settle by reference to a standard independent of either side's will: market value, precedent, expert opinion, cost, professional norms, law, what a fair procedure would yield. Frame each issue as a joint search for the fair standard; yield to principle, never to pressure.

## Positions versus interests

A position is what someone says they want. An interest is why they want it. The classic illustration: two sisters quarrel over one orange and split it in half. One squeezes her half for juice and throws the peel away; the other grates the peel of her half for a cake and throws the fruit away. Their positions were identical ("I want the orange"), so the obvious compromise halved it. Their interests were complementary, and had they asked why, each could have had all of what she needed. Positions hide the deal that interests would reveal.

Probe for interests by asking why a side holds a position, why not the alternative they reject, and what would happen to them if they did not get it. Most positions rest on several interests, and the most powerful are basic human needs: security, recognition, control, belonging, economic well-being.

## BATNA: the real source of power

**BATNA** is your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement: what you will actually do if this deal falls through (take the other job offer, sue, stay with the current vendor, walk away). It is the standard against which any proposed agreement is measured. You should reject any deal worse than your BATNA and accept any deal better than it.

Your power in a negotiation comes from your BATNA, not from stubbornness, size, or bluster. A strong, well-understood alternative lets you negotiate calmly and walk if needed; a weak or unknown one makes you negotiate from fear. Therefore: identify your BATNA, improve it before you negotiate (a better outside option is leverage you build, not luck), and never reveal a weak one. Estimate the other side's BATNA too: the weaker their alternative, the more they need a deal with you.

The opposite error is the "bottom line" fixation, a single rigid figure set in advance to protect against a weak agreement. It is too inflexible and inhibits invention. The BATNA is the better safeguard because it is a real-world alternative, not an arbitrary number.

## Reservation point, target, and ZOPA

- **Reservation point (walk-away).** The least favorable deal you would still accept, derived from your BATNA. Past it, your alternative is better than the deal.
- **Target (aspiration).** The ambitious-but-justifiable outcome you open toward and aim for. Aspirations anchor the negotiation; set them by what objective criteria can support, not by what feels safe.
- **ZOPA**, the Zone Of Possible Agreement, is the overlap between your reservation point and theirs. If your walk-away is "pay no more than 100" and the seller's is "accept no less than 80," any deal between 80 and 100 beats both sides' alternatives, and the ZOPA is 80 to 100. **The ZOPA may not exist.** If your maximum is below their minimum, no agreement beats both BATNAs, and no deal is the correct outcome. Recognizing an empty ZOPA early saves both sides from a deal that is worse than walking away. The single most common self-deception is confusing your aspiration with the ZOPA: where you wish the deal would land says nothing about whether their numbers allow it.

## Anchoring, concessions, and hardball tactics

- **Anchoring.** The first credible number shapes the range. Open with an ambitious figure you can justify on objective criteria, not an arbitrary extreme. If they anchor first and unreasonably, do not counter from their anchor; re-anchor to a standard.
- **Concessions.** Plan them in advance and make them shrink in size and slow in pace, so the other side reads that you are approaching your limit. Trade concessions for things you value less than they do (the orange again). Concede to principle and to interests, never merely to pressure.
- **Hardball tactics.** Recognize them and name the game rather than playing it: extreme anchors, good-cop/bad-cop, false deadlines, escalating demands, "take it or leave it," personal attacks, lock-in commitments. The counter is to separate the people from the problem, insist on objective criteria, and fall back on your BATNA. A tactic only works if you accept its frame.

## Classic failure modes (guard against these)

- **Positional bargaining.** Haggling over numbers with no grasp of why either side wants them. It produces arbitrary splits, strains the relationship, and leaves joint gains on the table. Guard: surface interests before trading positions.
- **No BATNA.** Walking in without knowing your alternative means negotiating blind, from fear, unable to tell a good deal from a bad one. Guard: define and strengthen your BATNA, and estimate theirs, before the session.
- **Aspiration mistaken for ZOPA.** Treating the outcome you want as the outcome that is possible. Guard: derive both reservation points and check whether they actually overlap.
- **Win/lose framing.** Assuming every gain for them is a loss for you, so you never look for mutual-gain options. Guard: invent options before dividing, separating creating value from claiming it.
