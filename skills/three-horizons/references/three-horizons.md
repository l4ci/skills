# Three Horizons Model

The Three Horizons model (McKinsey; Baghai, Coley, and White, *The Alchemy of Growth*, 1999) is a way to manage growth and innovation across time so that today's core business and tomorrow's new businesses are funded at the same time. It splits initiatives into three horizons, which are stages of maturity, not fixed calendar periods.

## The three horizons

- **Horizon 1: the core.** The mature businesses that produce most of today's revenue and cash. The job is to defend, extend, and optimize them, improving margin and efficiency. High certainty, near-term returns. Eventually every H1 business declines, so it cannot be the whole portfolio.
- **Horizon 2: the emerging.** Rising businesses and opportunities that are gaining customers and revenue but are not yet profitable at scale. The job is to invest and build them into the next core. Medium certainty, medium-term returns. H2 is where today's experiments become tomorrow's engine.
- **Horizon 3: the options.** Nascent ideas, experiments, and bets on the future: research, pilots, small ventures, options on businesses that may or may not exist yet. The job is to create and test viable options cheaply, killing most and scaling the few that work. Low certainty, long-term returns.

The horizons describe maturity, so a project's horizon depends on its stage, not its age, and a fast-moving industry compresses all three into a shorter span.

## How to use it

1. **Place the initiatives.** Sort the organization's current and proposed activities into the three horizons by maturity, with each one's role, revenue or value, investment, and risk.
2. **Check the balance.** A healthy portfolio runs all three at once: H1 generates the cash, H2 builds the next core, H3 seeds the options. The common pathology is over-investing in H1 (the urgent, the measurable) while H2 and H3 are starved, which mortgages the future for the present. The opposite pathology, all H3 and no H1 engine, is rarer but fatal.
3. **Trace the pipeline.** Initiatives should flow inward over time: H3 options graduate to H2 businesses, H2 businesses mature into H1 core. Look for gaps: an empty H3 means no future options; a thin H2 means a cliff when H1 declines.
4. **Allocate and manage differently.** Each horizon needs different funding, metrics, talent, and governance. Judging H3 by H1 metrics (profitability, certainty) kills the future; running H1 like H3 wastes the cash engine. State how each horizon should be measured and managed.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating horizons as calendar time.** They are maturity stages. A long-term-feeling project can be H2 if it is already gaining customers.
- **Tyranny of the core.** H1 always has the loudest claim on resources because its returns are certain and measurable. Without a deliberate ring-fence, H2 and H3 starve.
- **Same metrics everywhere.** Demanding near-term profit from H3 options destroys them. Each horizon needs its own success measures.
- **An empty or invisible H3.** No options means no future once the current core matures. A thin H3 is a finding, not a blank to skip.
