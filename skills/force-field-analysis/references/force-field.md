# Force Field Analysis: the equilibrium model, forces, and scoring

Kurt Lewin's force field analysis (1943, from his field theory of change) treats any current situation as a state of equilibrium held in place by two opposing sets of forces. **Driving forces** push toward the proposed change or desired state. **Restraining forces** resist it and hold the status quo in place. The situation sits still not because nothing is happening, but because the two sets are in balance. The status quo *is* that balance point.

To move toward the desired state you change the balance: strengthen the driving forces, weaken the restraining forces, or both. Lewin's central insight is that these two routes are not equivalent. Adding driving forces raises the overall tension in the system and tends to provoke an equal and opposite counter-pressure as the restrainers push back harder. Removing or weakening restraining forces lowers tension and lets the system move toward the desired state with less resistance. Reducing restrainers is therefore usually the more effective and more durable route to change.

The unit of analysis is a **specific, well-defined change** (a decision, proposal, or goal), not a vague aspiration. Forces can only be judged against a target state that is precise enough to be for or against.

## Method

1. **Define the change.** State the desired state precisely. "Move to a four-day week for the support team by Q3" can be analyzed; "improve morale" cannot, because no force can be judged for or against it.
2. **List the forces.** Surface the driving forces and the restraining forces separately, hunting across the lenses below so the list is not lopsided.
3. **Score each force** by strength on a 1-to-5 scale, with a rationale.
4. **Read the net field.** Compare the total weight of drivers against restrainers. Is the change feasible as the field stands?
5. **Decide the leverage moves.** Identify which restrainers to weaken (preferred) and which drivers, if any, to strengthen, weighing feasibility, and judge whether the equilibrium can realistically be shifted.

## Scoring scale

Rate each force on strength from 1 to 5:

| Score | Strength | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Very weak | Present but barely moves the situation |
| 2 | Weak | A minor push or minor drag |
| 3 | Moderate | A real factor, neither decisive nor trivial |
| 4 | Strong | A major push or a major barrier |
| 5 | Very strong | On its own capable of carrying or blocking the change |

Score each force on its own terms: how hard does this single force push or resist, regardless of the others. The net read in step 4 weighs the two columns against each other; it does not change any individual score. Score with a rationale, and where evidence is available (a budget figure, a survey result, a named stakeholder's stated position, a regulatory deadline) ground the score in it rather than asserting a number.

## The lenses to hunt across

Both driving and restraining forces hide in the same places. Hunt each side across these lenses so neither column is shallow:

- **People**: individual motivation, fear, fatigue, what people stand to gain or lose, who champions the change and who quietly resents it, habits and comfort with the status quo.
- **Organizational**: incentives and reward structures, reporting lines, politics, competing priorities, available budget and time, leadership backing or its absence, culture and "how things are done here".
- **Technical**: existing systems and tooling, technical debt, skills and capability gaps, dependencies, what the current setup makes easy or hard.
- **External**: market pressure, customers, competitors, regulation, suppliers, deadlines, the economic climate, anything outside the organization pushing for or against the change.

A force is a **driver** when it pushes toward the desired state and a **restrainer** when it holds the status quo. The same lens usually yields both: a regulatory deadline can be a driver (you must change by a date) while regulatory complexity is a restrainer (the change is hard to make compliant).

## Reading the net field

Sum or weigh the two columns and read the gap, but do not treat it as arithmetic. A change facing one restrainer scored 5 can be blocked even when five small drivers outweigh it on paper, because that one barrier is binding. Name the binding restrainers and the load-bearing drivers, not just the totals. The read answers one question: as the field stands today, will the situation move, hold, or need active intervention before it can move?

## The central principle: weaken restrainers before adding drivers

When the field shows the change is not feasible as is, the instinct is to add driving force: more pressure, more incentive, more urgency from the top. Lewin's model warns against making this the first move. The system is in equilibrium because restrainers are matching drivers. Push harder on the drivers and the restrainers tend to push back with equal force; tension rises, the situation may not move, and what does move is fragile and snaps back once the pressure is released. Weakening a restrainer removes the opposition rather than overpowering it: tension falls, and the situation drifts toward the desired state and tends to stay there. So the highest-leverage move is usually to ask, for each strong restrainer, "what would make this barrier smaller or go away?" before asking "how do we push harder?". Strengthening drivers still has a place, especially weak or absent drivers that cost little to add, but it is the second tool, not the first.

## Pitfalls

- **Stacking the deck.** Listing mostly drivers (or mostly restrainers) produces a foregone conclusion, not an analysis. Hunt both sides across every lens with equal effort; a list that is ten drivers and two restrainers usually means the restrainer hunt was lazy, not that the change is easy.
- **Listing without scoring.** A bare list of forces with no strengths cannot be read as a field. Two weak drivers and one strong restrainer is a different situation from two strong drivers and one weak restrainer, and only the scores show which.
- **Adding pressure instead of removing barriers.** Reaching first for more driving force provokes counter-pressure and produces fragile change. Look to weaken restrainers first.
- **A vague target state.** If the change is not precise ("be more agile"), no force can be judged for or against it and the whole field is mush. Sharpen the desired state before listing forces.
- **Treating it as a one-time snapshot.** The field shifts as the change proceeds: today's restrainer can be neutralized, and acting on the field changes it. The analysis is a starting read to revisit, not a verdict fixed for the duration.
