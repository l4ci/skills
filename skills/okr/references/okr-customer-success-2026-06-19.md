# OKRs: Customer Success, Q3 2026

> _Illustrative example output for the `okr` skill. This customer-success team is fictional; every figure, name, and claim below is invented to demonstrate the report's shape, not researched. Do not cite these numbers._

## 1. Header

- **Scope:** Customer Success team (6 people), Q3 2026 (Jul 1 – Sep 30)
- **Date:** 2026-06-19
- **Facilitated as:** one OKR drafting cycle (clarify → draft → stress-test → ambition/cadence → alignment)
- **What the team owns:** onboarding and renewals. It does **not** own pricing, packaging, or product roadmap, those are dependencies, flagged in §5.

---

## 2. Objectives

Two objectives. Both are qualitative and time-bound; neither carries a number (the numbers live in the key results).

1. **Turn the renewal moment from a cliff into a non-event.** Customers should reach their renewal date already convinced, not negotiated into staying.
2. **Make the first 30 days the reason customers expand, not the reason they leave.** A new account should hit real value fast enough that growth becomes the default path.

A third candidate, "Become the company's biggest growth engine", was cut. It restates the company DIRECTION rather than naming what *this* team will do, and at company scope it is not the team's to own alone. The two objectives above are the team's contribution to it.

---

## 3. Key results

Scoring scale per KR: 0.0–1.0 (0.0 nothing · 0.3 token · 0.7 real progress short of target · 1.0 target met). Objective score = average of its KRs.

### Objective 1: Turn the renewal moment from a cliff into a non-event.

| # | Key result | Baseline | Target | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| KR1.1 | Lift net revenue retention (NRR) | 98% | **104%** | Outcome |
| KR1.2 | Cut gross logo churn in the 30-day window around the renewal date | 6.2% / quarter | **3.5%** | Outcome |
| KR1.3 | Raise the share of renewals closed **≥30 days before** the renewal date | 41% | **70%** | Outcome (leading) |
| KR1.4 | Lift the post-renewal relationship NPS (surveyed 2 weeks after renewal) | +22 | **+40** | Outcome |

*Why these four:* NRR (1.1) is the headline the company DIRECTION cares about; logo churn (1.2) catches the customers NRR can hide behind expansion; early-close rate (1.3) is the leading indicator that the renewal stopped being a last-minute scramble; relationship NPS (1.4) guards against "retained but resentful": a renewal won by discount or pressure that erodes the next one.

### Objective 2: Make the first 30 days the reason customers expand, not the reason they leave.

| # | Key result | Baseline | Target | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| KR2.1 | Cut median time-to-first-value (account reaches its first defined success milestone) | 19 days | **under 8 days** | Outcome |
| KR2.2 | Raise day-30 activation rate (account using ≥3 core features with ≥5 weekly active seats) | 47% | **68%** | Outcome |
| KR2.3 | Grow the share of new accounts that expand (seats or tier) within 90 days of going live | 11% | **22%** | Outcome |

*Why these three:* time-to-first-value (2.1) is the cause; activation (2.2) is the state that makes a customer hard to lose; early expansion (2.3) is the outcome that feeds the growth-engine DIRECTION. Three KRs are enough: adding a fourth ("onboarding CSAT") tested as redundant with 2.1/2.2 during the stress-test and was dropped.

## 5. Alignment

**Ladder up to the company DIRECTION**: *"make existing customers our biggest growth engine":*

| Company DIRECTION | Team OKR that serves it |
|---|---|
| Existing customers drive net new revenue | Obj 1 / KR1.1 (NRR 98→104%) and Obj 2 / KR2.3 (90-day expansion 11→22%) |
| Reduce revenue leakage | Obj 1 / KR1.2 (renewal-window churn 6.2→3.5%) |
| Customers succeed fast enough to grow | Obj 2 / KR2.1, KR2.2 (time-to-value, activation) |

**Top-down vs bottom-up:** Objective 1 was set largely top-down (the NRR number traces directly to the company growth target). Objective 2 came **bottom-up** from the onboarding pod, who argued: correctly, that the renewal cliff is mostly built in the first 30 days. The mix is roughly half-and-half, which is the healthy balance.

**Cross-team dependencies (made explicit):**

- **KR1.1 (NRR) and KR2.3 (expansion)** both depend on **Product** shipping the usage-based tier and on **Sales/RevOps** owning expansion pricing, neither is this team's to control. These KRs are partly *influence*, not pure *ownership*; flagged as the largest delivery risk. Confidence on KR1.1 should be tracked against the Product roadmap, not just team activity.
- **KR2.1 (time-to-first-value)** depends on a **Product** onboarding-instrumentation change (defining and tracking the "first success milestone" event). If that slips, 2.1 becomes unmeasurable mid-quarter. Dependency raised with Product before the quarter starts.

## 6. Check log (Stage-3 stress-test)

Four critics ran in parallel against the first draft. Findings and the revisions they forced:

**Outcome vs output.** Two of the original KRs were disguised tasks. *"Launch the redesigned onboarding flow"* (a thing the team does) was replaced by **KR2.1 time-to-first-value** and **KR2.2 activation** (things that change *because* of it). *"Run 4 renewal-readiness reviews per account"* was cut entirely, a competitor could run the reviews and the objective would still fail. The early-close-rate idea survived as **KR1.3** because it measures a customer behaviour, not a team activity.

**Measurability.** The original *"improve the renewal experience"* had no number and was rewritten into **KR1.4 relationship NPS (+22 → +40)** with a defined survey trigger (2 weeks post-renewal). Every KR now carries a baseline, a target, and a measurement definition. One gap remains: **KR2.1's "first success milestone"** is only measurable once Product ships the instrumentation, noted as a dependency, not a wording fix.

**Completeness.** The critic caught that hitting an NRR target alone could mask logo churn (a few big expansions hiding many small departures). That gap added **KR1.2 (logo churn in the renewal window)**: so Objective 1 cannot score well while quietly bleeding customers. For Objective 2, the critic confirmed the three KRs cover cause (2.1), state (2.2), and result (2.3) with no obvious gap.

**Ambition.** The draft was **sandbagged** on the renewal side: the original NRR target was 100%, a near-certainty given the 98% baseline, dressed as a goal. Raised to **104%** and the objective kept **committed**, since the team and company are treating it as a real promise. Onboarding was the opposite risk: the first draft's *"time-to-first-value under 3 days"* had no credible path on current staffing, so it was relaxed to **under 8 days** and the whole objective relabelled **aspirational**: a 0.7 there is the expected, healthy outcome. The redundant fourth onboarding KR (onboarding CSAT) was dropped here as overlapping with 2.1/2.2.

**Net effect of the stress-test:** 2 outputs converted to outcomes, 1 output deleted, 1 unmeasurable KR quantified, 1 completeness gap closed (KR1.2 added), 1 sandbagged target raised, 1 impossible target relaxed, 1 redundant KR removed. Final set: 2 objectives, 7 key results.

---

## 7. Caveats

- **A perfect score on a weak set means little.** These KRs measure what the team chose to make visible; if the choice is wrong, hitting 1.0 still won't make existing customers the growth engine. Completeness was tested, but it is a judgement, not a proof.
- **Scores judge the goals, not the people.** Especially on the aspirational onboarding objective, a 0.7 is success. Keep these out of compensation, the moment a KR sets a bonus, the incentive flips toward safe targets and the ambition collapses.
- **Without the review rhythm this rots.** The weekly (Obj 1) and biweekly (Obj 2) check-ins are not optional ceremony; they are how the dependency risks on KR1.1, KR2.1, and KR2.3 get caught in July instead of discovered at September grading.
- **Two KRs are influence, not ownership.** NRR and 90-day expansion depend on Product and Sales. If those dependencies slip, the team can do everything right and still miss, which is a signal about the *plan*, not a failure of the *team*.
- All figures here are illustrative for documentation purposes and were not researched.
