# Psychological Safety: the construct, the markers, and the leader practices

Amy Edmondson's psychological safety is the shared belief, held by members of a team, that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking: speaking up with an idea, a question, a concern, a mistake, or a challenge without fear of being punished, dismissed, or humiliated. It is a property of the team **climate**, not a trait of any individual, and it is one of the strongest predictors of team learning and, through learning, of performance.

The point is not that the team is comfortable. Psychological safety is not niceness, not consensus, not lowering the bar. High safety with low standards produces a pleasant team that learns nothing. The target is the combination of high safety **and** high accountability, what Edmondson calls the learning zone. Diagnose the climate, then prescribe toward that zone, never toward comfort.

The unit of analysis is a **team** with a leader and recurring interaction, not a whole company and not an individual. Psychological safety is local: it varies team to team inside the same organization, because it is built and broken by how a team's members and leader respond to interpersonal risk day to day.

## What it is not

Naming the construct by its boundaries keeps the diagnosis honest. Psychological safety is **not**:

- **Niceness.** A team can be unfailingly polite and have low safety, because the politeness is what stops anyone raising the hard thing. Safety is the freedom to be candid, not the obligation to be agreeable.
- **Consensus or harmony.** Safe teams disagree openly. Absence of conflict is more often a symptom of low safety (no one risks dissent) than of high safety.
- **Comfort.** Comfort is the absence of challenge. Safety is the presence of candor under challenge. They are different axes; the 2x2 below keeps them apart.
- **Lowering the bar.** Safety does not mean excusing poor work or softening standards. It means people can admit problems and ask for help while still being held to a high standard. Trading standards for warmth is the comfort-zone trap, not safety.

## The behavioral markers

Edmondson's measure of team psychological safety probes seven beliefs. Each is a marker; read the climate against all seven, since a team can be safe on some and not others.

1. **Mistakes are not held against you.** When someone makes a mistake on this team, it is not used to blame or punish them.
2. **Tough and sensitive issues can be raised.** Members are able to bring up problems and hard topics, not just the easy ones.
3. **Difference is accepted.** People on this team are not rejected for being different.
4. **It is safe to take an interpersonal risk.** Speaking up, dissenting, or admitting uncertainty does not feel dangerous.
5. **Help is easy to ask for.** It is not difficult to ask other members of this team for help.
6. **No one undermines another's efforts.** No one on this team would deliberately act to undercut a colleague's work.
7. **Unique skills are valued and used.** Members' distinct skills and talents are recognized and drawn on, not wasted.

Low scores on the **negative-keyed** markers (mistakes held against you, undermining, rejection of difference) are the loudest signals: they mean interpersonal risk is actively punished, not merely unrewarded.

## The 2x2: safety against accountability

Psychological safety is one axis. The other is **accountability**: the standards the team holds and the demand to meet them. Crossing them yields four zones, and the goal is one specific corner.

| | Low accountability / standards | High accountability / standards |
|---|---|---|
| **High psychological safety** | **Comfort zone.** People feel safe and at ease, but little gets demanded, so little is learned. Pleasant and unproductive. | **Learning and high-performance zone.** People take risks, admit mistakes, and ask for help **while** being held to a high bar. This is the target. |
| **Low psychological safety** | **Apathy zone.** Neither safe nor stretched. People withhold effort and conserve themselves; disengagement. | **Anxiety zone.** High demands without safety. People hide mistakes, do not ask for help, and stop speaking up, so problems surface late and large. |

The single most common misuse of this framework is to push a team out of anxiety **into comfort** by lowering standards, and call that psychological safety. The correct move from anxiety is up, raising safety while **holding** the standard, into the learning zone. Safety is the enabler of high performance, not a substitute for it.

## The three leader practices

Edmondson identifies what leaders do to build safety. These are the levers a diagnosis prescribes, not vague exhortations to "be supportive".

1. **Frame the work as a learning problem, not an execution problem.** When the work is uncertain and interdependent (which is most knowledge work), say so. Acknowledge that the team faces problems no one has fully solved, so input from everyone is needed. Framing the work as pure execution tells people there is one right answer and their job is to deliver it silently, which suppresses voice.
2. **Acknowledge your own fallibility.** Say plainly that you may miss things, that you make mistakes, that you need to hear when you are wrong. A leader who models being wrong makes it safe for others to be wrong.
3. **Model curiosity by asking questions.** Ask genuine questions and actually listen. Proactive inquiry signals that voice is expected and valued, and it creates the necessity for others to speak.

Team-level practices reinforce these: responding productively (not punitively) to bad news and mistakes, treating failures as data, normalizing help-seeking, and protecting dissent so the person who raises the hard thing is thanked, not penalized.

## Reading the climate as a whole

Scoring the seven markers is the start. The diagnosis is in placing the team and explaining the drivers:

- **Placement.** Locate the team on the safety axis from the markers, and on the accountability axis from observed standards and how they are enforced. Name the zone. A team in anxiety and a team in comfort both need work, but opposite work.
- **Weakest markers.** Which of the seven beliefs is most violated, and what concrete behavior drives it. "Mistakes are held against you" anchored to a specific blame episode is a diagnosis; a low average is not.
- **The behaviors, not the feelings.** Safety is built and broken by what people do when someone takes a risk. Trace the climate to observable responses: what happens to the person who admits an error, raises a concern, or asks a question.
- **The prescription.** Pair every safety intervention with the accountability check. The aim is the learning zone, so a prescription that would raise safety by relaxing the bar is wrong by construction.

## Common pitfalls

- **Conflating safety with comfort or niceness.** The most frequent error. A warm, agreeable, conflict-free team can be deeply unsafe, because the warmth is purchased by no one ever raising the hard thing. Diagnose candor under challenge, not pleasantness.
- **Treating it as an individual trait.** Psychological safety is a team climate, not a personality, a mood, or a measure of who is "brave". Two people behave differently on two teams because the teams differ, not because they do. Diagnose the climate; prescribe to the leader and the team.
- **Lowering standards in the name of safety (the comfort-zone trap).** Mistaking the goal as comfort rather than the learning zone. Safety without accountability does not raise performance; it relaxes it. Always hold the standard while raising the safety.
- **Asserting the climate without evidence.** Declaring a team "unsafe" from a vibe. Ground each marker in observed behavior, episodes, and patterns, and lower confidence where the signal is thin rather than inventing it.
- **Prescribing slogans.** "Be more open" is not an intervention. The levers are the three leader practices and concrete team norms; prescriptions name who does what differently when the next risk is taken.
