# Six Sigma DMAIC

DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) is the core problem-solving cycle of Six Sigma: a data-driven method for improving an existing process by reducing defects and variation. Its discipline is that you do not jump to solutions. You quantify the problem, find and prove the root causes with data, then fix those causes and lock in the gain.

DMAIC is for improving an existing process where data can be gathered. For designing a new process, the related DMADV is used instead.

## The five phases

### Define
Pin down the problem and the goal. Outputs: a problem statement (what is wrong, where, how big, since when, in measurable terms, with no presumed cause or solution), the goal and its target, the scope (process start and end), the customer and the CTQs (Critical-to-Quality characteristics: what the customer actually requires), and a project charter. A high-level process map (SIPOC: Suppliers, Inputs, Process, Outputs, Customers) frames the territory.

### Measure
Establish the facts about current performance. Outputs: a clear definition of the defect and the metric, a data-collection plan, a validated measurement system (can you trust the numbers), and a baseline of current performance and its variation. You cannot improve what you have not measured; this phase is where opinions become data.

### Analyze
Find and verify the root causes of the problem, do not guess them. Generate candidate causes (cause-and-effect or fishbone diagrams, 5 Whys, process analysis), then test them against the data rather than accepting the plausible-sounding one. The output is a short list of root causes proven to drive the defect, separating the vital few causes from the trivial many.

### Improve
Develop, select, and pilot solutions that address the proven root causes (not symptoms). Generate options, choose against impact and feasibility, pilot on a small scale, and confirm with data that the change actually moves the metric before full rollout.

### Control
Sustain the gain so the problem does not return. Outputs: a control plan, standardized procedures, monitoring (for example control charts / statistical process control) with response rules when the metric drifts, and handover to the process owner. The discipline of Control is what separates a real improvement from a temporary one.

## How to use it

1. **Hold the gates.** Each phase has a question it must answer before the next begins: Define (is the problem clear and worth solving), Measure (do we trust the baseline), Analyze (are the root causes proven), Improve (does the pilot work), Control (will it hold). Do not advance on a phase that has not answered its question.
2. **Stay data-driven.** Claims about causes and effects are tested against data, not asserted. The method exists to defeat the plausible-but-wrong fix.
3. **Fix causes, not symptoms.** Improve targets the root causes proven in Analyze. A fix that treats a symptom returns.

## Common pitfalls

- **Jumping to a solution in Define**, then using the rest of the cycle to justify it.
- **Skipping Measure or trusting bad data**, so the baseline and everything after it is unreliable.
- **Guessing root causes in Analyze** instead of verifying them against data, the most common failure.
- **Piloting nothing**, rolling a change out fully before proving it works.
- **Neglecting Control**, so the process drifts back and the gain evaporates.
