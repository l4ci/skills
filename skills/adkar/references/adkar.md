# ADKAR: the five elements and the barrier-point logic

Prosci's ADKAR model (Jeff Hiatt, *ADKAR: A Model for Change*, 2006) explains change at the level of the individual. Organizations do not change; the people in them do. A project succeeds only when enough of the affected people make their own transition, and ADKAR names the five building blocks each person has to move through to get there.

The five blocks are **sequential**: Awareness, then Desire, then Knowledge, then Ability, then Reinforcement. An individual must achieve each before the next can take hold. The practical consequence is the **barrier point**: change stalls at the first block that is not achieved, and effort spent on later blocks before that one is resolved is wasted. Training people in Knowledge when they have no Desire produces trained, unwilling people, not change. Diagnose in order and fix the first gap first.

The unit of analysis is the **affected group**, not the whole organization. Different audiences sit at different barrier points: the team being reorganized may be stuck on Desire while the managers who must lead them are stuck on Knowledge. Assess per group, because a single org-wide verdict hides the gaps that actually block adoption.

## Strength-rating scale

Rate each element, per affected group, on how well it is currently achieved:

| Score | State | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Absent | The element is not present at all; a hard block |
| 2 | Weak | Some presence, but far short of what the change needs |
| 3 | Partial | Mixed; present for some of the group or some of the time |
| 4 | Strong | Largely achieved, with minor gaps |
| 5 | Achieved | Fully in place and durable |

The barrier point for a group is its first element scoring 3 or below as you read Awareness to Reinforcement. Scores after the barrier are provisional: until the barrier is cleared, a high Knowledge or Ability score cannot actually be relied on, because the person has not yet committed to using it.

## 1. Awareness of the need for change

Does the person understand *why* the change is happening, *why now*, and what the risk of not changing is? Awareness is about the nature of the change and its drivers, not the mechanics of doing it.

Diagnostic questions:
- Can the person state, in their own words, why the change is needed?
- Do they understand the business or external drivers behind it (competition, cost, regulation, customer demand)?
- Do they grasp the risk of *not* changing, for the organization and for themselves?
- Do they see why now, rather than later or never?
- Is the "what's wrong with today" clear, or do they think the current state is fine?

What "achieved" looks like: the person can explain the reason for the change and the cost of standing still, without needing to agree with it yet. Awareness is understanding, not consent.

Common gaps: the change was announced as a *what* with no *why*; the message came from a distant source the group does not trust; the group sees no problem with the status quo; competing messages drown the signal.

## 2. Desire to participate in and support the change

Does the person *want* to engage, and support the change with their own effort? Desire is the personal choice to take part. It rests on "what's in it for me" (and "what's the cost to me"), on the person's own situation, and on whether they trust the people leading the change.

Diagnostic questions:
- What does the person stand to gain or lose, as they see it?
- Is their motivation intrinsic (they believe in it) or only compliance (they will do the minimum)?
- Do they trust the sponsor and their own manager?
- What is their personal history with past changes here?
- Are there fears (job security, competence, status, workload) driving resistance?

What "achieved" looks like: the person actively engages, not merely complies. Visible support, willingness to put in effort, advocacy to peers.

Desire is the hardest block and the one most often skipped. It **cannot be forced, only influenced**: through the personal case for change, sponsor and manager engagement, addressing the specific fears of the group, and removing the costs that make resistance rational. A mandate produces compliance, which decays the moment the pressure lifts. Resistance is information about where Desire is missing, not an obstacle to push past.

## 3. Knowledge of how to change

Does the person know *how* to change: the skills, behaviors, processes, and tools the new way demands, both during the transition and after it? Knowledge has two halves: how to change (the transition) and how to perform once changed (the new state).

Diagnostic questions:
- Does the person know what new skills and behaviors are required?
- Do they understand the new processes, systems, and tools?
- Do they know how their role and daily work will be different?
- Is training available, and matched to what the change actually needs?
- Do they know where to go when they get stuck?

What "achieved" looks like: the person can describe what they need to do differently and has been given the instruction to do it. Knowing is not yet doing (that is Ability).

Common gaps: training delivered before Desire exists (so it does not stick); training on the tool but not the new way of working; generic training that ignores the group's specific role; no support for the post-go-live state.

## 4. Ability to implement the change

Can the person actually *do* it, at the required level of performance, in real conditions? Ability is the gap between knowing and doing. People can complete training and still be unable to perform: under real workload, with real cases, at real speed.

Diagnostic questions:
- Has the person demonstrated the new behavior in practice, not just in training?
- Are they performing at the level the change requires, or just getting by?
- Do they have the time, tools, and access to practice and improve?
- Are coaching and hands-on support available during the ramp?
- Do barriers (workload, conflicting incentives, broken tooling) block performance even where willingness and knowledge are present?

What "achieved" looks like: the person performs the new way at the expected standard in their actual job, not in a sandbox.

Closing Ability takes time, practice, coaching, and the removal of physical or process barriers. It is not closed by more training; the gap is rarely knowledge.

## 5. Reinforcement to sustain the change

Is there anything *holding* the change in place so people do not revert to the old way? Reinforcement is what makes the new state durable: recognition, rewards, feedback, measurement, and the correction of backsliding.

Diagnostic questions:
- Is the new behavior recognized and rewarded, and the old behavior no longer rewarded?
- Are there mechanisms (metrics, audits, reviews) that surface regression?
- Do managers reinforce the change, or quietly tolerate the old way?
- Are incentives, performance measures, and processes aligned with the new state?
- Is there a feedback loop that catches drift and corrects it?

What "achieved" looks like: the new way is the normal way; people are recognized for it, the old way carries a cost, and regression is noticed and addressed.

Reinforcement is the most skipped block. Teams declare victory at go-live, support is withdrawn, the old incentives remain, and within months people drift back. Without it the earlier four blocks decay.

## Typical interventions per element

| Element | What it needs | Typical interventions |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Understanding of why and why now | Clear, repeated communication of drivers and risk of not changing; messages from a credible sponsor; explaining the "why" before the "what" |
| Desire | Personal motivation to engage | Sponsor visibility and active leadership; manager-led conversations; surface and address the group's specific fears; make the personal case; remove costs that make resistance rational; engage resisters rather than override them |
| Knowledge | Knowing how to change and perform | Role-specific training timed to need; job aids and documentation; access to experts; coverage of both the transition and the new state |
| Ability | Performing at the required level | Hands-on practice with real cases; coaching during the ramp; time and tools to build proficiency; remove process and tooling barriers |
| Reinforcement | Sustaining the new way | Recognition and rewards for the new behavior; aligned incentives and performance measures; metrics that surface regression; manager follow-through; feedback and correction loops |

Sponsors and the person's direct manager carry most of Awareness, Desire, and Reinforcement. Project and training teams carry most of Knowledge and Ability. A common failure is leaving Desire and Reinforcement to the project team, who cannot supply them.

## Pitfalls

- **Jumping to Knowledge when Desire is the gap.** The most common and most expensive error. Training is visible and easy to schedule, so projects default to it. If the group does not want the change, training produces capable people who will not use what they learned. Diagnose Desire first.
- **Treating the organization as one audience.** A single org-wide ADKAR score is an average that hides the gaps. Different groups sit at different barrier points and need different interventions. Assess per group.
- **Trying to force Desire.** Desire is a personal choice and responds to influence, not mandate. Pressure buys compliance that reverts. Address the actual fears and the personal cost; treat resistance as a signal of where Desire is missing.
- **Skipping Reinforcement.** Declaring victory at go-live and withdrawing support lets people drift back to the old way. Without recognition, aligned incentives, and a mechanism to catch regression, the change does not last.
- **Assessing once and not re-checking.** Barrier points move as the change progresses. A group stuck on Desire in month one may clear it and stall on Ability in month three. Re-assess, and shift effort to the new barrier.
- **Working blocks out of order.** Securing Ability before Knowledge, or Knowledge before Desire, wastes effort because each block depends on the ones before it. Read the elements in sequence and fix the first gap first.
