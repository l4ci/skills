# Porter's Value Chain Analysis

Porter's value chain (*Competitive Advantage*, 1985) breaks a firm into the activities through which it creates value, so the sources of competitive advantage can be located. The premise: advantage comes not from the firm as a whole but from specific activities, and from how they link together. The difference between the value a firm's output commands and the cost of performing its activities is its margin.

The chain has nine activity categories: five primary activities that create and deliver the product, and four support activities that enable them.

## Primary activities

- **Inbound logistics:** receiving, storing, and distributing inputs. Materials handling, warehousing, inventory, supplier relations.
- **Operations:** transforming inputs into the finished product. Production, assembly, manufacturing, service delivery.
- **Outbound logistics:** storing and distributing the product to buyers. Order fulfillment, warehousing of finished goods, delivery.
- **Marketing and sales:** giving buyers the means and reason to buy. Advertising, pricing, channel, sales force, promotion.
- **Service:** maintaining and enhancing value after sale. Installation, repair, training, support, warranty.

## Support activities

- **Firm infrastructure:** general management, planning, finance, legal, quality. Supports the whole chain.
- **Human resource management:** recruiting, training, developing, and compensating people across all activities.
- **Technology development:** R&D, process and product technology, design, automation. Improves activities, not only the product.
- **Procurement:** the function of purchasing inputs, distinct from the inputs themselves, applied across the chain.

## How to use it

1. **Describe each activity** as the firm actually performs it, not the textbook version.
2. **Assess two things per activity:**
   - **Cost:** the activity's share of total cost and its cost drivers. Where does the money go.
   - **Value:** how much the activity contributes to what buyers will pay, through either lower cost or differentiation they value.
3. **Compare to competitors.** An activity is a strength only relative to rivals. Mark each as an advantage, a parity, or a disadvantage.
4. **Examine the linkages.** Value often comes from how activities connect (for example, design choices in technology development that cut service costs), not from activities in isolation. Linkages are hard for rivals to copy and are a frequent source of durable advantage.
5. **Tie to the strategy.** For a cost-leadership strategy, hunt cost drivers and waste. For a differentiation strategy, find the activities that create what buyers value and could create more. The same chain reads differently depending on the advantage sought.

## Output

The analysis should locate where the firm's competitive advantage actually lives (which activities and linkages), where it leaks value or carries excess cost, and what to do: strengthen the advantage-creating activities, fix or outsource the weak ones, and exploit linkages.

## Common pitfalls

- **Listing activities without assessing cost and value.** A description is not an analysis.
- **No competitor comparison.** Advantage is relative; an activity done well is not an advantage if every rival does it as well.
- **Ignoring linkages.** Treating activities as silos misses the most defensible advantages.
- **Detaching from strategy.** Cost and differentiation lenses surface different priorities; analyze through the one the firm is actually pursuing.
- **One value chain for a diverse firm.** Different products or segments often have different chains; analyze them separately when they diverge.
