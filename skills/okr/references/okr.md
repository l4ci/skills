# OKR (Objectives and Key Results)

OKR is a goal-setting method that pairs a qualitative objective with a small set of quantitative key results. Andy Grove developed it at Intel in the 1970s; John Doerr carried it to Google and later popularized it in *Measure What Matters* (2018). The objective says what you want to achieve and why it matters; the key results say how you will know you got there. The completeness rule binds them: if you achieve every key result, the objective must be met by definition.

OKRs are usually set per period (typically a quarter), reviewed on a regular cadence, and scored at the end. They cascade and align across levels, from company to team to individual, ideally as a mix of top-down direction and bottom-up commitment rather than a strict waterfall.

## The anatomy

### Objective
A single qualitative sentence stating what you want to achieve in the period. It should be memorable, direction-setting, and time-bound, with the meaning behind the goal visible in it. It is not a metric. A few examples:

- "Delight new customers with a fast, obvious onboarding experience."
- "Make our mobile app the way most users choose to engage."
- "Win the trust of enterprise security buyers."

Keep the count low: 1-3 objectives per unit per period. If a number has crept into the objective ("Reach 10,000 users"), that number is a key result wearing the wrong hat.

### Key results
For each objective, 3-5 key results that are quantitative and measurable, each with a number and a target. Together they must cover the objective: hitting all of them should leave no honest doubt that the objective was achieved. Each key result should measure an **outcome** (a result the business or customer experiences) rather than an **output** (a task the team completes).

A baseline matters. "Increase activation rate to 60%" is sharper when the document also records that it stands at 42% today, because the target then carries a known starting point and a visible gap.

## Outcome versus output

This is the distinction that separates a real key result from a task list. An output is something you do; an outcome is something that changes because you did it. Teams default to outputs because outputs are inside their control and easy to mark done. But a quarter of completed tasks can still leave the objective unmet, which is exactly what the outcome test guards against.

| Objective | Bad key result (output) | Good key result (outcome) |
|---|---|---|
| Delight new customers with a fast onboarding experience | Launch the redesigned onboarding flow | Reduce median time-to-first-value from 25 min to under 10 min |
| Delight new customers with a fast onboarding experience | Run 3 onboarding usability workshops | Raise day-7 activation rate from 42% to 60% |
| Win the trust of enterprise security buyers | Publish the SOC 2 report | Cut security-questionnaire turnaround from 12 days to 3 |
| Make the mobile app the primary channel | Ship 5 new mobile features | Grow mobile share of active sessions from 30% to 55% |

The bad column can all be true while the objective fails; the good column cannot. A useful check: if a competitor did the task for you, would the objective be met? If not, the task is an output.

## Committed versus aspirational

Not all OKRs carry the same expectation, and naming which is which is what keeps scores honest.

- **Committed OKRs** are promises the unit is expected to fully deliver. The target score is ~1.0, and a miss is a real failure that demands a response. Resourcing and schedules are adjusted to hit them.
- **Aspirational (stretch) OKRs** reach beyond what is comfortably achievable. Here ~0.7 is the expected, healthy outcome; consistently scoring 1.0 means the goals were sandbagged, set too low to be a stretch at all.

The ~0.7 norm exists so that ambitious goals do not punish the people who set them. It only works if everyone knows in advance which OKRs are aspirational, so a 0.7 reads as success rather than shortfall.

## Scoring

At period end, each key result is scored, commonly on a 0.0-1.0 scale (0.0 nothing, 0.3 a token, 0.7 short of the target but real progress, 1.0 fully met). The objective's score is usually the average of its key results. The score is a learning instrument, not a verdict on the people: a low score on an aspirational OKR prompts the question "what did we learn and what do we change," not "who is to blame." Tying scores to pay turns the whole system defensive and is the surest way to kill ambition (see pitfalls).

## Cadence and review rhythm

OKRs run on a rhythm, not a single event:

- **Set** at the start of the period (commonly a quarter), with company OKRs first, then team and individual.
- **Check in** regularly through the period (weekly or biweekly is common), updating confidence on each key result and surfacing what is at risk early.
- **Score and reflect** at the end: grade each key result, write down what was learned, and feed it into the next period's OKRs.

An annual horizon is sometimes set above the quarterly OKRs as a longer-range frame.

## Alignment and cascade

OKRs connect levels so that local goals serve the larger strategy. A team's objective often picks up a company key result and turns it into an objective of its own, with its own key results below it. Done well, this is bidirectional: leadership sets direction top-down, and teams contribute roughly half their OKRs bottom-up, committing to what they believe they can move. A rigid waterfall, where every OKR is dictated from above, produces compliance and little ownership. Alignment also makes dependencies explicit, so a key result that relies on another team's work is visible to both.

## Common pitfalls

- **Key results that are a task list.** The most frequent failure: outputs ("ship X", "hold 3 workshops", "hire 2 engineers") standing in for outcomes. The quarter ends with everything done and nothing moved.
- **Objectives that are actually metrics.** A number in the objective ("Hit $2M ARR") means the qualitative direction is missing and the objective-and-key-result split has collapsed.
- **Too many OKRs.** A dozen objectives and twenty key results is a backlog, not a set of priorities. Few objectives, each with 3-5 key results, is the discipline.
- **Sandbagging.** Setting key results the unit is certain to hit, so every score lands at 1.0. The flip side of impossible stretch, and just as useless. Aspirational OKRs should feel uncomfortable.
- **Set and forget.** OKRs written at the start of the quarter and never looked at again until grading. Without the check-in rhythm, they neither steer the work nor catch risk in time.
- **OKRs as the basis for compensation.** The most corrosive mistake. The moment a score sets someone's bonus, every incentive points toward easy, safe key results, and the ambition the method exists to create disappears.
