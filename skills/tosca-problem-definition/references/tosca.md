# TOSCA: problem definition

TOSCA sharpens a vague problem into a crisp, solvable one before any solving begins. It comes from the McKinsey-rooted approach in *Bulletproof Problem Solving* (Charles Conn and Robert McLean, 2018). A well-defined problem is half-solved; most failed analysis traces back to solving the wrong problem precisely.

TOSCA is five elements:

- **T - Trouble:** the specific pain or gap that makes this worth solving now
- **O - Owner:** the decision-maker who owns the problem and will act on the answer
- **S - Success criteria:** what a good outcome looks like, measurably and by when
- **C - Constraints:** the real boundaries the solution must fit inside
- **A - Actors:** the stakeholders who affect or are affected by the problem and its solution

The deliverable is a single crisp problem statement plus the structured brief behind it.

---

## The five elements

### T - Trouble
The felt problem: the gap between where things are and where they should be, and why it matters now.

Sharp questions: What exactly is wrong? What is the gap between the current and the desired state? Why does this matter now rather than later? What happens if nothing is done?

Pitfalls:
- **Symptom, not root.** "Sales are down" is a symptom. The trouble is whatever is causing it. Push until the trouble would, if solved, remove the symptom.
- **Solution disguised as problem.** "We need a new CRM" is a solution. The trouble is the underlying need ("reps lose deals because they cannot see customer history"). Strip the implied solution out.

### O - Owner
The person or body that owns the decision, has authority to act, and will use the answer. The analysis exists to serve their decision.

Sharp questions: Whose problem is this? Who has the authority and budget to act on the answer? Who is accountable for the outcome? What does this owner actually care about?

Pitfalls:
- **No real owner.** A problem nobody owns is not worth framing; the answer will sit unused.
- **Powerless owner.** If the named owner cannot actually act, find who can, or the work is academic.

### S - Success criteria
What success looks like in concrete, measurable terms, with a target level and a deadline, reflecting what the owner values (time, cost, quality, risk, growth).

Sharp questions: How will we know the problem is solved? What metric and what threshold? By when? Which dimensions matter most to the owner, and what are the trade-offs between them?

Pitfalls:
- **Vague.** "Improve things" or "be more efficient" cannot be tested. Force a number and a date.
- **Conflicting criteria unnamed.** Faster, cheaper, and higher quality usually trade off. Name the trade-off and the owner's priority rather than pretending all can be maximized.

### C - Constraints
The genuine boundaries within which any solution must fit: budget, time, legal, technical, political, resource.

Sharp questions: What is off the table? What limits are fixed (budget, deadline, headcount, legal, technology)? What resources cannot change?

Pitfalls:
- **Assumptions treated as constraints.** Over-constraining quietly kills the best options. Challenge each stated constraint: is it a hard boundary or an untested assumption? Mark which are real and which are assumed.
- **Real constraints ignored.** Under-constraining yields elegant but infeasible answers. Surface the limits that actually bind.

### A - Actors
Everyone who affects or is affected by the problem and its solution: those who must approve, who could block, who carries it out, who feels the impact.

Sharp questions: Who is involved, impacted, or able to block? Who must approve? What does each want, and where do their interests conflict?

Pitfalls:
- **Missing a key stakeholder**, especially one who can block, which surfaces too late.
- **Listing actors without their interests**, which makes the list decorative rather than useful.

## What a good problem statement looks like

A finished TOSCA produces one statement that is:

- **Specific and concrete**, not a theme or a topic
- **Measurable and time-bound**, carrying the success criteria
- **Decision-oriented**, naming the owner's decision it serves
- **Answerable**, scoped so analysis can actually resolve it
- **Free of an embedded solution**, stating the problem, not a preferred answer

Often phrased as a question the owner needs answered. Example of weak versus strong:

- Weak: "We need to improve customer retention."
- Strong: "How can [Owner] raise 12-month retention of new subscribers from 60% to 75% within the next two quarters, without increasing customer-acquisition cost, given the current product team of six?"

The strong version carries the trouble (retention gap), owner, success criteria (60% to 75%, two quarters), a constraint (no CAC increase), and a resource boundary (team of six), and it invites analysis rather than presupposing the fix.
